## I. INTRODUCTION

This case is on remand following the appeal of our prior final written decision (Paper 78, “Decision”). In the Decision, we found that none of the challenged claims 1–6 of U.S. Patent No. 7,041,786 B2 (Ex. 1001, “the ’786 patent”) had been shown unpatentable because while Petitioner $^{2}$ had established a prima facie showing of obvivousness, that showing was “outweighed by Patent Owner’s comparatively strong evidence demonstrating unexpected results.” Dec. 60.

Those findings and our conclusion on obviousness were appealed to the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit (“Federal Circuit”). See Paper 83 (“CAFC Decision”). The Federal Circuit did not disturb our determination that Petitioner “established a prima facie case of obviousness.” See CAFC Dec. 6–8. However, it took issue with the unexpected results analysis in the Decision, explaining that “without more explanation from the Board as to its determination that the experiments [offered by Patent Owner to show unexpected results] compared the challenged claims to the closest prior art, we cannot reach a judgment on MSN’s challenges to the Board’s holding of non-obviousness.” Id. at 14. Accordingly, the Federal Circuit vacated the Decision and remanded the case for further consideration of the unexpected results evidence. Id.

More specifically, the Federal Circuit identified “three reasons” why “the Board’s determination that the[se] experiments compared the claimed invention with the closest prior art lack[ed] adequate explanation” and instructed us to address these issues on remand. First, the Court stated we “should determine, based on the evidence put forward by the parties, whether plecanatide exhibits topoisomerism and whether the testing of the challenged claims should encompass both the active and inactive forms.” Id. at 12. $^{3}$ Second, the Court instructed us to reconsider what the closest prior art compound is in view of Patent Owner’s “contention that highly pure A-form uroguanylin could be maintained as a lyophilized powder,” noting that “[i]f it is true . . . that uroguanylin can be maintained and therefore tested in its active A form as a lyophilized powder, that would support that the experiments [which were conducted in solution] did not use the closest prior art.” Id. The '786 patent issued on May 9, 2006, from a utility application filed on March 28, 2002, and claims priority to a provisional application filed on January 17, 2002. Ex. 1001, codes (22), (45), (60).

The '786 patent is titled “Guanylate Cyclase Receptor Agonists for the Treatment of Tissue Inflammation and Carcinogenesis.” Ex. 1001, code (54). According to the Specification,

[t]he present invention relates to the therapeutic use of guanylate cyclase receptor agonists as a means for enhancing the intracellular production of cGMP. ## III. ANALYSIS

The focus on remand is Patent Owner's unexpected results showing. In the Decision, we found there were three categories of unexpected results data that evidenced non-obviousness: (1) interconversion stability, (2) potency, and (3) binding affinity. $^{15}$ See Dec. 37–59. Categories (1) and (2) are based on the Patent Owner's own data in Table 4 of the Specification and studies described in Exhibits 2027 and 2028. $^{16}$ Id. at 38. Category (3) is based on data that was published in a research article, Liu. $^{17}$ Id. at 56.

On remand, Patent Owner continues to assert that these three categories of data demonstrate unexpected results compared to the closest prior art on remand. See PO Remand Br. 2. Petitioner disputes the same. See generally Pet. Remand Resp. (urging “Patent Owner has not met its burden to show its alleged ‘unexpected results’ compared the invention to the closest prior art, or that the unexpected results are commensurate in scope with the claims.”).

We begin by addressing the three issues identified by the Federal Circuit. See CAFC Dec. 12–13. After addressing those issues, we assess the specifics of each category of Patent Owner's results as well as Petitioner's argument that those results are not reasonably commensurate with the scope of the claims. Finally, we weigh the record as a whole to reach a conclusion on obviousness.

## A. Whether plecanatide exhibits topoisomerism

The parties agree that plecanatide, like uroguanylin, exhibits topoisomerism, meaning it “exists in, and can interconvert between, two topoisomeric forms, one active and the other inactive.” PO Remand Br. 1, 4 (citing Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 50, 53; Ex. 2040); see also Pet. Remand Resp. 28 (acknowledging same). This means that claim 1, which recites a “peptide consisting of the amino acid sequence of SEQ ID NO:20,” encompasses that peptide regardless of its topoisomeric form. Ex. 1063 ¶ 150 (“Sequence ID No. 20 does not require any particular topoisomer . . . but instead would encompass a composition of peptide consisting of Sequence ID No. 20 that is purely an ‘active’ topoisomer, purely an ‘inactive’ topoisomer, or any mixture of two or more topoisomers sharing Sequence ID No. 20.”).

Patent Owner agrees that “Claim 1 encompasses plecanatide in both topoisomeric forms,” but attempts to distinguish claim 2, urging that it “focuses on the active topoisomer of plecanatide by reciting ‘a guanylate cyclase receptor agonist peptide consisting of the amino acid sequence of SEQ ID NO:20.” PO Remand Br. 5 (emphasis in original). Petitioner responds, asserting that “[t]o the extent . . . Patent Owner is now raising claim construction to argue that claim 2 does not stand or fall with claim 1,” something it has never done before, “it is far too late . . . for such gamesmanship.” Pet. Remand Resp. 29. Petitioner further points to evidence suggesting that even the inactive topoisomer is likely to have some agonist activity. Id. at 28 (citing Ex. 1078 ¶¶ 22–23).

We agree that it is too late for Patent Owner to argue that claim 2 does not stand or fall with claim 1. As Petitioner points out, “Patent Owner has never separately argued the patentability of claims 2–6” before. Pet. Remand

Resp. 28 n. 16. And even now, Patent Owner does not articulate any argument for treating claim 2 (or any of the other challenged claims) differently from claim 1. PO Remand Br. 5 (asserting only that claim 2 “focuses on plecanatide’s active form”); see generally PO Reply (electing not to respond to Petitioner’s arguments regarding claim 2). But even if we were to consider such an argument, we do not discern any reason to treat claim 2 differently than claim 1 in assessing Patent Owner’s unexpected results showing. It is undisputed that the active topoisomer of plecanatide is a guanylate cyclase receptor agonist peptide and the evidence of record shows such. See infra § III.C.ii–iii (showing plecanatide’s activity and binding affinity). Thus, there is a nexus between the peptide of claim 2 and the alleged unexpected results.

Moreover, to the extent Patent Owner contends that claim 2 is limited to only the active topoisomer, we disagree. First, Patent Owner does not cite evidence to support its assertion that the “inactive form of plecanatide is not an agonist for the claimed receptor.” See PO Remand Br. 5. In contrast, Petitioner points to evidence that even the “inactive” topoisomer of uroguanylin shows some guanylate cyclase receptor agonist activity in cGMP assays. Ex. 1078 ¶ 23; Ex. 2010, 239 (Fig. 8); Ex. 2074, 48 (observing that the inactive topoisomer “showed maximal 10%” activity of the active topoisomer). For this reason, we credit the testimony of Petitioner’s expert Dr. Peterson that an ordinarily-skilled artisan would not expect the inactive form of plecanatide to be “wholly inactive” or “devoid of agonist activity.” Ex. 1078 ¶ 23.

Second, as Petitioner points out, nothing in the claims or Specification of the '786 patent defines the level of activity required for the peptide to be considered a “guanylate cyclase receptor agonist.” Pet. Remand Resp. 29: Ex. 1078 ¶ 24. Accordingly, even if the ‘inactive” topoisomer exhibits greatly reduced activity relative to the “active” form, we do not read claim 2 to exclude the former by reciting a “guanylate cyclase receptor agonist peptide.” Ex. 1001, 37:4–5.

As for the Federal Circuit's related question about whether the testing should encompass both of plecanatide's topoisomers (see CAFC Dec. 12), we note that it likely does even if the parties' evidence does not directly show such. Because plecanatide is subject to topoisomerism it exists as a mixture of those topoisomers when it is in solution that trends to an equilibrium state of the isomeric forms. Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 51, 73. As explained below, the relevant assays are carried out in solution. See infra § III.B. Thus, even when testing plecanatide that had been purified to a high degree of its active form, it is likely some minute amount of inactive topoisomer was present in these tests, either as an impurity or due to interconversion during testing. Id. But, as explained below in § III.D, we do not understand this to undermine the weight of Patent Owner's unexpected results evidence.

## B. What is the closest prior art

In the Decision, we found that the closest prior art is “human uroguanylin as it naturally exists, i.e., a mixture with some interconversion between topoisomers.” See Dec. 39. That finding was premised on evidence demonstrating that uroguanylin readily interconverts between topoisomers in solution. See id. at 38–39 (citing Ex. 2010, 238; Ex. 2011, 28, 31; Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 67–68; Ex. 2025 ¶¶ 49–52).

The Federal Circuit took issue with our finding because it “did not address MSN’s contention that highly pure A-form uroguanylin could be maintained as a lyophilized powder.” CAFC Dec. 12. It explained that:

If it is true, as MSN contends, that uroguanylin can be maintained and therefore tested in its active A form as a lyophilized powder, that would support MSN's argument that the experiments did not use the closest prior art. But in finding that the closest prior art is a “mixture” of A- and B-form uroguanylin, the Board only cited evidence stating that uroguanylin solutions could not be maintained for extended periods of time. Its broad conclusion that a mixture of A and B-form uroguanylin—regardless of its formulation—is the closest prior art therefore does not reasonably follow from the solution-specific evidence it cited in support.

Id. (citation omitted). Taking the Court's guidance into account, we now reconsider the arguments and evidence on this point.

First, it is undisputed that human uroguanylin is subject to topoisomerism. See generally PO Remand Br.; Pet. Remand Resp.; see also Ex. 2011, 27; Ex. 2020, 223; Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 64–67. Because uroguanylin is subject to topoisomerism, it naturally exists as a mixture of its topoisomeric forms. Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 51, 73. This is particularly the case when it is in solution at biologically relevant temperature and pH. Ex. 2024 ¶ 68; Ex. 2025 ¶¶ 49–51; see also Ex. 2010, 236 (“At 37°C, 25% of both uroguanylin-15 isomers are interconverted within 24 h.”); Ex. 2011, 29 (Fig. 5 showing more rapid interconversion at 37°C and acidic pH). Under such conditions, it was understood that uroguanylin readily interconverts between its topoisomeric forms. Id.

It was also known that Reversed-phase High Performance Liquid Chromatography (“RP-HPLC”) could be used to isolate and purify each of the topoisomeric forms of human uroguanylin to at least 95% purity. Ex. 2011, 29 (reporting that each uroguanylin topoisomer “could be separately isolated at purities greater than 99%” as determined by RP-HPLC); Ex. 2074, 48 (same); Ex. 2075, 5153 (using RP-HPLC to purify the biologically active form to “\~99% purity” for use in T84 bioassay); see also Ex. 2020, 224, 229 (RP-HPLC to purify followed by T84 bioassay to characterize active form).

In addition, it is not disputed that the closest prior art is human uroguanylin that has been purified to at least 95% of the active topoisomer (“purified (>95%)”). Pet. Remand Resp. 5, 29 (“the commonly accepted purity criteria for a peptide to be used in research is greater than 95% purity”); Ex. 1078 ¶ 25; Ex. 1080, 35:24–36:10 (both sides’ declarants agreeing); see also PO Remand Br. 5–7, 9 (arguing that the purified (>95%) active form was used in its experiments). But importantly, this does not mean that the closest prior art contains only the active topoisomer of uroguanylin. Because human uroguanylin is “a dynamic peptide,” even purified (>95%) active form human uroguanylin can include a small amount (<5%) of the inactive topoisomer. See, e.g., PO Remand Br. 5, 8 (“Though human uroguanylin’s topoisomers can be isolated and separately purified, they are still prone to interconversion.”), 16 (“Some amount of the inactive topoisomeric form is inevitable.”); Pet. Remand Resp. 9 (“The parties also agree that, even after purification, uroguanylin was known to interconvert from its active form to an inactive form).

Moreover, while the record shows that the purified active form of uroguanylin can be lyophilized to a powder form and refrigerated to stabilize it against interconversion, these are not the relevant conditions for its testing.

See Ex. 2010, 236 (“No conversion of the lyophilized uroguanylin isomers after long term storage of up to 3 months at $-20^{\circ}C$ ”); Ex. 2011, 29 (purity “maintained for more than one year when [purified] compound was stored in a freezer as an amorphous powder”). The experiments Patent Owner relies upon to show unexpected results are liquid assays. Ex. 2072 ¶ 11 (“[T]he T84 cell bioassay is a solution-based test.”). So too are the assays described for related peptides in the prior art. $^{18}$ Ex. 2010, 232 (T84 cell bioassay in solution); Ex. 2011, 28 (same); Ex. 2020, 224 (same); Ex. 2075, 5152 (same); see also Ex. 2027, TRUL00018211; Ex. 2028, TRUL00018268; Ex. 2046, 3778 (solution-based cell binding assays and pharmacokinetic studies). Such assays are designed to mimic biological conditions. See Ex. 2072 ¶ 11 (“[B]ecause human uroguanylin rapidly goes into solution in the gastrointestinal tract, and because human uroguanylin needs to be in solution for it to be biologically active in the gastrointestinal tract, we tested it in well-known solution-based assays to acquire information about how it might behave in vivo.”). For these reasons, a POSA would understand that, even when starting from a highly isomerically pure powder, some interconversion may occur after the powder is solubilized for testing. See Ex. 2011, 31 (observing that “interconversion may occur during course of the analysis” and results may vary “if peptide solutions have been left at room temperature for a few days, especially under acidic conditions”).

## C. Whether Patent Owner's experiments used the closest prior art

We find that Patent Owner has the better position because its evidence shows that the results in the '786 patent and Exhibits 2027 and 2028—which Patent Owner uses to support its claims of unexpectedly better interconversion stability and potency—were obtained from “best on best” testing of the purified (>95%) active topoisomers of human uroguanylin and plecanatide. $^{21}$ Dr. Shailubhai, one of the named inventors, testified that his team used synthesized human uroguanylin that was purified to 95% or more of the active topoisomer in these experiments. Ex. 2072 ¶¶ 4–5. To do this, Dr. Shailubhai had uroguanylin prepared by manufacturers who purified it to at least 95% of the active topoisomer using RP-HPLC, according to published procedures in Exhibit 2020 (“Klodt”), Exhibit 2074 (“Chino”), and Ex. 2075 (“the Shailubhai publication”). Id. ¶¶ 6–9. Dr. Shailubhai explains that the purified uroguanylin was shipped to him as a lyophilized

powder and stored at or below -20 °C until the time of testing. Id. ¶¶ 10, 12. As explained above, that frozen lyophilized powder uroguanylin does not readily interconvert. Ex. 2010, 236; Ex. 2011, 29. When the assay was ready, a solution of uroguanylin was prepared “and immediately used . . . in the assay” to minimize interconversion as much as possible. Id.

Dr. Shailubhai's testimony is corroborated by the testimony from Dr. Pennington. Dr. Pennington worked at Bachem Biosciences, Inc. (“Bachem”), the company that supplied the test peptides for the studies described in Exhibits 2027 and 2028. Ex. 2073 ¶ 6; see also Ex. 2027, TRUL00018210; Ex. 2028, TRUL00018267 (identifying Bachem as the peptide supplier). Dr. Pennington testified that the human uroguanylin supplied to Dr. Shailubhai during the timeframe of these studies came from him and that he purified it using RP-HPLC such that it contained greater than 95% of the active topoisomer. Ex. 2073 ¶¶ 8–10. Dr. Pennington also corroborated Dr. Shailubhai’s testimony that the uroguanylin was shipped to him as a lyophilized powder kept at or below -20 °C. Id. ¶ 11.

The references cited in the '786 patent and Exhibits 2027 and 2028 further corroborate Dr. Shailubhai's testimony that the studies described in Table 4 of the Specification and in Exhibits 2027 and 2028 used human uroguanylin that had been purified (>95%) of the active topoisomer. The '786 patent states that the T84 bioassay in its example “was assayed as previously reported” in the Shailubhai publication. Ex. 1001, 15:36–41, 17:32 (citing Ex. 2075). Exhibits 2027 and 2028 similarly state that the T84 bioassays were conducted “using [the] published procedure” in the

Shailubhai publication. Ex. 2027, TRUL00018211; $^{22}$ Ex. 2028, TRUL00018268. The Shailubhai publication makes clear that its T84 bioassay procedures involve testing of uroguanylin peptides that had been purified to remove the inactive topoisomer. Ex. 2075, 5153 (“The major peptide peak, exhibiting a potent biological assay, was further purified by reverse phase-HPLC to \~99% purity and used for this study.”).

In addition to citing the Shailubhai publication, the '786 patent states that the assayed peptides were “custom synthesized and purified (>95% purity) using a published procedure” in Klodt. Ex. 1001, 15:53–54 (citing Ex. 2020). According to Patent Owner’s expert, one of ordinary skill in the art would understand this to mean 95% or greater isomeric purity, i.e., no more than 5 percent of anything else, including topoisomers of the same peptide. Ex. 1060, 114:19–116:10. That testimony is supported by the disclosure in Klodt, which refers to Chino’s observation that solid-phase synthesis of human uroguanylin “yields two topological isomers which could be separated by HPLC.” Ex. 2020, 223 (citing Ex. 2074). $^{23}$ Klodt further describes procedures for synthesis, purification, and analytical HPLC methods that it uses to “confirm” Chino’s observation of uroguanylin’s two isoforms. See id. 223–224 (describing synthetic procedures and RP-HPLC methods); 227 (confirming Chino's result). $^{24}$ Indeed, Petitioner's expert, Dr. Peterson, appears to agree that the procedure taught in Klodt will yield topoisomerically–pure human uroguanylin. See Ex. 1063 ¶ 37 (testifying that Klodt teaches how to separate and characterize uroguanylin's active topoisomer).

Nevertheless, on appeal, Petitioner took what the Federal Circuit observed were “contradictory positions as to what level of purity Klodt refers.” CAFC Dec. 13. That is, in one context Petitioner urged that “Klodt refers to purity with respect to other compounds, not topoisomerism,” whereas in another it urged that the “procedure in Klodt was used to purify peptides at the topoisomer level.” Id. Based on the evidence above, we find that the record supports the latter, topoisomeric-purity interpretation. Indeed, in its post-remand brief Petitioner does not dispute that the procedure taught in Klodt will result in peptides purified to greater than 95% of their active form. Instead, Petitioner attempts to defend against the charge that it took contradictory positions on Klodt on appeal by urging that it has “consistently represented that plecanatide was purified relative to its topoisomer.” Pet. Remand Resp. 8 n. 3. The problem with that argument is that it implies the ’786 patent’s statement that the peptides in Table 4 were “purified (>95%

purity)” means that some of those peptides (plecanatide) were purified at the topoisomeric level, but others (uroguanylin) were not. Ex. 1001, 15:53–54. We see no basis in the record for adopting Petitioner’s inconsistent, peptide-specific interpretation of that statement.

Accordingly, the fact that the '786 patent and Exhibits 2027 and 2028 expressly state that the experiments therein use the procedures in the Shailubhai publication and Klodt further corroborates Dr. Shailubhai's testimony that these experiments used the purified ( $>95\%$ ) active form of human uroguanylin. Moreover, the fact those procedures are cited in the '786 patent itself demonstrates corroboration in a contemporaneous document because the patent was filed near in time to the study dates indicated in Exhibits 2027 and 2028. Compare Ex. 1001 (March 2002 filing date), with Ex. 2027, TRUL00018203; Ex. 2028, TRUL0018260 (September to December 2001 study dates).

Two other documents further corroborate aspects of Patent Owner's testimonial evidence. The first is Exhibit 2076, which is an “Analytical Data Sheet” dated May 24, 2012, for an order of uroguanylin supplied to Dr. Shailubhai from Peptides International. Ex. 2076, 1, 3. It includes an “HPLC profile” for the uroguanylin in that order showing 96.1% purity and indicates it was being provided as a powder and stored at -20 °C. Id. at 1–3. Dr. Pennington explained that Peptides International was the company he joined in 2010, after leaving Bachem, and that in that new role he continued to synthesize and supply Dr. Shailubhai with human uroguanylin purified to remove the inactive topoisomer. Ex. 2073 ¶ 7. The data sheet in Exhibit 2076 is dated about a decade after the experiments Patent Owner relies upon to show unexpected results. However, Dr. Pennington testified that Exhibit

2075 confirms “his usual specifications for synthesized human uroguanylin purified to greater than 95%” and “delivered [to Dr. Shailubhai] as a lyophilized powder at or below -20 °C” as he “always did.” Id. ¶¶ 12–15. Accordingly, while Exhibit 2076 is not a data sheet for the particular peptides used in the experiments in Exhibits 2027 and 2028 and therefore not direct evidence that those peptides were purified to their active topoisomer, it corroborates Dr. Pennington’s testimony regarding the specifications he says he has always used to supply human uroguanylin in a document that long predates any of the parties’ present disputes.

The second document is Exhibit 2040—a letter from Dr. Pennington to Dr. Shailubhai dated March 31, 2004, which quotes a price for a “scale up” in the production of SP-304 (plecanatide) as well as a price for “the parent compound uroguanylin.” Ex. 2040, 1. The letter notes that uroguanylin is “notorious” for interconversion and difficult to purify, whereas plecanatide had been found to be less so based on Bachem’s initial work and therefore “[p]urification was greatly simplified allowing us to offer the very favorable price [for plecanatide] above.” See id. (quoting a price for plecanatide that is 5 time less than uroguanylin on a per mg basis). As Dr. Pennington testified, his letter in Exhibit 2040 confirms that Dr. Shailubhai “regularly requested that [he] provide him with solid-phase synthesized human uroguanylin and SP-304 (plecanatide) purified with respect to their active topoisomers.” Ex. 2073 ¶ 12; see also Ex. 2072 ¶ 6 n. 3 (similar testimony from Dr. Shailubhai regarding Exhibit 2040). While this letter is dated a few years after the experiments in Exhibits 2027 and 2028, it refers back to the “same priority treatment as you [Dr. Shailubhai] have already experienced with us.” Ex. 2040, 1. Accordingly, the letter in

Exhibit 2040 further corroborates Dr. Pennington's testimony that he "always" supplied uroguanylin, purified to its active topoisomer, to Dr. Shailubhai. Ex. 2027 ¶ 12.

Petitioner's arguments disputing Patent Owner's showing are not persuasive. See Pet. Remand Resp. 5–26. Petitioner's strongest argument is the allegedly contradictory statements from Patent Owner's counsel in the EPO opposition. Id. at 5–8. Those statements apparently refer to the same experimental data that Patent Owner relies on here, but Patent Owner's counsel before the EPO argued the data “were obtained using uroguanylin in its naturally-occurring form (i.e., a mixture of A- and B-forms) and [plecanatide]. This is in contrast to the . . . data presented by Opponent 1, which specifically uses the A-form of each peptide.” Ex. 1067, 103.

Patent Owner's attempt to reconcile its EPO statements with its present position is not compelling. See PO Remand Br. 15–16; PO Remand Reply 10. While it is true, as Patent Owner asserts, that it “never argued that plecanatide was tested against a 1:1 mixture of uroguanylin topoisomers” and even a purified (>95%) active form uroguanylin “is a mixture” of topoisomer, Patent Owner’s statements to the EPO suggest something different that its present position, i.e., that the uroguanylin was tested as a naturally-occurring mixture of topoisomers as opposed to uroguanylin that had been purified by RP-HPLC to at least 95% of the active topoisomer. See PO Remand Reply 10.

However, even accepting the contradictory import of the EPO statements, those statements do not overcome the weight of Patent Owner's present showing that its experiments, in fact, tested human uroguanylin that had been purified to $95\%$ or greater of the active topoisomer. As explained above, Patent Owner's present showing is premised on testimony from multiple witnesses and corroborated by contemporaneous citation to experimental methods from multiple references—all of which consistently teach the testing of topoisomerically pure uroguanylin—as well as additional, ancillary documents (Exhibits 2040 and 2076). While the EPO statements undercut that showing to some extent, they do not overcome the weight of the evidence. Put differently, Petitioner frames this as a binary issue—either Patent Owner's explanation of what was tested was correct “then” or it is correct “now.” See Pet. Remand Resp. 6. We find that the current record, which is aided by more fulsome evidence and argument regarding that testing than that present in the EPO record (Ex. 1067), demonstrates that Patent Owner's present explanation is the correct one.

Petitioner's arguments that Patent Owner's witnesses lack personal knowledge are unavailing. See Pet. Remand Resp. 9–16. Petitioner argues that Dr. Shailubhai did not observe the synthesis and purification of the tested uroguanylin and did not personally perform any the comparative studies. Id. at 10–12 (asserting the studies were conducted by Dr. Dheer's team and recorded in Dr. Dheer's notebook). But this does not show that Dr. Shailubhai lacked personal knowledge of the facts in his declaration.

To the contrary, Dr. Shailubhai's testimony that he supervised the team that performed the experiments in the '786 patent and Exhibits 2027 and 2028 is unrebutted. Ex. 2072 ¶ 4; see also Ex. 2023 ¶¶ 10, 17, 30; Ex. 2066 ¶¶ 10, 17, 30 ("I directed the preparation and testing" and "have first-hand knowledge" of the test data). That team included Dr. Dheer who was the principal scientist conducting those experiments. Ex. 2072 ¶ 13; Ex. 1079, 42:22–43:8. While he did not perform the experiments, Dr. Shailubhai testified that he: (1) authorized Dr. Dheer to perform them; (2) “personally observed” and was “actually present in the lab when the experiments were run;” (3) was “involved in reviewing and looking at the methods to be used” in those experiments; and (4) “supervised and reviewed all the data.” Ex. 1079, 42:22–44:2. This is more than sufficient to establish foundation for his testimony describing how those experiments were conducted and what materials were used.

Moreover, while Dr. Shailubhai did not personally observe the synthesis and purification of the tested peptides, which were prepared by third party manufacturers such as Bachem, he did instruct them to provide topoisomerically-pure peptides according to the procedures in Klodt and Chino. Ex. 2072 ¶ 6; see also Ex. 1079, 27:7–12 (manufacturers given methods and publications); 61:16–62:217 (“it was our instructions to all of those companies to use those publications [Klodt and Chino] for matters of synthesis and purification”), 91:14–17 (same for Bachem). Dr. Pennington’s testimony confirms that he received that instruction and consistently followed it for the uroguanylin supplied by Bachem. Ex. 2073 ¶¶ 8–12. There is no reason to suspect that the peptide manufacturers identified in the Specification did not similarly follow the instructions and methods that Dr. Shailubhai gave them to prepare these peptides.

Petitioner speculates that Bachem and Dr. Pennington may not have actually supplied the peptides used in the studies recorded in Exhibits 2027 and 2028. See Pet. Remand Resp. 14–16 (arguing that the Specification, which identifies different manufacturers for the peptides in Table 4, describes the same experiments in Exhibits 2027 and 2028 and assuming therefore that Bachem did not provide the uroguanylin for any of the

experiments Patent Owner relies upon). We are not persuaded. Exhibits 2027 and 2028 both identify Bachem as the peptide supplier. Ex. 2027, TRUL00018210; Ex. 2028, TRUL00018267. And Dr. Pennington testified that he provided uroguanylin to Dr. Shailubhai in the relevant time frame. Ex. 2073 ¶ 9. $^{25}$ But even if Petitioner's speculation that Exhibits 2027 and 2028 describe the same experiments as the '786 patent were correct, this would not undermine the thrust of Dr. Shailubhai's testimony that these experiments (whether separate or part of the same set of assays) tested uroguanylin that had been purified to 95% or greater of the active topoisomer.

Petitioner additionally contends that, even if “the uroguanylin peptides in the studies were purified to remove the inactive topoisomer, none of these experiments speak to what happened after the uroguanylin peptides were purified, i.e., how the uroguanylin was shipped, stored and prepared for use in the studies.” Pet. Remand Resp. 21–22. We disagree. Both Dr. Shailubhai and Dr. Pennington offered testimony within their personal knowledge on these subjects. See Ex. 2073 ¶¶ 11–12 (testifying that following purification the purified uroguanylin was stored and shipped as a stable lyophilized powder at temperatures at or below -20 °C); Ex. 2072 ¶ 10 (testifying that the received lyophilized powder “was immediately stored at or below -20 °C in my lab” and “[o]nce an assay was ready, we prepared a solution of human uroguanylin from the stabilized lyophilized powder and

immediately used it in the assay"). Accordingly, Petitioner's argument is not that there is no evidence on these points, but instead that some of the details in the witness testimony is not also recorded in the presently-available documentary evidence.

Looking at the evidence as a whole, we find that the lack of documentary support for some aspects of the witness testimony does not undermine Patent Owner's showing. First, as explained above, the documentary evidence corroborates the key fact that Dr. Shailubhai requested and received peptides for use in these experiments that had been purified according to prior art methods to 95% or greater of the active topoisomer. See, e.g., Ex. 1001, 15:39–41, 53–54 (peptides were synthesized, purified, and assayed according to the methods in the Shailubhai publication and Klodt); Ex. 2020, 223–224 (RP-HPLC method to isolate and purify human uroguanylin); Ex. 2027, TRUL00018211; Ex. 2028, TRUL00018268 (assays performed as described in the Shailubhai publication); Ex. 2040 (quoting prices for purified plecanatide and uroguanylin); Ex. 2075 (T84 assay method using uroguanylin purified by RP-HPLC to isolate the active topoisomer at \~99% purity).

Second, any details missing from the documentary record (e.g., that peptides were refrigerated prior to use and that mixed solutions were promptly tested) are sufficiently filled by credible fact witness testimony. Indeed, as Patent Owner observes, “[i]t defies logic to suggest that skilled artisans, including Drs. Shailubhai and Pennington, were aware of uroguanylin’s propensity to interconvert but that Dr. Shailubhai would have obtained the purified active form of uroguanylin—at great cost—only to

ship, store, and prepare it in a manner permitting its interconversion." PO Remand Reply 9.

Third, that there are some gaps in documents and witness memory is not unreasonable given that the experiments in question occurred almost 25 years ago. As Dr. Shailubhai explains, the experiments were conducted at Synergy Pharmaceuticals toward the end of 2001. Ex. 2073 ¶ 13. Synergy Pharmaceuticals went through bankruptcy proceedings before being purchased by the present Patent Owner. Id. Moreover, Dr. Dheer, whose lab notebooks are referenced in Exhibits 2027 and 2028, is now deceased. Id. It is therefore “not surprising” that Patent Owner was unable to locate some of the potentially relevant documents. See NFC Tech., 871 F.3d 1367, 1374 (explaining that adequate corroboration for inventor testimony can be found even on somewhat limited documentation, particularly for events that occurred twenty or thirty years prior where “the inventor’s inability to submit [certain] documents [i]s ‘not surprising’ given the amount of time that had passed”) (quoting Loral Fairchild Corp. v. Matushita Elec., 266 F.3d 1358, 1365 (Fed. Cir. 2002)). $^{26}$ We find the available documentary and testimonial (Pennington) evidence adequate, particularly when considering the passage of time in this case, to corroborate Dr. Shailubhai’s testimony that the purified (>95%) active topoisomer of uroguanylin was used in the experiments described in the ’786 patent and Exhibits 2027 and 2028.

In sum, we find Patent Owner's evidence that its experiments involved “best on best” testing of the claimed invention against the closest prior art credible. Having considered and addressed the issues identified in the CAFC Decision, we now turn to the specifics of Patent Owner's evidence and the parties' arguments for each category of results. $^{27}$

## i. Interconversion stability

Patent Owner argues that “[p]lecanatide’s particular combination of structural features yielded a peptide that is unexpectedly stabilized against interconversion [e.g., from its active to its inactive form] as compared to human uroguanylin.” Resp. 58. As evidence, Patent Owner offers data from an experiment involving “[s]amples of plecanatide (SP-304) and human uroguanylin (UroG).” Id. (citing Ex. 2028, TRUL00018269). As explained above, these peptides had been purified to greater than 95% of the active topoisomer prior to testing. The samples were “incubated for 16 hours at 37°C in a solution having a pH of 3.0” and “then analyzed by HPLC.” Id. (citing Ex. 2028, TRUL00018269). The results of that analysis are shown in the figure reproduced below. Ex. 2028, TRUL00018269.

Figure 1: HPLC Chromatographs of Uroguanylin and SP-304 Following 16-Hour Incubations at pH 3.0 in Aqueous Media at 37°C  
![](images/31948958bcf464651d41c6ecbe8440825e041e2ebe6d98de0ef640ff740cf07b.jpg)

The figure above shows HPLC chromatographs of uroguanylin on the left and SP-304 on the right. The uroguanylin chromatograph shows a first peak labeled “Active fraction” next to a second, smaller peak labeled “Inactive fraction.” Id. In contrast, the SP-304 chromatograph shows only a single peak. According to Patent Owner, the result shown in this data “would not have been expected, for nothing in the art would have suggested that the Asp $^{3}$ to Glu $^{3}$ substitution would have improved human uroguanylin’s isomeric stability, let alone to this degree.” Resp. 59 (citing Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 210–215).

We find Patent Owner's evidence persuasive to show that the Glu $^{3}$ substitution results in an unexpected improvement in interconversion stability. As explained above, RP-HPLC was a known technique for separating the topoisomers of human uroguanylin. Ex. 2010, 230–231; Ex. 2011, 27–28; Ex. 2020, 223; Ex. 2028, TRUL00018269; see also Ex. 1063 ¶¶ 37, 40 (Petitioner's expert confirming same). It was also known that one of human uroguanylin's topoisomers was biologically active,

whereas the other, i.e., what we and the parties refer to as the “inactive” topoisomer, had significantly less or no activity at the same concentration. Ex. 2011, 31 (“compound I shows significant activity in the cGMP producing assay, whereas compound II is practically inactive”); see also Ex. 2010, 238–239 (Fig. 8) (showing the “A isomer” had a potency “with a minimal concentration between $10^{-7}$ and $10^{-8}$ M,” but that the B isomer “had virtually no effect on cGMP level at $10^{-6}$ M”); Ex. 2074, 48 (compound II “showed maximal 10% of the activity of compound I at $10^{-6}$ M”). The chromatograph for uroguanylin in Patent Owner’s data is consistent with these teachings, showing two peaks representing the active and inactive topoisomers. Compare Ex. 2028, TRUL00018269 (Fig. 1) with Ex. 2011, 28 (Fig. 2). The chromatograph for SP-304 shows a markedly different result, i.e., a “single peak,” which was confirmed “using the cGMP T84 cell assay” to represent “the fully active conformer of SP-304.” Ex. 2028, TRUL00018269; Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 208–209.

We agree with Patent Owner and credit Dr. Davies's testimony (Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 210–215) that a POSA would not have expected the Glu $^{3}$ substitution to produce such a result. Indeed, the premise for Petitioner's “conservative substitution” rationale is that the Glu $^{3}$ substitution would have been expected to produce an analog having similar properties to human uroguanylin. See, e.g., Pet. 1, 36 (stating that a POSA “would have expected this particular conservative substitution to retain excellent receptor-activating activity”); Reply 3 (urging that the reason to modify a known compound may be an expectation that the new compound will have “similar properties to the old”) (internal quotations omitted). Patent Owner’s HPLC data, however, demonstrate a difference in kind between the data for SP-304 (a single HPLC peak) and uroguanylin (two distinct peaks corresponding to the active and inactive topoisomer) after incubation under the same conditions. As Dr. Davies explains, “topoisomers exist as a mixture of compounds with different[] spatial conformations” that are “continuously interconverting between spatial conformations” with “varying degrees of interconversion stability.” See Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 51–56. Thus, Patent Owner’s data show that the Glu $^{3}$ substitution results in a peptide that is demonstrably more stable against interconversion than the parent, unmodified peptide.

Exhibit 2010 (“Marx”) further shows that this result would not have been expected. The Asp $^{3}$ residue in human uroguanylin is the third residue from the amino-terminal end of that peptide. But Marx teaches that “isomerization [e.g., interconversion to the other topoisomer] is not affected by the amino-terminal region of uroguanylin.” Ex. 2010, 236. Marx also suggests that a different modification, i.e., adding a “third disulfide bond apparently would lead to a preference of a structure similar to the” biologically active isomer. See Resp. 39–40 (quoting Ex. 2010, 235). Thus, Marx suggests that a POSA seeking to stabilize uroguanylin against interconversion to the inactive isomer should pursue modifications other than the Glu $^{3}$ substitution.

Nevertheless, Petitioner cites Marx and Klodt, urging that the “prior art suggested acidic residues at positions 2-3 contribute to stabilization.” Reply 28. We disagree. As explained above, Marx teaches that it is the “ionizable side-chains” in Asp $^{2}$ and Asp $^{3}$ that “may be involved in the control of stabilization of the two isomers.” Ex. 2010, 236. The substitution of one acidic residue (Asp) with another residue having an acidic side chain (Glu) does not affect this; both the Glu $^{3}$ and Asp $^{3}$ peptide would still have an ionizable side chain at the same position. Petitioner also refers to Klodt's observation that “the ‘sterical bulk’ of ‘other residues’ far from the C-terminus may influence interconversion.” Reply 13 (quoting Ex. 2020, 228). But that teaching does not identify Asp $^{3}$ . Rather, what Klodt says is that “other residues, in particular between the inner cysteine residues, also influence [stability of topoisomers] by their sterical bulk.” Ex. 2020, 228 (emphasis added). Asp $^{3}$ is not located between the cysteine residues in human uroguanylin. See Ex. 1005, 1:50–55 (SEQ ID NO: 1); Ex. 1006, 52 (Fig. 6a) (showing that human uroguanylin has cysteine residues at positions 4, 7, 12, and 15). Accordingly, neither Marx nor Klodt supports Petitioner’s position that a POSA would have expected the result shown in Patent Owner’s HPLC data.

Petitioner's argument that Patent Owner has not shown that the incubation conditions for its experiment “(e.g., pH 3 for 16 hours)” are “relevant” is unavailing. Reply 27. First, Petitioner does not explain what it objects to regarding these incubation conditions. See id. To the extent Petitioner contends the conditions differ from those in vivo when a peptide is orally administered, that distinction does not undermine Patent Owner’s showing because the data still show that plecanatide exhibited unexpectedly improved interconversion stability under the conditions tested. Second, Petitioner’s suggestion that these conditions are somehow not relevant is at odds with the fact that the data were apparently submitted to the FDA as part of Patent Owner’s NDA. Ex. 2072 ¶ 13.

Petitioner's argument that Patent Owner should have additionally provided NMR data to confirm the structure of the peptide in each HPLC peak because “its HPLC protocol may have been optimized to separate topoisomers of [uroguanylin] but not SP-304” is not persuasive. Reply 28 (citing Ex. 1063 ¶¶ 146–147). RP-HPLC was a known technique for separating topoisomers of uroguanylin. Ex. 2010, 230–31; Ex. 2011, 27–28; Ex. 2020, 223; Ex. 2028, TRUL00018269; Ex. 1063 ¶ 40. Thus, we see no reason why Patent Owner’s HPLC data are not themselves sufficient to show the alleged results. Indeed, the premise for Petitioner’s argument, i.e., Dr. Peterson’s testimony that the SP-304 peak “is relatively broad with some degree of tailing, raising the question of whether the HPLC protocol employed was simply lacking in optimization to resolve the topoisomers of this peptide” (Ex. 1063 ¶¶ 146–147), is just speculation. In contrast, we find the testimony Patent Owner offers from Dr. Shailubhai—who “directed the preparation and testing” resulting in Patent Owner’s data, provides detailed information regarding the HPLC conditions, and concludes that the chromatograph shows “only a single peak” for SP-304 (Ex. 2066 ¶¶ 17–20)—is more credible than the speculative testimony from Dr. Peterson that Petitioner cites and more consistent with the chromatographic evidence itself.

## iii. Binding affinity

As discussed above, we find that Patent Owner's own experiments (i.e., the results in Table 4 of the Specification and in Exhibits 2027 and 2028) are sufficient to demonstrate unexpected results of the claimed peptide, but there is additional work from an external laboratory that bolsters this finding. Specifically, Patent Owner asserts that “[p]lecanatide also has an unexpected and superior binding affinity as compared to human uroguanylin.” Resp. 66. Patent Owner’s binding affinity results differ from the other categories of results because they are based on data from experiments described in a third-party, post-issuance research article, Liu (Exhibit 2046), rather than the data in the Specification of the ’786 patent and Exhibits 2027 and 2028. Before turning to what Liu’s data show, we first address the parties’ arguments regarding whether Liu’s binding affinity experiments tested the closest prior art.

Patent Owner asserts that “Liu used radio-labelled human uroguanylin and plecanatide, purified to their topoisomers, to evaluate their relative binding affinities.” PO Remand Br. 14 (citing Ex. 2046, 3777 (Abstr.), 3778 (Materials and Methods), 3779)). According to Patent Owner, “[b]efore testing binding affinities, Liu states that he obtained and purified to ‘homogeneity by RP-HPLC’ the active forms of human uroguanylin and plecanatide (referred to as ‘E3-uroguanylin’), which he confirmed through the use of the T84 cell bioassay.” Id. (quoting Ex. 2046, 3778). Patent

Owner further asserts that the RP-HPLC chromatogram in Figure 3 of Liu confirms this understanding. Id. at 15.

Petitioner argues “there is no evidence that the purified active form of uroguanylin was used in Liu” because while “Patent Owner points to the ‘chromatogram for radiolabeled peptides [in Figure 3], showing singular peaks for human uroguanylin and plecanatide,” it “makes no showing that the ‘singular peak’ for uroguanylin actually represents the active topoisomer.” Pet. Remand Resp. 25 (quoting PO Remand Br. 15)). In addition, Petitioner suggests Liu’s data is faulty because Liu provides no information regarding how the uroguanylin peptides were stored and how long the peptides were in solution prior to being tested.” Id. at 24–25 (citing Ex. 1078 ¶ 20; Ex. 1079, 136:8–19). $^{31}$

Patent Owner replies, urging that “[i]t cannot be credibly argued that the peer-reviewed Liu publication . . . would have studied peptides with sizable inactive content” given that “[b]y the time Liu published in 2009, uroguanylin was well-known to interconvert,” “Liu states that he synthesized and purified” the uroguanylin “via RP-HPLC to homogeneity,” and Liu confirms the peptides “had comparable activity” in its “T84 cancer cell line test.” PO Remand Reply 9–10 (citing Ex. 2046, 3778–3780).

Patent Owner again has the better position. Liu plainly discloses that RP-HPLC was used to purify both uroguanylin and plecanatide ( $E^{3}$ -uroguanylin) to homogeneity. Ex. 2046, 3778. Moreover, the

chromatograms in Figure 3 show a single, well-defined peak, which is consistent with the other evidence demonstrating topoisomeric purity. Id. at 3780. It is true that the Figure 3 chromatograms show the radiolabeled versions of the peptides used in Liu's experiments. See Ex. 1078 ¶ 21 (observing that the $^{111}$ In-DOTA-conjugated peptides represent a structural modification of the underlying peptide). But Liu separately uses precisely the same wording (“purified to homogeneity by RP-HPLC”) to describe purification of the unconjugated peptides. Ex. 2046, 3778. The evidence in Liu therefore more reasonably supports the position that the chromatograms in Figure 3 are representative of a level of “homogeneity” achieved through its purification of those peptides. Id.

Petitioner's other arguments are premised on speculation that Liu tested the purified inactive form of human uroguanylin instead of the active topoisomer. Such speculation is unfounded for several reasons. First, the foundation for Liu's study is uroguanylin's known activity as an “agonist that binds the guanylate cyclase C receptor (GC-C).” Ex. 2046, 3777 (Abstr.). That receptor “is overexpressed in human colorectal cancer.” Id. Liu's study seeks to use “radiolabeled uroguanylin analogs for in vivo detection” of colorectal cancer by testing their binding affinity for GC-C and measuring in vivo tumor uptake of the radiolabeled peptides. Id. The use of the inactive topoisomer would therefore undermine the purpose of Liu's study.

Second, both the uroguanylin used in Liu's binding assay and the radiolabeled conjugate in Liu's in vivo testing exhibited activity that Liu was able to compare to the corresponding results for plecanatide. See id. at 3779–3780. While plecanatide had significantly higher binding affinity, the fact

that Liu's purified uroguanylin and uroguanylin conjugate also showed activity in these tests is inconsistent with Petitioner's speculation that it was the purified inactive topoisomer, rather than the active topoisomer, that Liu tested.

Third, given uroguanylin's known interconversion issues and the significant effort Liu describes taking to purify it via RP-HPLC, it seems unlikely that Liu would then store it or unduly delay its testing in solution in a manner that would allow otherwise avoidable interconversion back to the inactive topoisomer to occur.

In sum, Patent Owner has sufficiently demonstrated that Liu's experiments used and tested the closest prior art, i.e., the purified ( $>95\%$ ) active topoisomer of uroguanylin. We now turn to what Liu's data show.

As noted above, based on the data in Liu, Patent Owner asserts that “[p]lecanatide also has an unexpected and superior binding affinity as compared to human uroguanylin.” Resp. 66. According to Patent Owner, the data in Liu’s Table 1, reproduced below, shows that plecanatide (E3-uroguanylin) binds to the GC-C receptor with 8-fold higher affinity than native human uroguanylin. Resp. 66 (reproducing table from Ex. 2046, 3779).

## D. Petitioner's argument that the results are not reasonably commensurate

In the Decision, we found that Patent Owner's evidence of unexpected results was reasonably commensurate with the scope of claim 1 even if that claim encompassed both the active and inactive topoisomer of the recited peptide. Dec. 49. The Federal Circuit did not reach Petitioner's challenge to that finding on appeal. CAFC Dec. 9. On remand, Petitioner reiterates its position that because the challenged claims “encompass both an ‘active’ and ‘inactive’ form of plecanatide,” Patent Owner has not shown that its results are commensurate with the scope of the claims. Pet. Remand Resp. 28–29; see Reply 22–23 (prior argument). We disagree.

Claim 1 is narrowly drawn to a peptide consisting of a single 16 amino acid long sequence. The other challenged claims are likewise limited to a peptide consisting of that sequence. See Ex. 1001, 37:4–38:9. While that peptide is subject to topoisomerism, both topoisomers are still the same compound with the same chemical structure. Ex. 2024 ¶ 50 (“[A] ‘topoisomer’ is the same chemical compound having different spatial arrangement of the various atoms in the molecule that occur because of rotations around bonds.”); see also CAFC Oral Arg. at 9:54–9:59, available at https://cafc.uscourts.gov/oral-arguments/24-1011\_04072025.mp3 (Petitioner’s counsel acknowledging that uroguanylin’s topoisomers “both have the same structure”). Thus, claim 1 is limited to just one compound, plecanatide.

The experiments Patent Owner relies upon test plecanatide against the nearly identical structure in human uroguanylin under biologically-relevant conditions where both peptides have been purified to an equal fraction (>95%) of the active topoisomer. These experiments show significant differences in potency and binding affinity resulting from the addition of a single methylene group in the side chain (i.e., glutamate versus aspartate) at position 3. We agree with Patent Owner that there is no reason to believe that similar results would not be obtained if the plecanatide and human uroguanylin were tested as mixtures containing higher fractions of their inactive topoisomers. See PO Remand Br. 2–3, 9–10 (“proportionally decreasing the amount of active topoisomer, and thus increasing the amount of inactive topoisomer, would effectively add a null set to the biological activity already observed in the best-on-best comparison”); Ex. 2077, 21:14–21 (Petitioner’s expert testifying that the inactive topoisomers of uroguanylin and plecanatide have similarly, low activity). This is consistent with Marx’s observation, based on experimental data therein, that the amount of cGMP produced by mixtures of uroguanylin with different ratios of active to inactive topoisomers “were between the pure isoforms A and B, with a linear relation to the molar ratio of uroguanylin isomers” and thus “an antagonistic effect” of the inactive topoisomer “can be excluded.” Ex. 2010, 238. Moreover, Patent Owner’s data showing that plecanatide is less subject to interconversion applies to both its active and inactive topoisomeric forms. Ex. 2028, TRUL00018269.

The mere fact that Patent Owner's experiments did not also test peptide mixtures comprising higher fractions of the inactive topoisomer does not defeat Patent Owner's unexpected results showing. “[A]bsolute identity of scope’ is not required” for the claims to be reasonably commensurate in scope with the experimental data. Genetics Institute, LLC v. Novartis Vaccines and Diagnostics, Inc., 655 F.3d 1291, 1308–09 (Fed. Cir. 2011). Nor is Patent Owner “required to test every embodiment within the scope of his or her claims” where, as here, there is adequate basis to conclude that “other embodiments falling within the claim will behave in the same manner.” In re Kao, 639 F.3d 1057, 1068 (Fed. Cir. 2011).

Finally, to the extent Petitioner suggests that Patent Owner's results are not commensurate because they do not show that plecanatide's inactive topoisomer exhibits superior properties in comparison to uroguanylin's active topoisomer, we disagree. See Reply 22–23 (arguing that because Patent Owner told the EPO $^{33}$ “the difference in active and other topoisomers was as least 7-fold,” which is less than the difference distinguishing the prior art, Patent Owner cannot show that its results are commensurate in scope

with the claims). Patent Owner does not need to show that plecanatide excels over uroguanylin “in all common properties.” In re Chupp, 816 F.2d 643, 646 (Fed. Cir. 1987); see also Novo Nordisk A/S v. Caraco Pharm. Labs., Ltd., 719 F.3d 1346, 1366 (Fed. Cir. 2013) (explaining it was enough that the patent owner demonstrated unexpected results in a particular patient population, it was not necessary to show those results in other populations).

Indeed, the facts in Chupp are illustrative. Like the claims here, the claims in Chupp were limited to one chemical compound that differed from the closest prior art by a single methylene group. Chupp, 816 F.2d at 644. The applicant submitted evidence showing that its claimed compound, which functioned as a selective herbicide, demonstrated superior results when used with certain crops, but not others. Id. at 645. The Board of Patent Appeals found those results were not reasonably commensurate “because the claims were limited to no particular weed or crop.” Id. But the Federal Circuit reversed, explaining that it was not necessary to demonstrate that the claimed compound “produce[s] superior results in every environment in which the compound may be used” because the evidence demonstrating superior activity in at least some environments was sufficient to rebut the prima facie case of obviousness. Id. at 646–47. So too here, given the narrow scope of the claims, we find that it is enough that Patent Owner has shown that plecanatide exhibits superior results in the tested environment (i.e., solution-based assays of purified (>95%) active topoisomer uroguanylin versus plecanatide of the same purity), particularly as those experiments were designed to obtain information as to how the peptides might behave in vivo in the gastrointestinal tract. Ex. 2072 ¶ 11.

In sum, we find that there is a sufficient nexus between Patent Owner's results and the challenged claims and that those results are reasonably commensurate with the same. Accordingly, for all of reasons explained above, the unexpected results Patent Owner relies upon are probative of non-obviousness.

## E. Conclusion for Ground 1

Weighing the record as a whole in view of the findings and analysis above as well as our findings and analysis relating to Petitioner's prima facie showing in the Decision, we conclude that Petitioner has not met its burden to show that claim 1 would have been obvious over Currie and Li. The evidence suggesting that it would have been obvious to make the Glu $^{3}$ substitution because it is a conservative substitution (see Dec. 20–38 (§ III.E.ii)) is substantially outweighed by Patent Owner's comparatively strong evidence demonstrating unexpected results relating to improved interconversion stability, potency, and binding affinity from the Glu $^{3}$ substitution. See Millennium Pharms., Inc. v. Sandoz Inc., 862 F.3d 1356, 1368 (Fed. Cir. 2017) (“Evidence of objective indicia [such as unexpected results] ‘can be the most probative evidence of nonobviousness in the record,’ and . . . enable[s] ‘the court to avert the trap of hindsight.’”) (quoting Leo Pharm. Prods., Ltd. v. Rea, 726 F.3d 1346, 1358 (Fed. Cir. 2013). Indeed, the interconversion and potency categories of Patent Owner's unexpected results showing are particularly strong such that even without the additional results in Liu, we would still reach the same ultimate conclusion on obviousness. Accordingly, Petitioner has not shown by a preponderance of the evidence that claim 1 is unpatentable.

## F. Other Grounds

Petitioner's other grounds challenge claims 2–6 and are premised on Petitioner's showing in Ground 1 for the limitations of claim 1. See Pet. 40–53. Petitioner relies on the additional references asserted in these grounds only for their teachings regarding the additional limitations recited in the dependent claims. Id. Because Petitioner has not shown that claim 1 would have been obvious over Currie and Li, the premise for Petitioner's other grounds has not been established. Accordingly, Petitioner has not shown by a preponderance of the evidence that claims 2–6 are unpatentable.

## IV. PATENT OWNER'S MOTION TO EXCLUDE

Patent Owner moves to exclude page 86 of Exhibit 1067 (the “American Peptide letter”) as hearsay. Paper 101, 1 (“MTE”). This motion is essentially a continuation of Patent Owner’s earlier motion to exclude Exhibit 1067 (Paper 53), which we granted in part with respect to the materials submitted by the opponent in the EPO opposition. See Dec. 63–72 (referring to these materials as “the Currie submission” and excluding the same as hearsay). The American Peptide letter was apparently among the materials submitted by the EPO opponent. See Ex. 1067, 93 (“Opponent 1 files herewith a letter from the American Peptide Company”). However, at the time of our Decision, we did not discern Petitioner’s reliance on that letter so page 86 of Exhibit 1067 was not included amongst the pages we excluded in the Currie submission. Dec. 63, 69 (excluding “pages 88–98 and 120–127 of Exhibit 1067,” but dismissing the remainder of Patent Owner’s motion as moot).

On remand, Petitioner specifically relies on the American Peptide letter. Pet. Remand Resp. 25 (quoting Ex. 1067, 86). Patent Owner sought authorization to file the present motion to exclude, which we allowed. Paper 99. Petitioner opposes that motion. See Paper 102 (“MTE Opp.”).

Having considered both sides' arguments, we grant Patent Owner's motion. The American Peptide letter contains out of court statements from a third party submitted by the EPO opponent. The statements in the letter were not made under oath, nor is there evidence that Patent Owner was ever able to question the author of that letter or anyone else with personal knowledge of the statements therein. Petitioner argues that the American Peptide letter is evidence “to doubt that the ‘singular peak’ for uroguanylin in Liu represents the active topoisomer of uroguanylin” because it evidences that American Peptide “provided its customers with the ‘B’ (i.e., inactive) form of uroguanylin at the time.” Pet. Remand Resp. 25. That is, Petitioner relies on the American Peptide letter for the truth of the matters asserted therein. Such use plainly implicates the rule against hearsay and falls squarely within our prior reasoning for excluding the Currie submission portion of Exhibit 1067. We incorporate that analysis herein. See Dec. 61–72.

Petitioner asserts that the American Peptide letter is admissible under the residual hearsay exception in FRE 807. MTE Opp. 3–5. We disagree. The American Peptide letter was submitted by the EPO opponent specifically for the purpose of opposing a foreign counterpart to the '786 patent. We see no reason to distinguish it from the other materials previously excluded as hearsay in the Currie submission. Petitioner's assertion that it contains sufficient guarantees of trustworthiness because it was "provided by a neutral third-party" (American Peptide) and "an individual having first-

hand knowledge, [American Peptide Company's] VP of sales and marketing" is speculation. See id. at 3–4. Based on the record before us, Patent Owner has never been afforded an opportunity to examine anyone regarding the statements in this letter or even confirm that it is, in fact, a letter from the American Peptide Company. The mere fact that it was submitted in a foreign patent proceeding does not make it any more trustworthy than any other untested assertion offered by an adverse party in a legal proceeding.

For these reasons, we grant Patent Owner's motion to exclude page 86 of Exhibit 1067.

## V. CONCLUSION

Petitioner has not shown, by a preponderance of the evidence, that claims 1–6 of the '786 patent are unpatentable.
