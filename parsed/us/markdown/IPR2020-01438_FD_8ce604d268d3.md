## D. The '820 Patent (Ex. 1001)

## 1. Specification

The '820 patent is titled “COMPOSITIONS AND METHODS FOR TREATING PTERYGIUM.” Ex. 1001, code (54). The '820 patent describes pterygium as “an ocular surface disease, where an abnormal epithelial and fibroblast growth extends from the nasal or temporal conjunctiva across the limbus onto the cornea. Pterygium patients often experience symptoms of ocular discomfort, hyperemia, and are at risk of visual impairment if the lesion encroaches on the visual axis.” Id. at 1:26–31. According to the Specification, “hyperemia” and “ocular hyperemia” “refer[] to redness in an eye resulting from an excess of blood in the white of the eyes (sclera).” Id. at 7:19–21. “[R]educing hyperemia,” therefore, indicates a “reduction in redness and/or increase in whiteness in an affected eye.” Id. at 7:21–24. The '820 patent further explains that pterygium is “a multifactorial disease” and that “several growth factors such as VEGF and PDGF are potential pathological factors.” Id. at 6:49–51

The '820 patent teaches treating early, mid, and late stage pterygium. See id. at 10:61–12:30. In one embodiment, the specification “provides a method for treating primary and recurrent pterygium by administering (1) a multikinase inhibitor . . . to the eye of a subject in need of such treatment.” Id. at 1:41–45. “[T]he disclosed methods are performed before surgical removal of pterygium, in conjunction with surgery for pterygium removal or after surgical removal of pterygium, to reduce or prevent pterygium recurrence." Id. at 2:10–14.

The '820 patent discloses the treatment of human patients (see id. at 10:61–11:16), as well as a rabbit cornea suture model (see id. at 16:25–19:4 (Example 1)) and a human pterygium mouse model (see id. at 19:6–20:45 (Example 2)).

The term “treatment” encompasses any treatment of a disease in a mammal, particularly a human, and includes: (a) preventing the disease and/or symptom(s) from occurring in a subject who may be predisposed to the disease or symptom but has not yet been diagnosed as having it; (b) inhibiting the disease and/or symptom(s), i.e., arresting their development; or (c) relieving the disease symptom(s), i.e., causing regression of the disease and/or symptom(s).

Id. at 8:13–21.

“[T]he term ‘multikinase inhibitor’ (MKI) refers to drug compounds (e.g., a small molecule) that reduce or inhibit the expression or activity of two or more kinases, including, for example, intracellular and/or cell surface protein kinases.” Id. at 8:28–32. The multikinase inhibitor is administered to the affected eye in the form of topical ocular formulation, an ointment, a gel, a sustained release semisolid formulation, a sustained release solid formulation or ocular implant. Id. at 3:10–15.

Nintedanib and sunitinib are both multikinase inhibitors that inhibit the key VEGFR (vascular endothelial growth factor receptor) and PDGFR (platelet-derived growth factor receptor) families with a few non-overlapping targets. Id. at 12:1–2. “Quite unexpectedly, the inventors found that kinase inhibitors with substantial kinase target overlaps can still have different efficacies.” Id. at 18:2–5.

Nintedanib showed a clear trend of superiority to sunitinib in reducing neovascular area. While the two kinase inhibitors have substantial target overlaps, distinct efficacies were noted in this rabbit model. Another advantage of nintedanib is that it inhibits a smaller number of targets than sunitinib, allowing for better safety margins and higher doses.

Id. at 11:26–32.

## II. ANALYSIS

## A. Person of Ordinary Skill in the Art

Petitioner asserts that a person of ordinary skill in the art would have had at least a doctorate degree in medicinal chemistry, organic chemistry, or related discipline and at least two years of experience in the development of pharmacologic therapies for ophthalmic applications. Pet. 34 (citing Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 34–38). Patent Owner does not contest Petitioner's definition with respect to the ordinarily skilled artisan. PO Resp. 10. Accordingly, we apply Petitioner's definition of a person of ordinary skill in the art in your analysis. See, e.g., Okajima v. Bourdeau, 261 F.3d 1350, 1355 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (“[I]t is always preferable for the factfinder below to specify the level of skill it has found to apply to the invention at issue . . . ”).

## B. Claim construction

In this inter partes review, we construe the claims of the '820 patent "using the same claim construction standard that would be used to construe the claim in a civil action under 35 U.S.C. § 282(b)." 37 C.F.R. § 42.100(b) (2019). Under that standard, the words of a claim are generally given their "ordinary and customary meaning," which is the meaning the term would have to a person of ordinary skill at the time of the invention, in the context of the entire patent including the specification. Phillips v. AWH Corp., 415 F.3d 1303, 1312–13 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (en banc).

Petitioner offers proposed claim constructions for the preamble and the phrase “therapeutically effective amount.” See Pet. 35–39.

## 1. Preamble

The preamble of claim 1 recites: “A method for reducing hyperemia or symptoms thereof in pterygium in an affected eye of a subject in need of such treatment, without surgically excising a pterygium.” Ex. 1001, 24:33–35.

Petitioner offers a proposed claim construction that suggests only the first portion of the preamble is limiting, and the negative limitation “without surgically excising a pterygium” is not limiting because it is a statement of intended use. Pet. 35. Petitioner argues that just because some preamble language is limiting does not mean that the entire preamble is limiting. Pet. 38 (citing Cochlear Bone Anchored Sols. AB v. Oticon Med. AB, 958

F.3d 1348, 1355 (Fed. Cir. 2020) (citing TomTom, Inc. v. Adolph, 790 F.3d 1315, 1323 (Fed. Cir. 2015))). Petitioner therefore argues that the clause “without surgically excising a pterygium” should be accorded no weight, because the clause does not provide an antecedent basis and is unnecessary to give life or meaning to the claim. The ’820 patent states that “the disclosed methods are performed before surgical removal of pterygium, in conjunction with surgery for pterygium removal or after surgical removal of pterygium, to reduce or prevent pterygium recurrence.” Ex. 1001, 2:10–14. Petitioner’s position is that the methods can be performed regardless of surgical intervention and, therefore, the clause does not further limit the claim. Pet. 38.

In our Institution Decision, we found that the negative limitation in the preamble “without surgically excising a pterygium” is limiting because it describes the patient population that is receiving the treatment. Dec. 15. The preamble therefore is necessary to give life and meaning to the method of applying the composition to a particularly defined patient population. The ’820 patent specification provides that the treatment can be applied to patients before, during, or after surgery. Ex. 1001, 2:10–13, 12:24–27; see also Ex. 1003, 10 (“At the mid stage of pterygium, where fibrovascular growth extends from conjunctiva to limbus, the goal is to stabilize pterygium progression to delay or to avoid surgery for removing pterygium or even to induce pterygium regression from visual axis / central cornea.”). The specification, therefore, describes a larger patient group than the patient group recited in the claims. Neither Petitioner nor Patent Owner has responded to our preliminary construction of the negative limitation recited in the preamble, see generally Reply; PO Resp. 8–9, nor do we see any

reason to modify it here. Accordingly, we maintain our construction of the preamble for this Decision. Specifically, we find that the negative limitation “without surgically excising a pterygium” is not an intended use statement but instead defines a particular patient population receiving the treatment. Dec. 15.

## 2. “Therapeutically effective amount”

Petitioner contends that “therapeutically effective amount” refers to an amount of an agent effective to produce the intended result. Pet. 39.

According to the '820 patent, “the terms ‘therapeutically effective’ and ‘effective amount’ refer to that amount of an agent effective to produce the intended pharmacological, therapeutic or preventive result.” Ex. 1001, 7:35–38. The '820 patent further explains that “[t]herapeutically effective dosages in the methods described herein can be determined by the treating physician.” Id. at 7:50–52.

In our Institution Decision, we found “therapeutically effective” and “effective amount” are reasonably construed as the amount of an agent that will produce the intended result as assessed by the person of ordinary skill in the art. Dec. 16. Petitioner does not address our construction in the Reply. See generally Reply. Patent Owner also does not contest this construction. PO Resp. 10. Accordingly, we maintain our construction for this Decision.

## D. Alleged Anticipation over Allgenesis PCT

Petitioner provides a detailed explanation to show that Allgenesis PCT sets forth each limitation of challenged claims 4 and 5 to a person of ordinary skill in the art. Pet. 49–58. Patent Owner opposes. PO Resp. 11–23.

According to Petitioner, Allgenesis PCT is subject to the first inventor to file provisions of the AIA. See Pet. 40 n.3. Thus, Petitioner contends that, for the purpose of applying Allgenesis $\mathrm{PCT}^5$ as art, the earliest effective filing date of Allgenesis PCT is June 22, 2015. Patent Owner does not appear to dispute Allgenesis PCT effective filing date. See PO Reply 1, 11.

Whether Allgenesis PCT is prior art to the '820 patent depends on whether the '820 patent is entitled to its earliest effective filing date of June 6, 2015. Petitioner contends that the '820 patent is not entitled to the June 6, 2015 filing date of the '063 provisional application, because the '063 provisional application does not provide sufficient written descriptive support or enablement under 35 U.S.C. § 112 for the claims of the '820 patent. See Pet. 23–34. Additionally, Petitioner contends that the Office previously determined that “Patent Owner’s June 6, 2015 priority document does not provide sufficient written description or enablement for the subject matter in its later-published nonprovisional applications.” Pet. 33 (citing Ex. 1015, 619–27, 637–54, 678–82).

## 1. The earliest priority date of the '820 patent

A claim for priority under 35 U.S.C. § 119(e) requires the patent application claiming priority be “disclosed in the manner provided by section 112(a) (other than the requirement to disclose the best mode) in [the] provisional application” to which priority is claimed. For the ’820 patent to have an effective filing date of June 6, 2015, the ’063 provisional application must describe (providing written description and enablement support for) the subject matter of the disputed claims. MPEP § 2151 (9th ed., rev. 10.2019, June 2020) (“a U.S. patent document is effective as prior art as of the filing date of the earliest application to which benefit or priority is claimed and which describes the subject matter relied upon, regardless of whether the earliest such application is a U.S. provisional or non-provisional application”).

The Federal Circuit has explained the conditions under which an issued patent is entitled to the filing date of a provisional application for prior art purposes, stating

for a non-provisional application to claim priority to a provisional application for prior art purposes, ‘the specification of the provisional [application] must contain a written description of the invention . . . in such full, clear, concise, and exact terms, to enable an ordinarily skilled artisan to practice the invention claimed in the non-provisional application.’ Further, we have previously stated that ‘for the non-provisional utility application to be afforded the priority date of the provisional application . . . the written description of the provisional must adequately support the claims of the non-provisional application.

Amgen Inc. v. Sanofi, 872 F.3d 1367, 1380 (Fed. Cir. 2017) (quoting Dynamic Drinkware, LLC v. National Graphics, Inc., 800 F.3d 1375, 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2015) and New Railhead Mfg., LLC v. Vermeer Mfg. Co., 298 F.3d 1290, 1294 (Fed. Cir. 2002)) (alterations in original).

Before we address the parties' arguments with respect to the written description and enablement issues raised in the Petition, we find it helpful to review the state of the art before the filing date of the '063 provisional application.

## (iii) Discussion

The written description requirement is found in section 112 of the patent statute, which provides that the patent’s specification must contain “a written description of the invention, and of the manner and process of making and using it.” 35 U.S.C. § 112(a). A specification that “reasonably conveys to those skilled in the art that the inventor had possession of the claimed subject matter as of the filing date” has adequate written description of the claimed invention. Ariad Pharms., Inc. v. Eli Lilly & Co., 598 F.3d 1336, 1351 (Fed. Cir. 2010). “[T]he test requires an objective inquiry into

the four corners of the specification from the perspective of a person of ordinary skill in the art.” Id.

The case law makes clear that “[c]ompliance with the written description requirement is essentially a fact-based inquiry that will ‘necessarily vary depending on the nature of the invention claimed.’” Enzo Biochem, Inc. v. Gen-Probe Inc., 323 F.3d 956, 963 (Fed. Cir. 2002) (quoting Vas-Cath Inc. v. Mahurkar, 935 F.2d 1555, 1562 (Fed. Cir. 1991)). In other words, what is required to meet the written description requirement “varies with the nature and scope of the invention at issue, and with the scientific and technologic knowledge already in existence.” Capon v. Eshhar, 418 F.3d 1349, 1357 (Fed. Cir. 2005).

A review of the '063 provisional application establishes that application contains similar words and limitations as those disclosed in the '820 patent. Compare Ex. 1003, 12 (claim 1: “A method for reducing hyperemia and symptoms in pterygia, stabilizing pterygium, inducing pterygium regression from visual axis/central cornea and treating pterygium recurrence following pterygiectomy, to an affected eye of a subject in need of such a treatment (1) a multikinase inhibitor; (2) an antimetabolite; and (3) a combination therapy of a multikinase inhibitor and an antimetabolite, in a proper dosage form.”) with Ex. 1001, 24:33–47 (claim 1: “A method for reducing hyperemia or symptoms thereof in pterygium in an affected eye of a subject in need of such treatment, without surgically excising a pterygium, comprising administering to the affected eye of the subject a therapeutically effective amount of a multikinase inhibitor.”). Because the '063 provisional application describes “reducing hyperemia and symptoms in pterygia, stabilizing pterygium, inducing pterygium regression from visual

axis/central cornea,” we determined that the ’063 provisional application reasonably supports treating a patient population that has not had surgical intervention immediately preceding the treatment.

The '063 provisional application also discloses a list of intracellular and/or cell surface protein kinases that may be the target of the multikinase inhibitor. Ex. 1003, 12 (claim 2). In addition, the '063 provisional application provides a list 40 multikinase inhibitors, specifically listing nintedanib. Id. (claim 3). The disclosure of the '063 provisional application, therefore, provides ipsis verbis support for the use of the multikinase inhibitor nintedanib recited in claims 4 and 5 of the '820 patent. Compare Ex. 1003, 12 (claim 3 (showing a list of inhibitors) with Ex. 1001, 24:38–47 (showing a list of inhibitors); see also Ex. 1025, 465–466 (the examiner providing a list of what words do not appear in the '063 provisional, implying that all other words find support).

We are not persuaded by Petitioner's contention that the '063 provisional application provides insufficient descriptive support for nintedanib as a treatment for reducing hyperemia or symptoms thereof in pterygium. We agree with Patent Owner that the '063 provisional application discloses a limited list of 40 multikinase inhibitors, which includes nintedanib, sunitinib, and pazopanib. See Ex. 1003, 10; PO Resp. 14 ("The '063 provisional application then goes on to specifically list nintedanib as one of those multikinase inhibitors."). Patent Owner contends, and we agree, that the treatment methods recited in claims 4 and 5 are supported by the results obtained with sunitinib. PO Resp. 14–15; Ex. 1010 ¶¶ 121–147; Ex. 1014, 521. Because sunitinib worked in an animal model for pterygia, Patent Owner contends that one of ordinary skill in the art

would understand that nintedanib would work similarly because both are multikinase inhibitors with very similar pharmacological potencies related to the VEGF receptors. PO Resp. 15; Ex. 2005 ¶ 46 (“[B]ecause both share key physiochemical properties, similar known formulation approaches would work equally well for both. The potency determines how effective a drug will work when it reaches the target tissue and the physiochemical properties dictate how the drug can be formulated.”). We credit Patent Owners expert over Petitioner’s expert on this point because Roth (discussed below) further supports this finding.

We are not persuaded by Petitioner's contention that reciting nintedanib in the specification of the '063 provisional application is insufficient to provide written descriptive support for the effective treatment using this multikinase inhibitor. Reply 13 (citing Nuvo Pharms., 923 F.3d at 1380). Unlike the facts in Nuvo, the record here supports Patent Owner's position that a person of ordinary skill in the art would have expected other kinase inhibitors to work similarly to the disclosed sunitinib.

Specifically, the prior art provides that the multikinase inhibitor – pazopanib $^{12}$ – has also been formulated for ocular application and was shown to provide significant reduction in corneal neovascularization (CNV). Ex. 1008 ¶¶ 58–62; Ex. 1009, 542. Roth further establishes that systemic therapy using nintedanib, sunitinib, or sorafenib in patients with cancer provides similar efficacy with respect to neovascularization. Ex. 1011, 1055. The evidence from Roth’s systemic application further supports Patent

Owner's position that sunitinib, pazopanib, and nintedanib work similarly. Therefore, the list of inhibitors recited in the '063 provisional application is more than just a random list based on wishful thinking as suggested by Petitioner.

The facts in the present appeal can also be distinguished from the facts in Biogen International because the disclosure of the '063 provisional application is limited to a single disease, pterygium, and treating its symptoms by topically administering an effective amount of a multikinase inhibitor. Biogen International, GMBH v. Mylan Pharmaceutical Inc., 18 F.4th 1333, 1344 (Fed. Cir. Nov. 30, 2021); Tr. 11:5–7. $^{13}$ The '063 provisional application states the treatment is based on “the support of our own in vitro and in vivo laboratory experimental data and of animal and clinical results reported in the literature, to invent treatment options for various stages of pterygia.” Ex. 1003, 9.

The prior art before the filing date of the '063 provisional application supports topical ocular application of two multikinase inhibitors sunitinib and pazopanib. Ex. 1008 ¶ 50; Ex. 1009, 542; Ex. 1010 ¶¶ 11, 121–147; Ex. 1014, 520. The application of either sunitinib or pazopanib resulted in a reduction of corneal neovascularization. Ex. 1009, 542; Ex. 1014, 524. Roth establishes that the multikinase inhibitors axitinib, nintedanib, pazopanib, sunitinib, and soranfenib behave similarly at least in systemic application to reduce neovascularization. See Ex. 1011, Abstract, 1055, 1056. Based on these disclosures before the filing date of the '063 provisional application in conjunction with the knowledge of the ordinarily skilled artisan, we agree with Patent Owner that the specification of the '063 provisional application provides sufficient written descriptive support for the effective topical ocular administration of nintedanib.

## (iv) Summary

In considering the totality of the evidence, we agree with Patent Owner that there is sufficient written descriptive support in the '063 provisional application for the application of a therapeutically effective amount nintedanib for the purpose of reducing hyperemia or symptoms thereof in a patient population with pterygium.

## (iii) Discussion

“Undue experimentation” is a test for enablement. In re Wands, 858 F.2d 731 (Fed. Cir. 1988). That some experimentation is required, even a considerable amount, is not “undue” if, e.g., it is merely routine or the specification provides a reasonable amount of guidance as to the direction in which the experimentation should proceed. Id. at 737. Factors to consider include “(1) the quantity of experimentation necessary, (2) the amount of direction or guidance presented, (3) the presence or absence of working examples, (4) the nature of the invention, (5) the state of the prior art, (6) the relative skill of those in the art, (7) the predictability or unpredictability of the art, and (8) the breadth of the claims.” Id. The above “Wands factors” are illustrative, rather than mandatory, and therefore “all of the factors need not be reviewed when determining whether a disclosure is enabling.” Enzo Biochem, Inc. v. Calgene, Inc., 188 F.3d 1362, 1371 (Fed. Cir. 1999). What matters for the purpose of the enablement inquiry are the particular facts being considered. Amgen, Inc. v. Chugai Pharm. Co., 927 F.2d 1200, 1213 (Fed. Cir. 1991).

## (1) Nature of the invention

The invention is directed to the topical application of a multikinase inhibitor – nintendanib – in a pterygium patient population in order to reduce hyperemia or symptoms of pterygium. The prior art supports the ocular application of other known structurally diverse multikinase inhibitors, in this case sunitinib and pazopanib, as well as the use of a monoclonal antibody bevacizumab, for reducing neovascularization when directly applied to affected tissues. See above II.D.1.a.i–iv; Ex. 1008; Ex. 1009, Ex. 1010; Ex. 1011; Ex. 1014.

## (2) Breadth of the claims

The claims are directed to the single multikinase inhibitor – nintedanib – for the treatment of pterygium in an affected eye of a subject that did not have the tissue surgically removed. The breadth of the claims is therefore narrowly focused on a single multikinase inhibitor.

## (3) Working examples

The '063 provisional application explains that there are “various mechanisms involved in forming pterygia, [and] treatment strategies should target multiple pathways including proliferation, inflammation, fibrosis and angiogenesis, that have been identified in its pathogenesis. Based upon these, we have hypothesized several strategies, with the support of our own in vitro and in vivo laboratory experimental data and of animal and clinical results reported in the literature, to invent treatment options for various stages of pterygia.” Ex. 1003, 9 (emphasis added).

This treatment hypothesis is supported by some experimental data conducted in our laboratory, where sunitinib, a potent inhibitor of multiple protein kinases of VEGFR2, PDGFRβ, KIT, RET, CSF1R and FLT3, has shown to be efficacious after three-times daily dosing for 14 days in an in vivo rabbit corneal suture model, an animal model for pterygia, in a prototype ocular solution formulation. Similar findings have also been reported in the literature, where topical ocular administration of sunitinib has shown more effective than bevacizumab via a subconjunctival injection, for the inhibition of corneal neovascularization in the rabbit corneal suture model.

Ex. 1003, 9 (emphasis added). Thus, the '063 provisional application provides a single working example using sunitinib in a rabbit corneal suture model and recognizes that their findings are similar to those reported in the prior art. The '063 provisional application thereby suggests that many of the details with respect to formulating these multikinase inhibitors “can be readily determined by the ordinarily skilled artisan, and can e.g., be obtained in animal models and in clinical studies reported in the literatures, for determining dosage, safety and efficacy according to standard methods known in the art.” Id. at 11. The '063 provisional application, therefore, evidences that much of the information relevant to practicing claims 4 and 5 would have been available to the skilled artisan by consulting the literature or relies on the use of standard art recognized methods. Id.; see also Ex. 1032, 51:10–15 (“[W]e know the potency of all of these compounds someplace. But I would imagine they have been described and one can compare the potency and solubility of those compounds to be able to predict which of them could be effective in the model and which are unlikely to be effective in the model.”).

Ex. 1003, 9. The '063 provisional application also discloses that “some experimental data conducted in our laboratory, where sunitinib, a potent inhibitor of multiple protein kinases of VEGFR2, PDGFRβ, KIT, RET, CSF1R and FLT3, has shown to be efficacious after three-times daily dosing for 14 days in an in vivo rabbit corneal suture model, an animal model for

pterygia, in a prototype ocular solution formulation.” Ex. 1003, 9–10. Just because the ’063 provisional application does not provide numbers and tables associated with their observed results does not mean we should not credit their experimental observations with regard to sunitinib. Based on these disclosures in the prior art and the disclosure in the '063 provisional application, we determine that there is sufficient showing that the ocular application of a known

multikinase inhibitor reasonably results in a reduction of neovascularization when applied to the eye. The record supports the conclusion that structurally diverse multikinase inhibitors, in this case sunitinib and pazopanib, as well as the antibody kinase inhibitor bevacizumab function similarly in reducing neovascularization. See above II.D.1.a.i–iv; Ex. 1008; Ex. 1009, Ex. 1010; Ex. 1014.

We disagree with Petitioner's contention that Rasmusson supports its position that the '063 provisional application does not provide an enabling disclosure for claims 4 and 5. It is well settled that patent applicants “are not required to disclose every species encompassed by their claims, even in an unpredictable art.” In re Angstadt, 537 F.2d 498, 502-03 (CCPA 1976). “[A] specification need not disclose what is well known in the art.” Genentech Inc. v. ## (5) Quantity of experimentation Effective amount

Petitioner contends that there is an undue amount of work required to determine if the forty inhibitors listed in the '063 provisional application are effective at reducing hyperemia or symptoms thereof in pterygium in a subject. Specifically, Petitioner contends that this would require at least 480 rabbits and this is a considerable and undue amount of work. Reply 31–32 (citing Ex. 1005 ¶ 74 (“explaining that testing of 480 rabbits plus controls ‘would have been extremely expensive and time-consuming’”); Ex. 1032, 51:16–55:7, 57:4–16; Ex. 1033 ¶¶ 42–44); Tr. 22:6–23; Ex. 2009, 70:18–22. The test for enablement is “not merely quantitative.” PPG Indus. v. Guardian Indus. Corp., 75 F.3d 1558, 1564 (Fed. Cir. 1996). On the contrary, “a considerable amount of experimentation is permissible, if it is merely routine.” Id.; In re Vaeck, 947 F.2d 488, 495 (Fed. Cir. 1991) (“That some experimentation may be required is not fatal; the issue is whether the amount of experimentation required is ‘undue.’”).

The remaining claims are limited to a single multikinase inhibitor – nintedanib. Therefore, the issue for those claims is whether undue experimentation is required to determine the effective amount of nintedanib for reducing hyperemia or symptoms thereof. The rabbit corneal suture model as well as the mouse model are both well-known and well-established animal models for testing neovascularization. Ex. 1001, 6:60–64, 16:30–38, 19:6–14; Ex. 1032, 53:4–14; Ex. 1014. At the time of filing the '063 provisional application, testing multikinase inhibitors for their ability to inhibit vascularization was routine. See Ex. 1008; Ex. 1009; Ex. 1010; Ex. 1014; Ex. 2005 ¶ 46.

Petitioner does not establish that testing nintedanib, one of the listed inhibitors the '063 provisional application, requires an undue amount of work. See Ex. 2009, 71:15–21; Ex. 1005 ¶ 74. Just because the testing may be extensive and even expensive, or because it requires the use of animals, does not establish that the effort required is undue.

Patent Owner's expert, Dr. Hohman, explains that the '063 provisional application shows that sunitinib, a potent inhibitor of multiple protein kinases, has been shown to be efficacious in a rabbit corneal suture model, an animal model for pterygia, using a prototype ocular solution formulation. Ex. 2005 ¶ 46. Based on the disclosure in the specification of the '063 provisional application and what was known in the art, Dr. Hohman avers that

[a] person of skill would understand from this that because sunitinib worked in an animal model for pterygia, so too would nintedanib because both are multikinase inhibitors with very similar pharmacological potencies on the VEGF receptors. Moreover, because both share key physiochemical properties, similar known formulation approaches would work equally well for both. The potency determines how effective a drug will work when it reaches the target tissue and the physiochemical properties dictate how the drug can be formulated.

Ex. 2005 ¶ 46; but see Ex. 1033 ¶ 23 (Dr. Malone “disagree[s] with Dr. Hohman’s opinion. Sunitinib and nintedanib have different and unique inhibition profiles, which would have been readily apparent and known to a person of ordinary skill in the art.”).

Even though nintedanib may have a different kinase inhibition profile with respect to the other multikinase inhibitors listed in the '063 provisional application, there is still significant overlap between the kinase targets for sunitinib, pazopanib, sorafenib, and nintedanib. See Ex. 1001, 9:1–44 (Table 1); Ex. 1033 ¶ 24. Dr. Hohman avers that “because sunitinib worked in an animal model for pterygia, so too would nintedanib because both are multikinase inhibitors.” Ex. 2005 ¶ 46. We credit Dr. Hohman’s testimony because it finds support in the prior art.

For example, Amparo exemplified testing the multikinase inhibitor pazopanib in the form of eye drops in human subjects and shows that it is effective at reducing corneal neovascularization (CNV). Ex. 1009, 542 (“[T]opical pazopanib is safe and well tolerated, and can effectively reduce established CNV as measured by invasion area, vessel length, and neovascular area.”). Pazopanib is a multikinase inhibitor that has significant overlap with the kinase targets of not only sunitinib but also nintedanib. See Ex. 1001, 9:1–45 (Table 1); Ex. 1016, 18, 24, 26; Ex. 1033 ¶ 24. Peyman and Pérez-Santonja exemplify testing sunitinib topically as an eye drop for treating corneal neovascularization. See Ex. 1010; Ex. 1014; Ex. 2009, 57:14–58:4 (acknowledging that topical application of sunitinib and pazopanib was known). Here, the prior art shows that two different multikinase inhibitors pazopanib and sunitinib are both effective for treating corneal neovascularization. This teaching, in conjunction with the teaching that in systemic therapy applications nintedanib functioned similarly to sunitinib

and soranfenib, supports Dr. Hohman's conclusion that nintedanib is expected to function similarly when topically applied. See Ex. 1011, 1055.

## Formulation

The next issue is whether undue experimentation is required for formulating nintedanib for topical application.

Petitioner contends that “[t]he single brief description of experimental data based on sunitinib fails to demonstrate the usefulness of the long list of multikinase inhibitors in the provisional.” Pet. 30 (citing Rasmusson, 413 F.3d at 1323). According to Petitioner, a number of the multikinase inhibitors listed in the ’063 provisional application are barely soluble in aqueous solutions at neutral pH. Pet. 31 (citing Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 67–69; Ex. 1026, 38 (“BIBF 1120 ethanesulfonate is classified as a drug substance with low solubility according to BCS (Biopharmaceutical Classification System).”)). Petitioner’s expert, Dr. Malone, identified that sunitinib has a most basic pKa of 9.09, nintedanib has a most basic calculated pKa of 7.43, and sorafenib has a most basic pKa of 2.66. Ex. 1005 ¶ 67. “Based on the relationship between the pKa of a compound and the pH of a solution containing the compound, the extent to which a compound will be protonated or deprotonated in the solution can be predicted.” Id. (n.3). Dr. Malone concludes that in terms of their physiochemical properties, these inhibitors are not equivalent. Id. ¶¶ 67–67. Dr. Malone finds that solubility is also an important factor to consider when formulating a compound for topical administration. Id. ¶ 69. According to Dr. Malone, nintedanib has a low measured solubility in aqueous solution. Ex. 1005 ¶ 69 (citing Ex. 1026, 8, 36, 38, 39).

[a]mong the physiochemical properties, the most important physiochemical property to an ophthalmic formulator is solubility.... [T]he estimated water solubilities for sunitinib and nintedanib at neutral pH are 6.3 mg/L and 9.6 mg/L, respectively. A person of skill would view these numbers as the same.

## Ex. 2005 ¶ 48.

## e) New argument

## (i) Petitioner's Contentions

Petitioner’s Reply introduces new arguments and new evidence to bolster their lack of enablement arguments presented in the Petition. Petitioner contends that there are barriers for delivery of nintedanib to the target tissue that would impact the efficacy of the treatment. Reply 22–32. In addition to tears that remove the drug from the surface of the eye, barriers include “[e]fflux transporters, such as P-glycoprotein (P-gp) which is expressed in cornea and conjunctiva, would also limit drug absorption into pterygium” and reaching the intracellular target. Because of the extensive metabolism by esterases associated with nintedanib yielding metabolites with lower activity, one of ordinary skill in the art would recognize that this is another barrier for effectively treating patients with pterygium. Id. at 24 (citing Ex. 1033 ¶¶ 36–38). Petitioner notes that “sunitinib is not metabolized by esterases like nintedanib, and even when metabolized, sunitinib’s major metabolite is equipotent with the drug compound itself.” Id. (citing Ex. 1033 ¶¶ 39, 41; Ex. 2013, 6, 35).

## (ii) Patent Owner's Contentions

Patent Owner contends that Petitioner in the Reply is presenting “two new theories concerning alleged difficulties related to delivering nintedanib to target tissue.” Sur-reply 11. The new theories are the “esterase theory” and the “efflux transporter theory.” Sur-reply 11–12; Tr. 41:7–10. ## (iii) Discussion

The legal authority applicable to Patent Owner's argument is summarized below.

Our reviewing court has explained:

It is of the utmost importance that petitioners in the IPR proceedings adhere to the requirement that the initial petition identify “with particularity” the “evidence that supports the grounds for the challenge to each claim.” 35 U.S.C. § 312(a)(3).

Unlike district court litigation—where parties have greater freedom to revise and develop their arguments over time and in response to newly discovered material—the expedited nature of IPRs bring with it an obligation for petitioners to make their case in their petition to institute. While the Board’s requirements are strict ones, they are requirements of which petitioners are aware when they seek to institute an IPR.

Intelligent Bio-Systems, Inc. v. Illumina Cambridge Ltd., 821 F.3d 1359, 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (holding that petitioner's reply brief and accompanying declaration exceeded the scope of the reply under 37 C.F.R. § 42.23(b)). In a subsequent case, the Federal Circuit held that a petitioner's reply arguments “amount to an entirely new theory . . . absent from the petition” and that “[s]hifting arguments in this fashion is foreclosed by statute, our precedent, and Board guidelines.” Wasica Fin. GmbH v. Cont’l Auto. Sys., Inc., 853 F.3d 1272, 1286 (Fed. Cir. 2017) (holding that the Board did not err in declining to consider petitioner’s reply argument).

Our rules require that a petition include “[a] full statement of the reasons for the relief requested” and that “[a] reply may only respond to arguments raised in the corresponding . . . patent owner response.” 37 C.F.R. §§ 42.22(a)(2), 42.23(b).

The trial practice guide elaborates on these rules, explaining that “Petitioner may not submit new evidence or argument in reply that it could have presented earlier, e.g. to make out a prima facie case of unpatentability.” Consolidated Trial Practice Guide November 2019 (“CTPG”), 73.

Applying the legal authority, we agree with Patent Owner that Petitioner's Reply improperly introduces new arguments and new evidence to bolster their lack of enablement arguments presented in the Petition. Petitioner does not explain why they are raising the issue of metabolism $^{16}$ and efflux transporters as being a barrier to drug delivery at this stage. Reply 22–23 (citing Ex. 1033 ¶¶ 36–41). Nor does Petitioner explain why it could not have introduced this evidence earlier. Under our rules, this is

procedurally improper as it belatedly presents additional rationales for questioning sufficiency of the support presented in the specification of the '063 provisional application. 37 C.F.R. § 42.23(b); CTPG 73–74. Because we determine that these new arguments are belatedly presented, we do not consider the merits of the arguments.

Even though “King’s experimental data is limited to animal studies,” one of ordinary skill in the art before the effective filing date of the claimed invention would have looked to Amparo for providing “further insight in the development of a pharmaceutical therapy for pterygium and its symptoms” using multikinase inhibitors in human subjects. Id. ¶ 141.

## c) Discussion

## (i) Motivation to combine

Inquiry into motivation to combine considers whether a person having skill in the art would have believed that there was a reason for reaching the claimed invention in the first place. Intelligent Bio-Systems 821 F.3d at 1368; see also KSR Int'l Co. v. Teleflex Inc., 550 U.S. 398, 418 (2007) (“it can be important to identify a reason that would have prompted a person of ordinary skill in the relevant field to combine the elements in the way the claimed new invention does”).

Petitioner relies on King for teaching the application of pazopanib in the form of an eye drops. Pet. 62–69. Petitioner contends that King does not mischaracterize pterygium as a posterior ocular disorder, but instead identifies pterygium as a disorder of ocular angiogenesis and lists it along with several other anterior ocular disorders that may be treated using King's methods. Reply 34 ("[P]terygium is correctly identified [in King] as a "disorder of ocular angiogenesis" and is listed with several other anterior ocular disorders that may be treated using King's methods." (Ex. 1008 ¶ 28; see also Ex. 1032, 76:11-77:21)).

According to Petitioner, King's teachings are not limited to systemic delivery. "King provides examples of topical ocular formulations of

multikinase inhibitors in the form of a solution. King specifically teaches topical eye drops formulated as a solution comprising pazopanib and pharmaceutically acceptable excipients," as well as testing the topical ocular formulations. Pet. 65 (Ex. 1008 ¶¶ 48, 58–59). King is thus "not limited to oral administration." Reply 33 (citing Ex. 1008 ¶¶ 48, 50, 58–62; Ex. 1005, ¶ 152). "Dr. Hohman[, Patent Owner's expert,] conceded that King's experimental results showed that drug also was delivered "to the conjunctiva...separate from the systemic delivery." Reply 34 (citing Ex. 1032, 78:5-19).

“Petitioner’s proposed combination relies on the topical route of administration [of a multikinase inhibitor] taught by King and Amparo—not systemic absorption.” Reply 35 (citing Pet. 65; Ex. 1033 ¶ 55). We agree with Petitioner, and find there is sufficient motivation to combine King and Amparo based on each references’ teachings of topically applying the multikinase inhibitor pazopanib for the purpose of inhibiting neovascularization.

Having determined that there is motivation to combine the references, we also agree with Petitioner that selection of nintedanib from King's disclosed list would have been obvious because King identifies the inhibitors as suitable alternatives. Pet. 65 (“It would have been obvious to a POSITA to use nintedanib, as taught by King, as the multikinase inhibitor in a topical ocular formulations as taught by King and Amparo.”); Tr. 7:7–9 (“We’re relying upon the expressed teaching in King that nintedanib was a suitable inhibitor for the treatment of pterygium.”). “[P]icking and choosing [from a list] may be entirely proper in the making of a 103, obviousness rejection, where the applicant must be afforded an opportunity to rebut with objective evidence any inference of obviousness." In re Arkley, 455 F.2d 586, 588 (CCPA 1972).

(ii) Missing limitation: “without surgical excision of the pterygium”

Patent Owner contends that claims 4 and 5 are directed at treating a patient “without surgical excision of the pterygium, which was the conventional treatment for pterygia as of June 6, 2015.” PO Resp. 34; Surn-reply 21. Patent Owner notes that Amparo studied safety not efficacy of the pazopanib administration, and explicitly cautions against drawing conclusions regarding efficacy of the topical pazopanib administration. Id. at 32. According to Patent Owner, Amparo provides data for only three patients, none of whom had pterygia. Based on this limited disclosure, “Dr. Hohman explains, a person of ordinary skill, reading Amparo, would not have been able to determine whether topically applied pazopanib could successfully treat pterygium.” PO Resp. 33 (citing Ex. 2005 ¶ 70).

Petitioner contends that “[n]one of the methods disclosed in King for treating pterygium involve the administration of a multikinase inhibitor in conjunction with surgical excision.” Pet. 63 (citing Ex. 1008 ¶ 153). Furthermore, Petitioner asserts that the patients included in Amparo’s treatment protocol were selected because they did not have either recent or planned surgery. Id.; see also Reply 36 (“Nothing in Amparo disparages treating patient without recent or planned surgery” (citing Ex. 1033 ¶ 57)).

Amparo selected “[o]nly patients with clinically stable CNV [corneal neovascularization] that extended at least 1-mm beyond the limbus . . . [and excluded patients that had] ocular surgery on the studied eye within the previous 3 months.” Ex. 1009, 2. This means that Patient-15 in Amparo, the one with recurring pterygium, did not have surgery at least within three months prior to participation in the trial. Id. at 3 (Table 2); Ex. 1005 ¶ 155. This reasonably supports the position that Amparo does not surgically excise $^{18}$ the pterygium while at the same time applying the pazopanib eye drop therapy. While Amapro may have provided before and after pictures for only three patients (see Ex. 1009, 5–6), Amparo concludes that “[t]reatment with topical pazopanib led to a 22% decrease of corneal neovessels (NA) and NA is a function of vessel caliber and length.” Ex. 1009, 6, 7 (“[T]he results of this study indicate that topical treatment with pazopanib 0.5% leads to a statistically significant reduction in CNV based on decrease in NA, VL, and IA.”). Treatment as disclosed in the ’820 patent encompasses prevention, arresting development, and regression. See Ex. 1001, 8:16–27. A reduction of corneal vessel with treatment in the patient population as described in Amparo, which includes a patient having recurring pterygium, reasonably supports reducing hyperemia or symptoms of pterygium as encompassed by claims 4 and 5 of the ’820 patent. See Ex. 1005 ¶ 155.

Accordingly, we conclude that Petitioner has demonstrated that the limitation “without surgical excision of the pterygium” is sufficiently supported in the teachings of King and Amparo.

## (iii) Expectation of success

King teaches applying “therapeutically effective amounts of the suitable inhibitor, and one or more pharmaceutically acceptable carriers, diluents, or excipients” to the eye of an animal subject. Ex. 1008 ¶ 30. King teaches “[t]he suitable inhibitor can be various inhibitors that inhibit one or more of the following receptors: VEGFR1, VEGFR2, VEGFR3, PDGFRalpha, PDGFRbeta, c-kit, and/or FGFR.” Ex. 1008 ¶ 19. King includes nintedanib (BIBF-1120) in a list of suitable inhibitors. Ex. 1008 ¶ 19.

King teaches the “[t]opical [application of] eye drops [that] were formulated in a buffered 7% cyclodextrin solution containing 5 mg/ml pazopanib.” Ex. 1008 ¶ 48. “Pazopanib (approximately 30 μl of a 5 mg/ml sterile-filtered solution) was administered twice daily [onto the eyes of rats]. Animals of the control group received a vehicle only.” Ex. 1008 ¶ 50. King teaches that in their choroidal neovascularization rat model, “[w]hen areas of vessel leakage were followed up by fluorescence angiography from post laser days 7 to 14, topically administered pazopanib significantly reduced development of [choroidal neovascularization] lesions. In contrast, leakage of [choroidal neovascularization] lesions continued to progress in eyes of the control group treated with the vehicle.” Ex. 1008 ¶ 59, see also id. ¶¶ 60–61. King, therefore, teaches that topical administration of pazopanib resulted in a reduction in the lesion size in treated eyes.

Amparo teaches using “[o]nly patients with clinically stable CNV that extended at least 1-mm beyond the limbus.” Id. at 2. Patients were instructed to apply one drop of pazopanib 0.5% solution into the eye four times per day for 3 weeks. Id. The patient population tested included a patient having a

diagnosis of “MGD, rosacea, DES, [and] recurrent pterygium.” Id. at 3 (table 2, patient 15). “The primary efficacy outcome measures were the size and extent of CNV. The efficacy of topical pazopanib treatment on CNV was evaluated by comparing corneal photographs acquired at baseline with photographs acquired on the follow-up visits.” Id. at 2. “Treatment with topical pazopanib led to a 22% decrease of corneal neovessels (NA) and NA is a function of vessel caliber and length.” Id. at 6. The study shows that “topical pazopanib is safe and well tolerated, and can effectively reduce established CNV as measured by invasion area, vessel length, and neovascular area.” Id.

"The reasonable expectation of success requirement refers to the likelihood of success in combining references to meet the limitations of the claimed invention." Intelligent Bio-Systems, Inc. v. Illumina Cambridge Ltd., 821 F.3d 1359, 1367 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (emphasis added). Expectation of success is assessed from the perspective of a person of ordinary skill in the art, at the time the invention was made. See Life Techs. Inc. v. Clontech Labs. Inc., 224 F.3d 1320, 1326 (Fed. Cir. 2000).

It is obvious to those skilled in the art to substitute one known equivalent for another. See In re Omeprazole Patent Litig., 483 F.3d 1364, 1374 (Fed. Cir. 2007) (“[T]his court finds no . . . error in [the] conclusion that it would have been obvious to one skilled in the art to substitute one ARC [alkaline reactive compound] for another.”). “Conclusive proof of efficacy is not necessary to show obviousness. All that is required is a reasonable expectation of success.” Hoffman-La Roche Inc. v. Apotex Inc., 748 F.3d 1326, 1331 (Fed. Cir. 2014).

Based on the teachings of King and Amparo, we agree with Petitioner that it would have been obvious to select any one of the multikinase inhibitors listed in King, including nintedanib, and apply them to the animal model disclosed in King or the human subjects as taught in Amparo. Pet. 65 (King “teaches that BIBF-1120 is a suitable inhibitor....BIBF-1120 is another name for nintedanib....It would have been obvious to a [person of ordinary skill in the art] to use nintedanib, as taught by King, as the multikinase inhibitor in a topical ocular formulations as taught by King and Amparo.” (citing Ex. 1008 ¶ 16; Ex. 1011, 1; Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 159–163)). Petitioner emphasizes that its position for the selection is not based on the inhibitors being suitable equivalents but instead relies on King’s express disclosure that any one of the inhibitors can be applied. Pet. 65 (“It would have been obvious to a POSITA to use nintedanib, as taught by King, as the multikinase inhibitor in a topical ocular formulations as taught by King and Amparo.”); Tr. 7:7–9 (“We’re relying upon the expressed teaching in King that nintedanib was a suitable inhibitor for the treatment of pterygium.”).

Patent Owner argues that King is directed to oral administration and treating diseases associated with the posterior of the eye, “specifically, retinal and choroidal ocular tissue.” PO Resp. 24 (citing Ex. 2005, ¶ 56; Ex. 2006, 1; Ex. 2007, 3). Patent Owner argues that King’s experiments are directed as neovascularization after rupture of Bruch’s membrane, $^{19}$ and “a useful model for neovascular age-related macular degeneration.” Id. at 26. King’s experimental model is not, however, a model for pterygium. Id. According to Patent Owner, King studied systemic concertation of pazopanib after topical administration. King’s “topical administration of pazopanib to one of the eyes resulted in detectable plasma concentrations of pazopanib, as well as detectable levels of pazopanib in the sclera, choroid, and retina of both the treated and untreated (fellow) eye.” Id. at 28. According to Patent Owner, “King teaches nothing regarding whether pazopanib, applied topically, could be delivered to the conjunctiva or cornea.” Id. at 29.

Patent Owner fails to address King's experimental treatment using a well-known animal model. Ex. 1005 ¶ 152. King may have been focused on showing the feasibility for oral administration of multikinase inhibitors for the purpose of treating angiogenesis in the eye, but the reference is art for all that it discloses and suggests to one of ordinary skill in the art. In re Lamberti, 545 F.2d 747, 750 (CCPA 1976); see also In re Susi, 440 F.2d 442, 446 n.3 (CCPA 1971) (disclosed examples and preferred embodiments do not constitute a teaching away from a broader disclosure or non-preferred embodiments.).

King's experimental treatment includes administering pazopanib solution (approximately $30~\mu \mathrm{l}$ of a $5\mathrm{mg / ml}$ sterile-filtered solution) twice daily to the eye of the animals. See Ex. 1008 ¶¶ 48–50, 58–62. Based on this disclosure, we are not persuaded that King is limited to oral administration. In one test, King topically instilled the pazopanib solution onto the eye of an animal, measured the plasma levels as well as levels in the treated eye cup tissues (sclera, choroid, and retina), and noted that that pazopanib was also detected in the eye that did not receive eye drops (the fellow (OD) eye). Ex. 1008 ¶ 62. We find no evidence in the record from which to conclude that topically applying the pazopanib solution to the eye, as disclosed in King, would not make the solution available to the tissues that are associated with the front (i.e., anterior) of the eye, which includes the cornea as well as the ocular conjunctiva. See e.g. Ex. 2005 ¶ 32 (providing a figure detailing the structural components of the eye); Reply 34 (“On cross-examination, Dr. Hohman conceded that King’s experimental results showed that drug also was delivered ‘to the conjunctiva…separate from the systemic delivery.’ Ex. 1032, 78:5-19 (admitting that in King this “topical ocular formulation diffuses . . . to the conjunctiva and eventually into the retina. So there’s a diffusion component, which is separate from the systemic delivery.”).”). Accordingly, we are not persuaded by Patent Owner’s contention that King is limited to systemic absorption.

Furthermore, Petitioner does not rely on the oral administration route taught in King to establish a reason to combine the references. Instead Petitioner is relying on the experimental animal model data taught in King as a reason to combine King with Amparo. See Pet. 63 (citing Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 152–152). Amparo acknowledged that “pazopanib [formulated as an eye drop] has been shown to inhibit laser-induced choroidal NV” (neovascularization), the experimental model studied in King. Ex. 1009, 1.

To specifically study the effects of topical pazopanib on the corneal tissue, i.e., tissue associated with the front of the eye, Amparo plugged the superior and inferior puncta of the study eye to avoid any systemic absorption. Ex. 1009, 8. Amparo concluded that “[t]reatment with topical pazopanib led to a 22% decrease of corneal neovessels (NA) and NA is a function of vessel caliber and length.” Id. There is no reason to conclude that the other inhibitors listed in King would not function similarly at inhibiting neovascularization.

Accordingly, we conclude that Petitioner has demonstrated that the that based on the combination of King and Amparo there is reasonable expectation of success that nintedanib would result a reduction in neovessels when topically applied.

## (iv) Unexpected results

We next determine if there are any secondary considerations that when considered against the totality of the evidence that leads us to conclude that the claims are patentable. Sur-reply 5.

Petitioner directs our attention to the “unexpected nature” of nintedanib in the context of their enablement arguments to establish that multikinase inhibitors are not equivalent. Pet. 31–32. Petitioner contends that Patent Owner “admitted that sunitinib[, pazopanib,] and nintedanib are not equivalent or substitutable during prosecution of a continuation of the ’820 Patent (Appl. No. 16/210,896),” because “nintedanib showed unexpected results as compared with sunitinib—the only compound tested at the time the ’063 provisional was filed.” Reply 19 (citing Ex. 1023, 9–11) (emphasis removed). Petitioner having directed our attention to the unexpected properties of nintedanib at least in the context of their

enablement discussion, we now cannot ignore this evidence in the context of an obviousness determination.

The panel can draw its own inferences from the arguments and evidence before it. See Rovalma, S.A. v. Bohler-Edelstahl GmbH & Co. KG, 856 F.3d 1019, 1027 (Fed. Cir. 2017) (noting that the Board is not precluded “from relying on arguments made by a party and doing its job, as adjudicator, of drawing its own inferences and conclusions from those arguments”). “After a prima facie case of obviousness has been made and rebuttal evidence submitted, all the evidence must be considered anew.” In re Eli Lilly & Co., 902 F.2d 943, 945 (Fed. Cir. 1990) (citing In re Piasecki, 745 F.2d 1468, 1472 (Fed. Cir. 1984)).

According to the '820 patent:

Nintedanib and sunitinib both inhibit the key VEGFR and PDGFR families with a few non-overlapping targets (see Table 1). But the unique set of kinase targets of nintedanib seems to make it more effective and safe than sunitinib, indicating that nintedanib may be one of the most powerful multikinase inhibitors for reducing corneal neovascularization. The unique profile for the inhibition of the following intracellular and/or cell surface protein kinases at the maximum in vitro IC50 are specified as VEGFR (1, 2, 3) (IC50<50 nM), PDGFR (α,β) (IC50<100 nM), FGFR (1, 2, 3) (IC50<150 nM), FGFR4 (IC50<1000 nM), FLT3 (IC50<50 nM), Lek (IC50<50 nM), Lyn (IC50<200 nM), and Src (IC50<200 nM). Three targets of FGFR4, Lyn and Src, are not inhibited by sunitinib or other common kinases inhibitors and these may differentiate nintedanib from sunitinib and others. In addition, the nintedanib's potencies on FGFR1-3 are substantially higher than that of sunitinib that may also contribute to the superior efficacy of nintedanib in the cornea suture rabbit model.

Ex. 1001, 12:1–20 (emphasis added).

Quite unexpectedly, the inventors found that kinase inhibitors with substantial kinase target overlaps can still have different efficacies. Although nintedanib and sunitinib both inhibit the key VEGFR family, they have several non-overlapping targets. As shown in Table 1, the target profile of nintedanib is different from that of sunitinib as well as several other MKI's considered very similar in the research community. The special set of kinase targets of nintedanib seems offer at least two surprising advantages: 1) it makes nintedanib a highly effective inhibitor of neovascularization, more so than sunitinib, as demonstrated in the rabbit suture model; 2) it targets fewer kinases than sunitinib, allowing for better safety margins and higher doses. Accordingly, it is understood that nintedanib has demonstrated an improved efficacy and a better safety profile as compared with sunitinib.

Ex. 1001, 18:2–18 (emphasis added).

We understand that the '063 provisional application does not provide any data with respect to nintedanib in the corneal suture model, nor does the '063 provisional application recognize that nintedanib shows surprising efficacy. Compare Ex. 1003 with Ex. 1001, 11: 26–27 ("Interestingly, nintedanib showed a clear trend of superiority to sunitinib in reducing neovascular area."). Not every property of a compound, however, needs to be fully recognized as of the filing date of the patent application. Genetics Institute, LLC v. Novartis Vaccines and Diagnostics, Inc., 655 F.3d 1291, 1307 (2011) (citing Knoll Pharm. Co. v. Teva Pharms. USA, Inc., 367 F.3d 1381, 1385 (Fed. Cir. 2004) ("There is no requirement that an invention's properties and advantages were fully known before the patent application was filed, or that the patent application contains all of the work done in studying the invention, in order for that work to be introduced into evidence in response to litigation attack.")). Indeed, our reviewing court has held "that evidence of unexpected results may be used to rebut a case of prima facie obviousness even if that evidence was obtained after the patent's filing or issue date." Id.

As discussed above, one of skill in the art would expect multikinase inhibitors to work similarly. See above F.1.c.iii. The '820 patent, however, provides evidence of unexpected results by comparing nintedanib to the closest prior art sunitinib. $^{20}$ Specifically, the '820 patent discloses that nintedanib provides “improved efficacy and better safety profile as compared to sunitinib.” Ex. 1001, 18:17–18. The '820 patent shows that in a rabbit cornea suture model the neovascular area is substantially reduced in the eyes treated with nintedanib as compared to sunitinib or the vehicle control. Ex. 1001, 11:17–24. Table 2 showing these results is reproduced below.

Given these surprising and unexpected results that nintedanib has improved efficacy and a better safety profile compared to sunitinib as reported in the '820 patent specification convinces us that on balance the weight of the evidence supports a finding that claims 4 and 5 have not been shown to be unpatentable.

After considering all the evidence anew before us, we conclude that Petitioner has not provided sufficient evidence to demonstrate that claims 4 and 5 are unpatentable.

## 2. Conclusion

Upon weighing all the evidence, including the competing testimony, the reasoning to combine the references, and the evidence of secondary considerations in the form of surprising unexpected results, we determine that Petitioner has not shown that claims 4 and 5 of the '820 patent are unpatentable.

## III. CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, we conclude that Petitioner has not established by a preponderance of the evidence that claims 4 and 5 of the '820 patent are unpatentable.
