## D. The '734 Patent (Ex. 1001)

The '734 patent issued on Jan. 28, 2020 from Application No. 16/248,569 (“the ’569 application”), filed Jan. 15, 2019. The '734 patent is titled “Methods for Increasing Genetic Progress in a Line or Breed of Swine Using Sex-selected Sperm Cells.” Ex. 1001, code (54). The '734 patent discloses “methods of increasing the genetic progress of a line, breed or herd of swine through the use of sex-selected sperm cells in artificial insemination techniques.” Id. at 1:17–20. As background, the '734 patent indicates that there is “a need in the swine industry to increase the rate of desirable genetic change in lines and breeds as well as to lower operational costs on breeding and commercial swine farms.” Id. at 1:27–30. The '734 patent purports to achieve these goals by allowing an operator of a breeding or commercial farm to “select the sex of offspring swine using sex-selected sperm cell samples and/or by allowing the use of far fewer genetically superior boars through the use of reduced sperm cell doses for artificial insemination procedures.” Id. at 1:30–35.

A “line” or “breed” is “a group of animals having a common origin and similar identifying characteristics.” Id. at 3:3–5. The ’734 patent describes that “any desirable genotypic or phenotypic trait can be used to construct a selection index for a line or breed of swine” and that such

phenotypic traits include “feed efficiency, $^{[4]}$ average daily gain, carcass lean, carcass quality, fertility, litter size and milk production.” Id. at 3:18–23. The rate of desirable genetic change of a herd or line can be increased by selecting the sex of the offspring in a line. Id. at 7:28–35. A genetic change, or a “genetic progress” means that for a given desired trait or a given undesirable trait, “the individuals in the successive generation will express the desired trait more strongly than the previous generation” or “the individuals in the successive generation will express the [undesirable] trait less strongly than the previous generation.” Id. at 7:30–36.

The '734 patent discloses that swine production “can be represented by a multilevel pyramid, with certain offspring at each level used in the next lower level for breeding.” Id. at 9:41–43. The top level of the pyramid is the “nucleus herd” and the next levels from top to bottom are the “daughter nucleus herd,” the “multiplier herd” and finally the “commercial farm,” respectively. Id. at 9:43–46. In such a pyramid, the nucleus herd “is typically comprised of 12 to 15 lines, although any number of lines may be represented, with each line comprising a number of desirable traits.” Id. at 9:47–49. The daughter nucleus level is a “line multiplication” level in which “sows from a line are inseminated with boar semen derived from the nucleus herd.” Id. at 10:17–19. In the next lower level of the multiplier herd level, “the resulting sows are typically crossed with boars of a different line.” Id. at 10:19–21. Here, a multiplier herd “is typically either a gilt multiplier or boar multiplier.” Id. at 10:21–22. A gilt is a young female pig that has not yet fallowed. Id. at 6:20–21. Gilt multipliers are then used to “produce parent gilts” and boar multipliers are then used to “produce parent boars.” Id. at 10:22–23. “Parent gilts are sent to commercial farms to replace old or dead gilts and sows, and parent boars are sent to boar studs to produce the sperm cells for use in artificial insemination.” Id. at 10:23–26.

According to the '734 patent, its method of increasing the profitability of a swine herd or farm includes “collecting a semen sample from a boar” and “sorting said semen sample into at least two subpopulations of sperm cells, wherein at least 80% of a first subpopulation bears (i) X-chromosomes if the female pig results in a higher net income per pig, or

(ii) Y-chromosomes if the male pig results in a higher net income per pig." Id. at 6:6–17. A sow is then inseminated with sperm cells from the first subpopulation to produce offspring. Id. at 6:17–19. Further, an embryo or a zygote is selected "based on the presence or absence of a genetic marker" and the "genetic marker can be a marker for, or associated with, for example, any of the swine traits disclosed herein." Id. at 2:30–38.

## B. Principles of Law

To establish anticipation under 35 U.S.C. § 102, each and every element in a claim, arranged as recited in the claim, must be found in a single prior art reference. See Net MoneyIN, Inc. v. VeriSign, Inc., 545 F.3d 1359, 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2008); Karsten Mfg. Corp. v. Cleveland Golf Co., 242 F.3d 1376, 1383 (Fed. Cir. 2001). Although the elements must be arranged or combined in the same way as in the claim, “the reference need not satisfy an ipsissimis verbis test,” i.e., identity of terminology is not required. In re Gleave, 560 F.3d 1331, 1334 (Fed. Cir. 2009) (accord In re Bond, 910 F.2d 831, 832–33 (Fed. Cir. 1990)).

A claim is unpatentable under 35 U.S.C. § 103(a) if the differences between the claimed subject matter and the prior art are such that the subject matter, as a whole, would have been obvious at the time of the invention to a person having ordinary skill in the art. KSR Int'l Co. v. Teleflex, Inc., 550 U.S. 398, 406 (2007). The question of obviousness is resolved on the basis of underlying factual determinations, including “the scope and content of the prior art”; “differences between the prior art and the claims at issue”; and “the level of ordinary skill in the pertinent art.” Graham v. John Deere Co., 383 U.S. 1, 17–18 (1966). Graham also identifies secondary considerations of nonobviousness as part of the inquiry but no such evidence has been presented during this proceeding.

A patent claim “is not proved obvious merely by demonstrating that each of its elements was, independently, known in the prior art.” KSR, 550 U.S. at 418. An obviousness determination requires finding “both ‘that a skilled artisan would have been motivated to combine the teachings of the prior art references to achieve the claimed invention, and that the skilled artisan would have had a reasonable expectation of success in doing so.” Intelligent Bio-Sys., Inc. v. Illumina Cambridge Ltd., 821 F.3d 1359, 1367–68 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (citation omitted); see KSR, 550 U.S. at 418 (for an obviousness analysis, “it can be important to identify a reason that would have prompted a person of ordinary skill in the relevant field to combine the elements in the way the claimed new invention does”). Further, an assertion of obviousness “cannot be sustained by mere conclusory statements; instead, there must be some articulated reasoning with some rational underpinning to support the legal conclusion of obviousness.” KSR, 550 U.S. at 418 (quoting In re Kahn, 441 F.3d 977, 988 (Fed. Cir. 2006)); In re NuVasive, Inc., 842 F.3d 1376, 1383 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (a finding of a motivation to combine “must be supported by a ‘reasoned explanation’” (citation omitted)).

## C. Claim Construction

In an inter partes review for a petition filed on or after November 13, 2018, “claim[s] of a patent . . . shall be construed using the same claim construction standard that would be used to construe the claim[s] in a civil action under 35 U.S.C. [§] 282(b), including construing the claim[s] in accordance with the ordinary and customary meaning of such claim[s] as understood by one of ordinary skill in the art and the prosecution history pertaining to the patent.” 37 C.F.R. § 42.100(b); see Changes to the Claim Construction Standard for Interpreting Claims in Trial Proceedings Before the Patent Trial and Appeal Board, 83 Fed. Reg. 51,340 (Oct. 11, 2018) (codified at 37 C.F.R. § 42.100(b) (2019)) (amending 37 C.F.R. § 42.100(b) effective November 13, 2018). A term’s ordinary and customary meaning “is its meaning to the ordinary artisan after reading the entire patent.”

Phillips v. AWH Corp., 415 F.3d 1303, 1321 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (en banc). Nevertheless, “it is always necessary to review the specification to determine whether the inventor has used any terms in a manner inconsistent with their ordinary meaning,” because “[t]he specification acts as a dictionary when it expressly defines terms used in the claims or when it defines terms by implication.” Vitronics Corp. v. Conceptronic, Inc., 90 F.3d 1576, 1582 (Fed. Cir. 1996).

Both Petitioner and Patent Owner agree that the preamble is limiting. See Pet 27 (“The preamble to claim 1 of the ’734 patent is limiting in its recitation “increasing the genetic progress of a line or breed of swine within a genetic nucleus.”); PO Resp. 20.

Petitioner requests construction of the terms “genetic progress,” “line or breed,” “genetic nucleus,” “feed efficiency,” “average daily gain,” and “carcass lean.” Pet. 9–12. The terms “genetic progress,” “line or breed,” and “genetic nucleus” are found in the independent claim 1. The term “line or breed” is also found in some of the dependent claims. While the terms “feed efficiency,” “average daily gain,” and “carcass lean” are terms found in dependent claim 5.

Patent Owner only provides a separate construction of “genetic nucleus” PO Resp. 20–24.

1. genetic nucleus

According to Petitioner, “[t]he ’734 patent discloses that ‘[s]wine production can be represented by a multilevel pyramid, with certain offspring at each level used in the next lower level for breeding,’ that the ‘top level of the pyramid is the nucleus herd,’ and that ‘any number of lines may be represented’ in a nucleus herd.” Pet. 10 (citing Ex. 1001, 9:41–49)

(second alteration in original). Petitioner contends that this provides an incomplete description of genetic nucleus because “commercial breeders must also use offspring of the genetic nucleus for breeding within the genetic nucleus itself.” Id. (citing Ex. 1004, 410 (“Most genetic improvement in pigs is based on selection within pure breeds or lines.”), 417 (Fig. 16.4); Ex. 1002 ¶ 32). Petitioner proposes that “genetic nucleus” means “an animal herd comprising one or more lines and positioned at the top of a multi-level pyramidal hierarchy of herds in a commercial herd breeding structure, wherein certain offspring of the herd are used for breeding within the herd and certain offspring are used for breeding in the next lower level of the pyramid.” Id. at 10 (citing Ex. 1002 ¶ 32).

Patent Owner agrees with the first portion of Petitioner's interpretation, but disagrees on the remainder. Patent Owner argues that one of ordinary skill in the art would not find the additional language “wherein certain offspring of the herd are used for breeding within the herd” in Petitioner’s interpretation accurate in describing a genetic nucleus. PO Resp. 21 (citing Ex. 2001 ¶ 21). Patent Owner contends “that the plain and ordinary meaning of ‘genetic nucleus’ is ‘an animal herd comprising one or more lines and positioned at the top of a multi-level pyramidal hierarchy of herds in a commercial herd breeding structure.’” Id. (citing Ex. 2005 ¶¶ 31–33 (“The ’734 Patent does not specify this additional requirement as part of the meaning of ‘genetic nucleus,’ nor would the additional language be accurate in describing every genetic nucleus.”)).

Nucleus herd and genetic nucleus are understood to be synonymous terms. See Tr. 8:22–9:10; Ex. 1002 ¶ 40 (“At the peak of the pyramid is the nucleus herd, also known as the genetic nucleus. This is the herd in which genetic improvements are implemented, such as by the isolation or introduction of desirable traits.”). The ’734 specification describes pig breeding as a pyramid with the “nucleus herd” at the top of the pyramid and commercial farms at the bottom. Ex. 1001, 9:43–44. According to the ’734 specification, “[a] nucleus herd is typically comprised of 12 to 15 lines, although any number of lines may be represented, with each line comprising a number of desirable traits. . . . Only the offspring with the highest measured selection index are retained in the nucleus herd.” Id. at 9:47–49 (emphasis added). Here, the retention of offspring with the most desirable features in the nucleus herd reasonably implies that these individuals are selected to also propagate the desirable trait within the nucleus herd.

Having considered the record as a whole, we determine that Petitioner's position with respect to the interpretation of “genetic nucleus” is reasonably supported within the record. At oral argument, however, Petitioner acknowledged that omitting the language “wherein certain offspring of the herd are used for breeding within the herd” from the claim construction would not change whether the challenged claims read on the applied references. See Tr. 14:6–16:28. Because the additional language proposed by Petitioner is unnecessary in order to resolve the disputed issues in this case, we do not adopt it here. We, therefore, construe “genetic nucleus” as an animal herd comprising one or more lines and positioned at the top of a multi-level pyramidal hierarchy of herds in a commercial herd breeding structure.

## D. Ground 1A: Anticipation of Claims 1–5 by Dekkers, as evidenced by either Maxwell or Garner (claims 1–4), or Kim (claim 5)

Petitioner contends that claims 1–5 are unpatentable as anticipated by Dekkers, as evidenced by either Maxwell or Garner (claims 1–4), or Kim (claim 5). Pet. 26–41; Pet. Reply 8–16. Patent Owner opposes. PO Resp. 24–39; Sur-reply 10–16.

## 1. Overview of Dekkers (Ex. 1004)

Dekkers teaches “[g]enetic improvement in pigs.” Ex. 1004, 390. According to Dekkers, the general principles of genetic improvement includes an objective of a breeding program that produces “most improvement per unit of time.” Id. at 391. A “goal is then to maximize genetic change over generations or over time.” Id. at 392. Dekkers indicates that in most countries, pig breeding programs “operate in a three-tier pyramidal structure.” Id. at 393. Dekkers discloses that at the peak of the pyramid are the “nucleus breeding farms that actually generate the genetic changes.” Id. (emphasis omitted). Below that tier are “multiplier farms that carry out specific matings or crossbreeding for the production of large numbers of females.” Id. (emphasis omitted). These females are then sold, under the lower tier, to “commercial producers for piglet production and finishing to produce market pigs” for slaughterhouses. Id. (emphasis omitted). Variations of the tiers of the pyramid are possible. Id.

Dekkers further discloses that one of the most used reproduction technologies “is AI,” or artificial insemination. Id. at 395–96. Dekkers also discloses sperm sexing technology that allows “the production of either male or female offspring for sire or dam lines,” which can be very useful for the nucleus and multiplication tiers. Id. at 396. “The technique involves the separation of spermatozoa carrying either X or Y chromosomes (Maxwell et al., 2004; Garner, 2006)” in which the “sorted sperm can then be used for AI or embryo transfer.” Id. Dekkers also discloses that molecular genetics have revealed new classes of DNA polymorphisms, such as SNPs (single nuclear polymorphisms), that allow for direct selection of “an individual’s genotype for genes or genetic markers that are associated” with a trait. Id. at 407. Certain types of genetic markers are applied “for within-breed genetic improvement.” Id. Dekkers discloses that genetic selection (GS) is an enhanced version of marker assisted selection (MAS) “that involves the selection of animals for breeding on the basis of their genotype for tens of thousands of ‘random’ SNPs that cover the genome.” Id. at 414.

Petitioner asserts that Dekkers “discloses methods of increasing genetic progress in swine. . . . Dekkers explains that in pig breeding programs, selection response depends on, inter alia, selection accuracy, selection intensity or standardized selection differential, and generational interval.” Pet. 27–28 (citing Ex. 1004, 391; Ex. 1002 ¶¶ 30, 55, 56, 100–103). Petitioner contends that Dekkers states that “sperm sexing technology allow[s] for the production of either male or female offspring for sire or dam lines, and this can be very useful for the nucleus and multiplication tiers. The technique involves the separation of spermatozoa carrying either X or Y chromosomes (Maxwell et al., 2004; Garner, 2006).” Pet. 33–34 (citing Ex. 1004, 396). Petitioner’s expert testifies that the sorting methods

referenced in Dekkers, specifically, Maxwell and Garner both evidence sorting at 85-95 % accuracy, which falls within the range cited in claim 1. See Ex. 1002 ¶ 113; Pet. 34.

Patent Owner opposes. See PO Resp. 24–39; Sur-reply 10–16. ## Specifically, Patent Owner contends that

Petitioner does not argue that the Dekkers reference itself expressly discloses the required 80% sex skew, and Petitioner's expert admits the required disclosure is missing. EX 2005, ¶¶ 58–63; EX 2004, 54:17-55:10. Nor does Dekkers incorporate by reference the disclosures of Maxwell or Garner. To incorporate matter by reference, “a host document must contain language clearly identifying the subject matter which is incorporated and where it is to be found; a mere reference to another application, or patent, or publication is not an incorporation of anything therein.” Callaway Golf Co. v. Acushnet Co., 576 F.3d 1331, 1346 (Fed. Cir. 2009) (internal quote and citations omitted, emphasis in original).

PO Resp. 29. Patent Owner contends that Dekkers's paragraph referencing sex sorting technology makes only a general disclosure of sex sorting technology “and is not an express disclosure of sorting at an 80% skew rate (or any other rate).” Id. at 26 (citing Ex. 1004, 396; Ex. 2005 ¶¶ 58–63); Ex. 2004, 54:17–55:10 (Petitioner’s expert acknowledging that “Dekkers doesn’t write 80 percent in this paragraph.”).

In response, Petitioner contends that “Maxwell and Garner—each cited in Dekkers—evidence knowledge attributable to a [person of ordinary skill in the art] regarding porcine sex-skewed semen.” Pet. Reply 14 (citing Ex. 1097 ¶ 45). Dr. Baes testifies that “from a commercial pig production perspective, [a person of ordinary skill in the art would understand that] using semen with less than 80% sex skew would not be efficient or economical.” Pet. Reply 15 (citing Ex. 1003 ¶ 113; Ex. 1097 ¶ 46; Ex. 1095, 149 (“Sorting conditions must be optimized for each species to achieve an acceptable (90 percent or better) sperm sexing accuracy”).

We agree with Patent Owner that in an anticipation challenge under 35 U.S.C. § 102, Petitioner cannot rely upon specific disclosures in Maxwell or Gardner based on the generic reference to these publications cited Dekkers. Merely citing Maxwell or Gardner in association with sorting sperm as discussed in Dekkers is not incorporation by reference with any level of particularity. See PO Resp. 29 (citing Callaway Golf Co. v. Acushnet Co., 576 F.3d 1331, 1346 (Fed. Cir. 2009) (To incorporate matter by reference, “a host document must contain language clearly identifying the subject matter which is incorporated and where it is to be found; a mere reference to another application, or patent, or publication is not an incorporation of anything therein.” (internal quote and citations omitted, emphasis in original)); see also Cook Biotech Inc. v. Acell, Inc., 460 F.3d 1365, 1376 (Fed. Cir. 2006) (“To incorporate material by reference, the host document must identify with detailed particularity what specific material it incorporates and clearly indicate where that material is found in the various documents.”).

The disclosure in Dekkers relied on by Petitioner recites: “The technique involves the separation of spermatozoa carrying either X or Y chromosomes (Maxwell et al., 2004; Garner, 2006).” Ex. 1004, 396. We note that Maxwell and Garner are review articles discussing the state of sperm sexting technology generally. See Ex. 1005; Ex. 1006. We agree with Patent Owner that the generic citation to these review articles as set out in Dekkers is not an incorporation by reference of any specific detail from these articles. Unlike the disclosures at issue in Callaway and Cook, Dekkers does not use language such as “reference is made to” (Callaway, 576 F.3d at 1346) or “the disclosure of which is expressly incorporated herein by reference” (Cook, 460 F.3d at 1376) in conjunction with identifying a particular portion of the cited reference that our reviewing court has held to be sufficient to indicate to one of skill in the art that the referenced material is fully incorporated in the host document. Because Dekkers does not incorporate by reference the level of sorting accuracy as required by the claims – at least 80% – the preponderance of evidence of record does not support Petitioner’s position the Dekkers anticipates claims 1–5 of the ’734 patent.

Petitioner contends that Maxwell and Garner evidence that a person of ordinary skill in the art “would have been aware of challenges associated with sex-sorted semen and that using semen with less than 80% sex skew would not be efficient or economical.” Pet. Reply 22 (citing Ex. 1002 ¶ 113). We find that this reasoning is more applicable to an obviousness-based challenge and not the present anticipation challenge.

## E. Ground 1B: Obviousness of Claims 1–5 over Dekkers and either Maxwell, Garner, or Kim

Petitioner contends that claims 1–5 are unpatentable as obvious over the combination of Dekkers with either Maxwell or Garner (claims 1–4), and Kim (claim 5). Pet. 41–45. Patent Owner opposes. PO. Resp. 35–43.

## 1. Overview of Maxwell (Ex. 1005)

Maxwell teaches sex-sorting of mammalian spermatozoa “for genetic improvement of farm animals.” Ex. 1005, 79. Maxwell discloses that sex pre-selection before insemination to produce a specific sex in livestock offspring results in “faster genetic progress” for “the production of male and female crossbred lines in the pig industry.” Id. at 80. In sperm sexing, the “greater the difference in DNA content between X- and Y-chromosome bearing (X and Y) spermatozoa, the more efficiently the two populations can be resolved and separated by flow cytometric sorting.” Id. at 80–81. According to Maxwell, generally, “X and Y purities of more than 85% can be achieved in most species, depending on the rate of sorting.” Id. at 81.

## 2 Overview of Garner (Ex. 1006)

Garner teaches parameters that are “needed for optimization of flow cytometric sexing mammalian sperm” and updates for “the current status of sperm sexing for various species.” Ex. 1006, 943. Garner discloses that the precise measurement of “the DNA content difference between X- and Y-chromosome bearing sperm of mammals has provided an effective means of separating viable gametes carrying either the X- or Y- chromosome with an accuracy of 85–95%.” Id. at 944. According to Garner, “swine was the first large animal species where the feasibility of using” the technology of predetermination of sex using flow cytometry/cell sorting, was demonstrated. Id. at 952. Consequently, the “adoption of sperm sexing for production of founder animals with superior genetic qualities” can be used to increase the availability of animals with superior genetic traits for swine producers. Id.

## 3. Overview of Kim (Ex. 1007)

Kim teaches a missense variant of the porcine melanocortin-4 receptor (MC4R) gene that is associated with fatness, growth, and feed intake traits. Ex. 1007, 131. Identification of this missense mutation in the pig MC4R gene was confirmed by sequence of the PCR product. Id. at 133.

[G]iven the strong relationship of MC4R variants to leanness, growth, and feed intake, this mutation could be used immediately for marker-assisted selection . . . to develop lines of pigs to satisfy particular customer requirements. For instance, in sow lines where appetite is normally decreased after farrowing, selection for the MC4R allele 2 could help to improve feed intake. Furthermore, in some lines deemed to be too fat, selection for allele 1 could be employed, and in lines that were considered to be growing too slowly, allele 2 selection could be employed. Therefore, genotyping for the MC4R mutation in pig breeding lines will improve the selection efficiency of feed-related production traits including growth and leanness.

Id. at 134.

## Claims 2 and 3

Petitioner asserts that Dekkers discloses a line or breed is a gilt line (claim 2) or a boar line (claim 3). Pet. 38–39.

Patent Owner does not address the limitations of claims 2 and 3 separately, relying instead on the contention that limitations of claim 1 are missing from the combination of Dekkers with either Maxwell, Garner, or Kim. See generally PO. Resp. 24–43.

We determine that Petitioner has shown sufficiently that Dekkers teaches the limitation of claims 2 and 3. In particular, Dekkers discloses that breeding at the nucleus level is divided into sire and dam lines.

It is important to note that the breeding objective is actually implemented at the nucleus level, but it is targeted to the production level and to future markets.

In commercial breeding, breeding goals are defined separately for sire and dam lines. The sire lines are mainly selected for genetic improvement in production traits such as growth rate, carcass quality, meat quality, feed conversion and conformation. The breeding goal for dam lines typically includes the same production traits, but in addition female reproductive traits, such as litter size, piglet survival, age at puberty, conception rate, number weaned and weaning to oestrus interval.

Ex. 1004, 397. Dekkers discloses that “new developments in sperm sexing technology allow for the production of either male or female offspring for sire or dam lines, and this can be very useful for the nucleus and multiplication tiers.” Ex. 1004, 396.

Based on the foregoing discussion and our consideration of the record as a whole, we determine that Petitioner has shown persuasively that the

combination of Dekkers in conjunction with either Maxwell or Garner teach each of the elements of claims 2 and 3. Accordingly, we determine that Petitioner has shown by a preponderance of the evidence that claims 2 and 3 would have been obvious based on the combination of Dekkers with either Maxwell or Garner.

## Claims 4 and 5

Petitioner asserts that Dekkers discloses genetic markers including single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) (claim 4), that are correlated with feed efficiency, average daily gain, or carcass lean (claim 5). Pet. 39–40.

Patent Owner does not address the limitations of claims 4 and 5 separately, relying instead on the contention that limitations of claim 1 are missing from the combination of Dekkers with either Maxwell, Garner, or Kim. See generally PO. Resp. 24–43.

On the complete record, we determine that Petitioner has sufficiently shown that Dekkers teaches the limitations of claims 4 and 5. In particular, Dekkers discloses the use of marker-assisted selection (MAS). Ex. 1004, 407. Dekkers explains that “[o]nce markers associated with the trait have been identified and validated, they can be used for genetic improvement.” Id. at 408. “Most genetic improvement in pigs is based on selection within pure breeds or lines. Within-breed selection can be improved with availability of genotype on markers that are associated with the trait(s) of interest.” Id. at 410. Dekkers Table 16.6, shown above in § II.E.5.f, discloses a list of known genetic markers, identifying the gene MC4R (melanocortin-4 receptor) that has been associated with lean growth, fatness, and feed intake traits, and gene HMGA 1 (high mobility group AT hook 1) that has been associated with the backfat thickness trait. Id. at 413 (Table 16.6). Dekkers teaches that single nuclear polymorphism (SNP) panels have become available, which allow for the detection of genetic markers that are associated with the trait based on population-wide linkage disequilibrium markers (LD) and with qualitative trait loci (QTLs). Id. at 408.

Petitioner argues that Dekkers expressly describes using SNPs as genetic markers for selection purposes. Pet. 45. Because Dekkers “discloses these [genetic] markers [associated with traits such as lean growth, fatness, feed intake, and backfat thickness] are for use in swine breeding to improve the listed traits, it would have been obvious to select SNPs in Table 16.6 [reproduced above in § II.E.5.f] relating to these traits as genetic markers, including MC4R” for selection purposes. Pet. 45. Kim cited by Dekkers teaches that MCR4 is a SNP and is useful as a genetic marker. In particular, Kim teaches that

given the strong relationship of MC4R variants to leanness, growth, and feed intake, this mutation could be used immediately for marker-assisted selection.... For instance, in sow lines where appetite is normally decreased after farrowing, selection for the MC4R allele 2 could help to improve feed intake.... Therefore, genotyping for the MC4R mutation in pig breeding lines will improve the selection efficiency of feed-related production traits including growth and leanness.

Ex. 1007, 134.

Based on the foregoing discussion and our consideration of record as a whole, we determine that Petitioner has shown persuasively that the combination of Dekkers in conjunction with either Maxwell or Garner in combination with Kim teach each of the elements of claims 4 and 5. Accordingly, we determine that Petitioner has shown by a preponderance of the evidence that claims 4 and 5 would have been obvious based on the combination of Dekkers with either Maxwell or Garner, and Kim.

## Summary

To summarize, based on the evidence and arguments presented by the parties based on the record as a whole, we determine that Petitioner has sufficiently shown by a preponderance of the evidence that claims 1–5 of the '734 patent would have been obvious over the combination of Dekkers, Maxwell, Garner, and Kim.

## G. Remaining Grounds of Obviousness

In the remaining grounds of obviousness, Petitioner asserts that: (1) claims 1–5 are unpatentable as obvious over Dekkers and Okere, Pet. 46–48, Pet. Reply 24–26; and (2) claims 1–5 are unpatentable as obvious over Buttram, Maxwell, and Kim, Pet. 48–59, Pet. Reply 26–30. Because Petitioner challenges the same claims in these grounds that we conclude are unpatentable based upon the obviousness challenge involving Dekkers in conjunction with Maxwell, Garner, and/or Kim as discussed above, we decline to reach these remaining grounds. See SAS Inst. Inc. v. Iancu, 138 S. Ct. 1348, 1359 (2018) (holding a petitioner “is entitled to a final written decision addressing all of the claims it has challenged”); Boston Sci. Scimed, Inc. v. Cook Grp. Inc., Nos. 2019-1594, -1604, -1605, 2020 WL 2071962, at \*4 (Fed. Cir. Apr. 30, 2020) (non-precedential) (recognizing that the “Board need not address issues that are not necessary to the resolution of the proceeding” and, thus, agreeing that the Board has “discretion to decline to decide additional instituted grounds once the petitioner has prevailed on all its challenged claims”).

## III. CONCLUSION $^{6}$

For the foregoing reasons, we conclude that Petitioner has shown by a preponderance of the evidence that claims 1–5 of the '734 patent are unpatentable as obvious over the combination of Dekkers, Maxwell, Garner, and Kim.
