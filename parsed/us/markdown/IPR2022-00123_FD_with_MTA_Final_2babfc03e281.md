## B. The '190 Patent

The '190 patent relates to a method for eliminating or reducing ototoxicity in pediatric patients receiving a platinum-based chemotherapeutic. Ex. 1001, Abstract; 1:22–25.

Platinum-based therapeutics are important in treating a variety of pediatric malignancies. Id. at 1:29–31. Unfortunately, at commonly used doses and schedules, platinum-based therapeutics, such as cisplatin and carboplatin, often cause hearing loss. Id. at 1:33–39. According to the '190 patent, there was a need for "safe and effective methods for treating pediatric patients to reduce ototoxicity and hearing loss in these patients that do not compromise the efficacy of the platinum-based therapeutic." Id. at 1:49–52.

According to the '190 patent, "STS [(sodium thiosulfate)] is a water-soluble thiol compound with reducing agent properties and is commercially available as an established antidote for acute cyanide poisoning." Id. at 8:61–63. STS "has been used in oncology for preventing cisplatin nephrotoxicity, carboplatin ototoxicity and as an antidote for extravasation of various chemotherapy agents." Id. at 8:63–67. The '190 patent describes administering an effective amount of STS to a pediatric patient following the administration of a platinum-based chemotherapeutic. Id. at 1:59–62. According to the '190 patent, "the administration of STS was found to not adversely affect the efficacy of the platinum based chemotherapeutic and decreased the incidence and severity of ototoxicity in pediatric patients." Id. at 1:62–65.

## C. Principles of Law Concerning a Motion to Amend

In an inter partes review, amended claims are not added to a patent as of right, but rather must be proposed as a part of a motion to amend.

35 U.S.C. § 316(d). Ordinarily, the petitioner “bears the burden of persuasion to show, by a preponderance of the evidence, that any proposed substitute claims are unpatentable.” 37 C.F.R. § 42.121(d)(2); Lectrosonics, Inc. v. Zaxcom, Inc., IPR2018-01129, Paper 15 (“Lectrosonics”), 3–4 (PTAB Feb. 25, 2019) (precedential); Bosch Auto. Serv. Sols. LLC v. Iancu, 878 F.3d 1027, 1040 (Fed. Cir. 2017).

But before considering the patentability of the proposed substitute claim, we first must determine whether the Revised MTA meets the statutory and regulatory requirements set forth in 35 U.S.C. § 316(d) and 37 C.F.R. § 42.121. Lectrosonics at 4. Patent Owner bears the burden of persuasion to show that: (1) the amendment proposes a reasonable number of substitute claims; (2) the amendment responds to a ground of unpatentability involved in the trial; (3) the amendment does not seek to enlarge the scope of the claims of the patent or introduce new subject matter; and (4) the original disclosure sets forth written description support for each proposed claim. Id. at 4–8.

Specifically, an amendment may not introduce new matter. 35 U.S.C. § 316(d)(3); 37 C.F.R. § 42.121(a)(2)(ii). New subject matter is any addition to the claims that lacks sufficient support in the subject patent's original disclosure. See TurboCare Div. of Demag Delaval Turbomach. Corp. v. Gen. Elec. Co., 264 F.3d 1111, 1118 (Fed. Cir. 2001) (“When [an] applicant adds a claim . . . , the new claim[] . . . must find support in the original specification.”). Thus, a motion to amend must set forth: “(1) The support in the original disclosure of the patent for each claim that is added or amended; and (2) The support in an earlier-filed disclosure for each claim for which

benefit of the filing date of the earlier filed disclosure is sought." 37 C.F.R. §§ 42.121(b)(1), 42.121(b)(2).

The test for new matter is whether the original application's disclosure “reasonably conveys to those skilled in the art that the inventor had possession of the claimed subject matter as of the filing date.” Ariad Pharms., Inc. v. Eli Lilly & Co., 598 F.3d 1336, 1351 (Fed. Cir. 2010) (en banc). It “requires an objective inquiry into the four corners of the specification from the perspective of a person of ordinary skill in the art” to determine whether the specification “describe[s] an invention understandable to that skilled artisan and show that the inventor actually invented the invention claimed.” Id.

## D. Level of Ordinary Skill in the Art

Petitioner contends that an ordinarily skilled artisan “would have possessed an advanced degree, such as a Ph.D., M.D., Pharm.D., and two years of experience with the treatment of cancer, including platinum-based therapy such as cisplatin.” Pet. 7–8 (citing Ex. 1007 ¶¶ 26–29).

Patent Owner argues that Petitioner's proposed definition of the level of ordinary skill is “overly broad.” MTA 7. According to Patent Owner, an ordinarily skilled artisan

would have at least the following qualifications: (a) an M.D. or equivalent medical degree, (b) residency or equivalent training in pediatrics, (c) completion of a fellowship or equivalent training program in pediatric oncology, and (d) at least 2 years in treating central nervous system cancers such as medulloblastoma with platinum-based therapies.

Id.; Revised MTA 11 (citing Ex. 2024 ¶¶ 73–90). Patent Owner appears to challenge the qualification of Dr. Earhart, Petitioner's declarant, as an ordinarily skilled artisan. MTA 9.

Petitioner contends Patent Owner's proposed definition of the level of ordinary skill is inappropriate because it “requires qualifications that exclude even the sole inventor of the ’190 Patent, Dr. Edward Neuwelt.” MTA Opp. 1. According to Petitioner, Dr. Neuwelt is “a neurosurgeon,” not a pediatric oncologist, and lacks the requisite “at least 2 years” experience in platinum-based therapies. Id. (citing Ex. 1003, 18; Ex. 1038, 98:9–17, 100:15–101, 4). We agree with Petitioner.

It is well settled that inventors are typically persons skilled in the field of the invention. Phillips v. AWH Corp., 415 F.3d 1303, 1313 (Fed. Cir. 2005) (en banc). Petitioner presents persuasive evidence to show that under Patent Owner's proposed definition of the level of ordinary skill, Dr. Neuwelt, the sole inventor, would not qualify as an ordinarily skilled artisan, because he is not a pediatric oncologist, even though he is considered “one of the world leading experts on this compound [STS] and otoprotection” and “an expert in the area of [STS] as a . . . drug to use in children with medulloblastoma.” Ex. 1038, 98:9–17, 99:17–18, 101:18–102:13.

Citing Dr. Earhart's testimony, Petitioner argues “a POSITA ‘may work as part of a multidisciplinary team and draw not only upon his or her own skills, but also take advantage of specialized skills of other team members.’” MTA Opp. 2 (quoting Ex. 1007 ¶ 26). The testimony of Dr. Rassekh, Patent Owner’s expert, supports this argument. Indeed,

Dr. Rassekh testified that “there [are] a number of people on the team that are involved in . . . administering a drug to a patient.” Ex. 1038, 80:19–22; see also id. at 99:7–20 (testifying both a researcher with expertise on STS and a pediatric oncologist are needed to “design a study together” to look the subject matter of the ’190 patent); 131:19–132:7 (testifying that a study committee, including audiologist, pharmacologist, pediatric oncologist, and statistician, would draft the study protocol).

Petitioner contends “Dr. Earhart – a pharmacologist and oncologist with decades of experience in both treating patients with cisplatin, and developing clinical protocols – more than qualifies as a POSITA.” MTA Opp. 2. We agree. For example, Dr. Earhart, an M.D. and a Ph.D., has “worked in academia and in industry as a physician and clinical pharmacologist since 1976.” Ex. 1007 ¶¶ 5, 6. He “first began treating patients [with advanced testicular cancer] using cisplatin in 1978,” and has “investigated and published on cisplatin, as well as other platinum-based chemotherapies.” Id. ¶ 8.

Thus, we adopt Petitioner's definition (see Pet. 7–8) as it is consistent with the prior art's demonstration of the level of ordinary skill in the art at the time of the invention. $^{5}$ In addition, we find that Dr. Earhart and Dr. Rassekh are both qualified to opine from the perspective of an ordinarily skilled artisan as both are persons of at least ordinary skill in the art. See Ex. 1007 ¶¶ 4–19, App. A; Ex. 2001 ¶¶ 9–35, App. A.

## 1. Case Law

As support for the ototoxicity limitation, Patent Owner relies on inferences drawn collectively from seven different tables and several passages in the '243 application. See Revised MTA 4 (citing "Figs.3, 4, 9, 12, 23, 27, 31; p.13, ¶¶1,2; p.22–31 (Example 1”)); see also id. at 5 (relying on numerous other tables and passages as support for the safety efficacy limitation). Patent Owner cites Nalpropion Pharms., Inc. v. Actavis Labs. FL., Inc., 934 F.3d 1344 (Fed. Cir. 2019) to support its argument that it can rely on “data from multiple tables in the specification to arrive at a claim limitation [to] satisf[y] the written description requirement.” Revised MTA 5. Nalpropion is distinguishable.

In $Nalpropion$ , the Federal Circuit affirmed the district court's finding that the challenged claim satisfied the written-description requirement. 934 F.3d at 1346. The challenged method claim recited administrating a drug and requires that the drug have an in vitro dissolution profile as defined by recited ranges. $Id.$ at 1347. The district court found that two tables in the specification adequately supported the dissolution data ranges. $Id.$ at 1351. Specifically, it found the values in the two tables “fell squarely” within the claimed ranges. $Id.$ at 1349. According to the Federal Circuit,

the [district] court was not convinced that relying on data from two tables presented a written description issue, noting that it found “nothing odd or invalidating about the inventors looking to different tables of dissolution data and other places in the

specification to determine the ranges for the claimed dissolution profile,” and finding that “multiple tests are necessarily required to establish a range.”

Id. at 1351 (citing Orexigen Therapeutics, Inc. v. Actavis Labs. FL, Inc., 282 F. Supp. 3d 793, 803 (D. Del. 2017)). Notably, part of the court's fact-finding involved evaluating and weighing experts' testimony. Id.

The Federal Circuit stated that “these findings are supported by the record, and we do not disturb them.” Id. It explained that “[w]hile as a general matter written description may not be satisfied by so-called equivalent disclosure, in this case, buttressed by the district court’s fact-finding, and where the so-called equivalence relates only to resultant dissolution parameters rather than operative claim steps, we affirm the district court’s conclusion.” Id.

The facts in the instant case are different from those in $Nalpropion$ . First, unlike the challenged claim there, which involved ranges, proposed amended claim 2 here does not recite any range. Thus, the district court's reasoning that “multiple tests are necessarily required to establish a range” does not apply. See $Nalpropion$ , 934 F.3d at 1351.

Second, unlike the two tables in Nalpropion, which discloses values that “fell squarely” within the claimed ranges (id. at 1349), none of the seven disparate figures and several passages Patent Owner relies on expressly discloses the ototoxicity efficacy limitation. See infra Section III.E.2.a. Instead, Patent Owner presents a series of complicated deductions, at least some of which are based on questionable premises (see id.), in order to calculate the reduction of ototoxicity in “all potential scenarios.” See Revised MTA 5–8.

Third, unlike the patentee in $Nalpropion$ , who assisted the district court with expert testimony, Patent Owner here relies on, not expert testimony, but mere attorney argument to tie together the data from the disparate figures in the '243 application, depriving us of a sound evidentiary foundation on which to assess the accuracy of Patent Owner's representations and to piece together the many uncertain pieces of the puzzle. See infra Section III.E.2.a.

Similarly, we are not persuaded by Patent Owner's reliance on Indivior UK Ltd. v. Reddy's Labs. S.A., 18 F.4th 1323, 1326 (Fed. Cir. 2021). See Reply 6. According to Patent Owner, in Indivior, the Federal Circuit upheld “a PTAB finding of written description support for claim 8 based on calculations attainable from tables which could be performed by a POSITA.” Id. That may well be the case; but the facts here, again, are different, because there is no evidence to show the calculations performed by Patent Owner's counsel, without any expert testimony or other competent evidence to support, “could be performed by a POSITA.” See id. Thus, Indivior, like Nalpropion, is distinguishable.

## 2. Ototoxicity Efficacy Limitation

## a. The '243 Application

Patent Owner argues that the '243 application provides written-description support for the ototoxicity efficacy limitation. Revised MTA 4–8 (citing "Figs.3, 4, 9, 12, 23, 27, 31; p.13, ¶¶1,2; p.22–31 (Example 1)"). Even if we agree with Patent Owner that Nalpropion and Indivior permit Patent Owner to rely on the disparate disclosures in the '243 application for support, we still find that Patent Owner has not met its burden to show that materials it points to provide sufficient support for the ototoxicity efficacy limitation.

We start with the text of the specification. Page 13 of the '243 application discloses "methods for reducing ototoxicity in patient[s] (i.e., pediatric patients) having a cancer and who are receiving a platinum based chemotherapeutic for treatment of the cancer." Ex. 2070, 13. This passage, however, does not mention the claimed age (less than 5 years), cancer type (medulloblastoma), or extent of disease (localized and non-metastatic).

Similarly, Example 1 of the '243 application discloses “[p]articipants in the study were aged 1-18 years at study entry and were newly diagnosed with hepatoblastoma, germ cell tumour, medulloblastoma or CNS primitive neuroectodermal tumour, neuroblastoma, osteosarcoma, or other cancer types treated with cisplatin.” Id. at 22. Although Example 1 discloses the study results “[f]or participants younger than 5 years” (id. at 28), it does not specify the results for patients in that age group with the required cancer type (medulloblastoma) and the extent of disease (localized and non-metastatic), as recited in proposed substitute claim 2.

We next move on to the seven figures Patent Owner relies on. Among those figures, only Figures 4 and 9 show data on hearing loss in medulloblastoma patients. Id. at 60, 65. Neither of them, however, describes the age of the patients or the extent of disease. In fact, as explained below, considering information from Figures 23 and 31, it is clear the medulloblastoma patients in Figures 4 and 9 are not limited to those “less than 5 years of age.”

Figure 31 provides “the characteristics of the tumors for patients under the age of 5 enrolled in the study of Example 1.” Id. at 9. The last figure Patent Owner relies on, Figure 27, “is a table providing the demographics of patients with osteosarcoma tumors enrolled in the study of Example 1.” Ex. 2070, 9. This figure does not appear to directly relate to the medulloblastoma patients. Yet Patent Owner manages to bring it into a theory that spans nearly three full pages, where Patent Owner sends us into a labyrinth, branched with if-this-then-that and either-or circumstances, and ending with a table of “all potential scenarios”—entirely without the guidance of expert testimony. See Revised MTA 5–8. What Patent Owner does not show is even a single actual scenario that provides support for the ototoxicity efficacy limitation, i.e., reduction of ototoxicity is at least about 60% for patients “less than 5 years of age” and with a “localized and non-metastatic medulloblastoma tumor.”

The lack of expert testimony supporting Patent Owner's arguments is especially troublesome here. It is true that in some patent cases, “expert testimony will not be necessary because the technology will be easily understandable without the need for expert explanatory testimony.” Centricut, LLC v. Esab Grp., Inc., 390 F.3d 1361, 1369 (Fed. Cir. 2004) (quotation marks omitted). This is not such a case. Regardless of the exact definition, the parties agree that the level of ordinary skill is high and may require input from a multidisciplinary team. See supra Section III.D. For example, the ’190 patent discloses the importance of statistical analysis in its exemplary studies. See Ex. 1001, 18:24–19:37, 32:46–33:3; see also Ex. 1038, 131:25–132:7 (Dr. Rassekh testifying that a statistician contributes to the development of study schema). This is because interpreting clinical study data is not always simple or straightforward.

One of Patent Owner's arguments illustrates this point well. Patent Owner argues that, in the '243 application, "Figs. 12 and 23 show that 3 medulloblastoma patients in the STS arm had prior cranial irradiation—2 had localized and 1 had disseminated disease." Revised MTA 6 (citing Ex. 2070, 68, 79). Although Figure 23 on its face shows that three medulloblastoma patients in the STS arm had prior cranial irradiation, and Figure 12 shows two patients with localized tumors had prior cranial irradiation, it is not apparent that the two patients referred to in Figure 12 have medulloblastoma, and not the other types of cancer listed therein. $^{7}$ See Ex. 2070, 68, 79.

This is especially so in view of Figure 14, which provides “the characteristics of the tumors of patients with disseminated disease enrolled in the study of Example 1.” Id. at 7. Figure 14 shows two patients with disseminated disease had prior cranial irradiation. Id. at 70. Thus, it is not clear to us, and Patent Owner does not explain, why among the three medulloblastoma patients in the STS arm who had prior cranial irradiation as shown in Figure 23, “2 had localized and 1 had disseminated disease,” and not the other way around. See Revised MTA 6. Given the doubt casted by Figure 14, we accord Patent Owner’s interpretation of the data little weight because it is unsupported by expert testimony or any other competent factual evidence. See In re Payne, 606 F.2d 303, 315 (CCPA 1979) (“Arguments of counsel unsupported by competent factual evidence of record are entitled to little weight.”).

The Revised MTA is replete with such flaws. Another example brings the seemingly irrelevant Figure 27 into the picture. After making the unsubstantiated argument that, among the three medulloblastoma patients in the STS arm who had prior cranial irradiation as shown in Figure 23, “2 had localized and 1 had disseminated disease,” Patent Owner continues, again, without citing any evidence for support, that “[t]his accounts for 3 of the 4 patients >5 years of age.” Revised MTA 6. According to Patent Owner,

Fig. 27 shows the fourth patient in the STS arm who received prior cranial irradiation had osteosarcoma. [Ex. 2070,] 83. Depending on the hearing outcome of the osteosarcoma patient with prior cranial irradiation, either 1 (osteosarcoma patient had hearing loss) or 2 (osteosarcoma patient did not have hearing loss) of 3 medulloblastoma patients with prior cranial irradiation had hearing loss. See Id. at 28, ¶2; Ex. 2024,¶102.

Paragraph 102 of Dr. Rassekh's Declaration, however, does not support Patent Owner's assertion. Instead, Dr. Rassekh testifies that

[t]he stratum of previous craniospinal radiation therapy contained 8 participants: 2 of the 4 (50%) participants receiving STS had hearing loss compared to 4 of the 4 (100%) participants not receiving STS, and none of the hearing loss assessments were changed at the 1 year follow up period. (Ex. 1001 at 20: 28-32, 47-50.)

Ex. 2024 ¶ 102. Dr. Rassekh offers no opinion on how many osteosarcoma patients with prior cranial irradiation and how many medulloblastoma patients with prior cranial irradiation had hearing loss.

We acknowledge that during deposition, Dr. Rassekh testified that “[t]here is data . . . in the patent figures that allows a person of skill in the art to look through the tables and show that the 60 percent bar is met in medulloblastoma, under five, localized.” Ex. 1038, 206:1–4; see also id. at 206:9–11 (“[I]f you look at the data, worse case scenario is a 60 percent bar is reached, based on the data presented in the patent.”).⁸ But Dr. Rassekh does not explain which figures and what data support his conclusion. Under our Rules, “[e]xpert testimony that does not disclose the underlying facts or data on which the opinion is based is entitled to little or no weight.” 37 C.F.R. § 42.65(a). Thus, we accord little weight to Dr. Rassekh’s deposition testimony on this issue. See Koito Mfg. Co., Ltd. v. Turn-Key-Tech, LLC, 381 F.3d 1142, 1152 (Fed. Cir. 2004) (stating that “[g]eneral and conclusory testimony” by an expert “does not suffice as substantial evidence of invalidity”).

In sum, we find the disclosures of the '243 application that Patent Owner relies on do not provide sufficient written-description support for the ototoxicity efficacy limitation.

As a preliminary matter, in its briefing, including the Revised MTA and the Reply, Patent Owner does not rely on these claims of the '243 application for support of the ototoxicity efficacy limitation. Thus, this argument, presented only during oral argument, is waived. See, e.g., Paper 12, 8 ("Patent Owner is cautioned that any arguments not raised in the response may be deemed waived.").

Counsel for Patent Owner pointed to the safety efficacy support table, which lists claim 34. Tr. 21:13–20; see also Revised MTA 5 (citing Ex. 2070, claim 34 as support for the safety efficacy limitation). But even if we were to deem this reference to claim 34 in the discussion of the safety efficacy limitation as part of the discussion of the ototoxicity efficacy limitation, we still would reject Patent Owner's argument.

Claim 34 is directed to the “method of claims 1–33.” Ex. 2070, 53. Each of claims 9, 10, and 12–33 is a multiple dependent claim. Id. at 50–53. Claim 9 is directed to the “method of claims 1–8;” claim 10 is directed to the “method of claims 1–9;” claim 12 is directed to the “method of claims 1–11;” so on and so forth, and finally, claim 33 is directed to the “method of claims 1–32.” Id. Thus, claim 34 would encompass an astronomical number of methods, and Patent Owner does not point to blaze marks directing us to the subject matter of the specific combination it attempts to rely on, i.e., the combination of claims 8, 13, 14, and 21. See Fujikawa v. Wattanasin, 93 F.3d 1559, 1570–71 (Fed. Cir. 1996).

And even if we were to arrive at the specific combination, the same problem still exists. Claim 13 recites “[t]he method of claims 1–12, wherein the cancer is localized.” Claim 14 recites “[t]he method of claims 1–13, wherein the cancer is selected from a germ cell tumor, hepatoblastoma, medulloblastoma, neuroblastoma, and osteosarcoma.” Claim 21 recites “[t]he method of claims 1–20, wherein the pediatric patient is about 5 years of age or less.”

To find support for the ototoxicity efficacy limitation in patient “less than 5 years of age” and with “a localized and non-metastatic medulloblastoma tumor,” an ordinarily skilled artisan would have to follow a “maze-like path, each step providing multiple alternative paths.” Regents of the Univ. of Minnesota v. Gilead Scis., Inc., 61 F.4th 1350, 1357 (Fed. Cir. 2023). That, the Federal Circuit explains, “is not a written description of what might have been described if each of the optional steps had been set forth as the only option.” Id. Thus, we find the multiple dependent claims Patent Owner referred to during the oral argument do not provide sufficient written-description support for the ototoxicity efficacy limitation.

## c. Summary

In sum, on this record, we agree with Petitioner that Patent Owner's arguments amount to nothing but “a convoluted and disjointed story—with no supporting expert testimony.” See Revised MTA Opp. 13. We find Patent Owner has failed to meet its burden to show sufficient written-description

support for the ototoxicity efficacy limitation, i.e., reduction of ototoxicity of at least about 60% for patients “less than 5 years of age” and with a “localized and non-metastatic medulloblastoma tumor.” On this basis alone, we deny Patent Owner’s Revised MTA.

## 3. Overall Survival Efficacy Element

Patent Owner argues that the '243 application provides written-description support for the overall survival efficacy limitation. Revised MTA 5 (citing “p.2,¶¶1,2;p.4,¶1; p.12,¶2; p.20,¶1; claim 34; Figs.5A-B, 13A-B, 16A-B, 17, 20A, 22A, 24A-B, 26A, 28A, 29A, 32A-B, 35A”). We are not persuaded.

The passages on pages 2, 4, 12, and 20 that Patent Owner relies on are general descriptions, and thus, do not provide sufficient support for the overall survival efficacy limitation. Figures 5A, 13A, 16A, 17, 20A, 22A, 24A, 26A, 28A, 29A, 32A, and 35A allegedly provide support for “relapse free survival,” a limitation independent from the overall survival limitation. See Revised MTA 8–9. Thus, we focus our discussion on, as Patent Owner does, Figures 5B, 13B, 16B, 24B, and 32B.

Figures 5B, 13B, 16B, 24B, and 32B show overall survival data for the observation arm and the STS arm. Ex. 2070, 61, 69, 72, 80, 88. As Patent Owner acknowledges, Figure 5B shows data for “the entire patient population for all cancers, all ages, and all extent of disease.” Revised MTA 9. Figure 13B shows data for localized disease and Figure 16B shows data for disseminated disease. Id. Neither specifies patient age or cancer type. Figure 24B shows data for medulloblastoma. Id. It does not specify patient age or extent of disease. Figure 32B shows data for patients <5 years of age. Id. It does not specify cancer type or extent of disease. In other words, none of the figures Patent Owner relies on show data for patients “less than 5 years of age” and with a “localized and non-metastatic medulloblastoma tumor,” as proposed amended claim 2 requires. See Ex. 2070, 61, 69, 72, 80, 88.

Without citing any support, Patent Owner argues that “[w]hen looking at 2 independent events, a POSITA would know that to arrive at the likelihood that both events would occur (in this case a death of a patient with localized disease <5 years of age) is to multiply the individual probabilities.” Revised MTA 9–10. Again, without citing any support, Patent Owner argues that for medulloblastoma patients, “in the OBS arm, a 12% probability exists that the death was a patient <5 with localized disease,” and in the STS arm, that number is 17%. Id. at 10. That means, Patent Owner continues, without citing any support, “likely 0 and at most 1 death” in each arm. Id. Based on these unsupported assumptions and calculations, Patent Owner asserts that “a POSITA would conclude that there is not a significant difference in OS [overall survival] for localized medulloblastoma patients <5 years of age receiving STS.” Id.

As stated above, interpreting data from clinical study is often complicated. Without any expert testimony—or for that matter, any evidence—in support, we accord this mere attorney argument no weight. See Payne, 606 F.2d at 315. Thus, we find Patent Owner has not met its burden to show that the '243 application provides written-description

support for the overall survival efficacy limitation. $^{9}$ On this additional basis, we deny Patent Owner's Revised MTA.

## 4. Summary

In sum, on this record, we find Patent Owner has failed to meet its burden to show sufficient written-description support for the ototoxicity efficacy limitation or the overall survival efficacy limitation for patients “less than 5 years of age” and with a “localized and non-metastatic medulloblastoma tumor.”

## F. Petitioner's Asserted Unpatentability Grounds

In light of our determination above that Patent Owner has not shown sufficient written-description support for proposed substitute claim 2 in the '243 application, we do not reach the grounds of unpatentability set forth in Petitioner's Opposition. See Revised MTA Opp. 17–25 (alleging non-enablement and obviousness).
