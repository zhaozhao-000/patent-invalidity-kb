## I. INTRODUCTION

This is a Final Written Decision in an inter partes review challenging the patentability of claims 1–19 of U.S. Patent No. 9,145,532 B2 (“the ’532 patent,” Ex. 1001). We have jurisdiction under 35 U.S.C. § 6.

Petitioner has the burden of proving unpatentability of the challenged claims by a preponderance of the evidence. 35 U.S.C. § 316(e). Having reviewed the parties' arguments and cited evidence, for the reasons discussed below, we find that Petitioner has not demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence that claims 1–19 are unpatentable.

## D. The '532 Patent (Ex. 1001)

The '532 patent relates to systems and methods for extracting solute from source materials. Ex. 1001, code (54), 1:6–7. According to the Specification, “many existing systems are configured to extract from a single container of source material,” and are thus “unable to simultaneously extract from materials stored in a plurality of distinct containers,” leading to a “bottleneck.” Id. at 1:11–16. “[M]any conventional extraction systems do not allow extraction to be performed in a single, closed loop process that reclaims solvent and re-introduces the reclaimed solvent in subsequent cycles of the system,” and manual reclaiming and reuse of solvent is inefficient. Id. at 1:22–28. The Specification identifies “a need for cooling reclaimed solvent to a liquid state prior to collect[ing]” the solvent, because “[l]iquid solvent may be more efficiently stored” than gaseous solvent. Id. at 1:41–44.

Figure 1 of the '532 patent is reproduced below.

![](images/89697710e53703a9869ea06589a331869fb5abc2153a18ab7cd7082b31b8388d.jpg)

Figure 1, reproduced above, depicts a system for extracting solute from a source material. Id. at 2:34–35. The system includes solvent source container 120, solvent compressor 130, detachable canister system 140, extract container 170, first pump 101, second pump 102, condensing system 105, and solvent collection container 115. Id. at 3:10–14.

The Specification explains that “[s]ource container output 122 is configured to direct solvent contained in solvent source container 120 to solvent compressor 130 via a solvent source line 197.” $^{2}$ Id. at 3:66–4:1. The Specification states that “solvent source line 197 may include an internal filter,” which “may be used to remove impurities in solvent prior to introducing the solvent to detachable canister system 140.” Id. at 4:4–7. “Solvent compressor 130 may be used to compress solvent to an extraction pressure, the extraction pressure selected to maintain solvent in a liquid state even when exposed to an elevated extraction temperature.” Id. at 4:53–56.

According to the Specification, “detachable canister system 140 allows a user to refill a selected canister as one or more of the other canisters remain closed and to continue extracting solute from contained source material.” Id. at 5:23–26. Additionally, “solvent compressor 130 is configured to communicate with each detachable canister individually,” so that “when one canister is attached and extracting, solvent compressor 130 may charge solvent for a second canister.” Id. at 5:21–22, 6:20–22.

The Specification describes an example in which butane is used to extract essential oils from lavender plants contained within a canister to produce an extract solution. Id. at 3:18–21. The extract mixture is heated “to a distilling temperature to produce an evaporated portion of the solvent in extract container 170,” where “[t]he distilling temperature . . . is greater than the boiling point of butane and less than typical essential oil boiling points.” Id. at 8:28–33. Condensing system 105 is configured to cool post-extraction solvent before the solvent reaches solvent collection container 115. Id. at 9:23–28.

## II. ANALYSIS

## A. Level of Ordinary Skill in the Art

## 1. Defining the Level of Ordinary Skill in the Art

We consider the grounds of unpatentability in view of the understanding of a person of ordinary skill in the art as of the effective filing date of the challenged claims. See, e.g., Graham v. John Deere Co., 383 U.S. 1, 17–18 (1966). Petitioner contends that a person of ordinary skill in the art (sometimes referred to herein as a “POSITA” or “POSA”) would typically have either:

(1) a bachelor's degree in mechanical engineering, chemical engineering, petroleum engineering, or an equivalent field; and some academic or industry experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material or similar devices; or

(2) at least four years of industry experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material or similar devices.

Pet. 11 (citing Ex. 1002 (Chess Decl.) ¶ 13).

Patent Owner “propose[s] to expand the level of ordinary skill in the art” recited in Petitioner’s proposal “to include educational experience in chemistry, biology, or equivalent fields of physical or life science,” as follows (Patent Owner’s proposed additions are shown in bold):

(1) a bachelor's degree in chemistry, biology, mechanical engineering, chemical engineering, petroleum engineering, or an equivalent field of physical science, life science, or engineering; and some academic or industry experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material or similar devices; or

(2) at least four years of industry experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material or similar devices.

PO Resp. 4–5; Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl.) ¶¶ 39–43. Patent Owner contends that these additions are appropriate because “[t]he ‘educational level of the inventor’ is relevant in determining the level of skill in the art,” and one of the inventors listed on the ’532 patent has a life sciences degree. PO Resp. 4, n.1 (citing Env’t Designs, Ltd. v. Union Oil Co., 713 F.2d 693, 696 (Fed. Cir. 1983)); Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl.) ¶ 42.

On the full trial record, we adopt Petitioner's proposed level of ordinary skill in the art, with Patent Owner's additions. The additions are consistent with the evidence of record. For example, we find that adding chemistry and biology degrees is appropriate in view of Dr. Miller's testimony that college-level chemistry and biology courses typically include training on extractive technologies. Ex. 1021 (Miller Tr.), 38:9–39:18. We also agree with Patent Owner that the educational level of the inventors may be considered (see Env't Designs, Ltd., 713 F.2d at 696), and on this record, we accept Patent Owner and Dr. Miller's undisputed representation that "named inventor Jason Wasserman had a bachelor's degree in Agricultural Science and Sustainable Agriculture, a life sciences degree." Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl.) ¶ 42; PO Resp. 4.

Thus, for the reasons above, we define a person of ordinary skill in the art as someone who would have had

(1) a bachelor's degree in chemistry, biology, mechanical engineering, chemical engineering, petroleum engineering, or an equivalent field of physical science, life science, or engineering; and some academic or industry experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material or similar devices; or

(2) at least four years of industry experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material or similar devices.

## 2. Whether Dr. Miller Qualifies as a Person of Ordinary Skill in the Art

Petitioner argues that Dr. Miller does not qualify as a person of ordinary skill in the art because “he lacks the specific academic or industry experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material or similar devices.” Reply 3. Petitioner argues that “between 1988–2015 [Dr. Miller] performed no organic butane extraction, organic propane extraction, or CO2 organic material extraction.” Id. (citing Ex. 1021 (Miller Tr.), 58:7–18). Petitioner further argues that between 1988 and 2015, “the sole organic closed-loop extraction” Dr. Miller performed was “Soxhlet-style extraction,” which Petitioner contends is “rudimentary and provides insufficient basis in establishing one’s standing as a PHOSITA.” Id. at 4 (citing Ex. 1021 (Miller Tr.), 59:4–16).

We are not persuaded that Dr. Miller does not qualify as a person of ordinary skill in the art. As an initial matter, we note that Petitioner is improperly trying to narrow its original proposed level of skill in the art to now require specific experience with “organic extractions” and/or “closed loop extraction systems.” See Reply 5 (“Dr. Miller cannot testify to the knowledge of one skilled in the art at the time of the ‘532 Patent because as of 2013, Dr. Miller had never performed organic extractions.”), 6 (criticizing Dr. Miller’s experience because “his experience and expertise in closed loop extraction systems began only after 2015”). Petitioner’s original proposal was much more broadly drawn, i.e., to “experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material.” Pet. 11. As explained in our Trial Practice Guide, in a reply brief, a party is not permitted to “proceed in a new direction with a new approach as compared to the positions taken in a prior filing,” as Petitioner attempts to do here. Trial Practice Guide, $^{11}$ 74; see also 37 C.F.R. § 42.23(b). We do not consider Petitioner’s belated arguments seeking to narrow its broad proposal to require experience with “organic extractions” or “closed loop extraction systems.”

Petitioner acknowledges that “Dr. Miller has advanced degrees in life sciences,” which comports with the educational requirement specified in part (1) of the level of skill we use herein. Reply 3; supra Section II.A.1 (referring to “a bachelor’s degree in . . . biology, . . . or an equivalent field of . . . life science”). Additionally, Dr. Miller has “decades (1988 to present) of direct experience in the use of partial and complete extractions of diverse materials in environmental studies.” Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl.) ¶ 8. He has also “studied, used, and developed thousands of extractions to solubilize and remove oils, wastes, metals, and other toxins from soil, water, plants, and animal tissues for analysis.” Id. He avers that “[e]xtractive chemistry was core to [his] business practice in 1988 and remains so to this day,” and that “[b]y 1992 [he] would have developed ordinary skill in the art having 4 years of experience relevant to the current subject.” Id. ¶ 10.

Based on this experience, we determine that Dr. Miller qualifies as a person of ordinary skill in the art because by 1992, he had “at least four years of industry experience working with, designing, or studying methods of extracting solute from a source material or similar devices,” as required by the level of ordinary skill we adopt herein. He also avers that he gained additional training and engaged in additional extraction work prior to the effective filing date. Id.

Additionally, between 2015 and 2020, Dr. Miller “enter[ed] the cannabis extraction and concentration industry, designing, building, and operating [his] own facility,” further qualifying him as a person of ordinary skill in the art. Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl.) ¶ 10; see also Reply 6 (acknowledging Dr. Miller’s post-2015 “experience and expertise in closed loop extraction systems”). Although this work started in 2015 (two years after the effective filing date of 2013), a person of ordinary skill in the art need not have been one of ordinary skill at the time of a patent’s filing date. See U.S. Endoscopy Group, Inc. v. CDX Diagnostics, Inc., IPR2014-00639, Paper 27, at 18 (Sept. 14, 2015) (“A witness must provide testimony about the level of skill in the art as of the critical date; however, the witness need not have acquired that knowledge as of the critical date.”).

For the reasons discussed above, we determine that Dr. Miller is qualified in this proceeding to testify from the perspective of a person of ordinary skill in the art.

## B. Claim Construction

In an inter partes review, we interpret a claim “using the same claim construction standard that would be used to construe the claim in a civil action under 35 U.S.C. 282(b).” 37 C.F.R. § 42.100(b). Under this standard, we construe the claim “in accordance with the ordinary and customary meaning of such claim as understood by one of ordinary skill in the art and the prosecution history pertaining to the patent.” Id.

Petitioner asserts that “the claim terms should be construed according to their ordinary and customary meaning,” but also states that certain terms (i.e., “canister,” “extract container,” “solvent collection container,” and “boiling point”) “warrant clarification.” Pet. 12. Patent Owner does not take issue with these terms, and instead requests that we construe certain of the claimed method steps “as distinct, sequential, and non-continuous steps,” and that we construe the phrase “exposing . . . for a predetermined period of time.” Id. For completeness, we note that although neither party formally requested construction of the terms “detachable canister interface” and “support” (recited in claim 9) and “line” (recited in claim 15), both parties present claim construction arguments as to the meanings of these terms. See, e.g., PO Resp. 32, 34–36, 37; Reply 22–24.

After consideration of the full trial record, we determine that in order to resolve the controversy between the parties, we need address only the

term “exposing . . . for a predetermined period of time.” See Realtime Data, LLC v. Iancu, 912 F.3d 1368, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2019) (“The Board is required to construe ‘only those terms . . . that are in controversy, and only to the extent necessary to resolve the controversy.’”) (quoting Vivid Techs., Inc. v. Am. Sci. & Eng’g, Inc., 200 F.3d 795, 803 (Fed. Cir. 1999)).

## 1. “exposing . . . for a predetermined period of time”

In step [1(d)], independent claim 1 recites “exposing the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time to create an extract mixture having the solute in solution with the solvent.” Ex. 1001, 17:35–37 (emphasis added). Independent claims 9 and 14 recite a similar step. Id. at 18:43–44, 19:31–34.

Neither party requests a construction for this term that encompasses a specific amount of time or achieves a specific goal (e.g., exposing the source material to solvent for an amount of time sufficient to extract the desired amount of oil). Instead, Patent Owner seeks a construction of this term that excludes continuous processes. PO Resp. 6.

More specifically, Patent Owner contends that “exposing . . . for a predetermined period of time” refers to “the time a source material is exposed to a volume of solvent,” and that the Specification makes clear the exposing step is always performed in a canister that is “closed to fluid communication.” See PO Resp. 6–9 (citing, e.g., Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl.) ¶¶ 31–35; Ex. 1001, 3:28–34, 5:23–26, 5:58–60, 5:49–51, 13:30–33, 18:58–19:10). Patent Owner suggests that this understanding of the term limits the claims to batch processes (and excludes continuous processes). See, e.g., PO Resp. 8 (“The ’532 patent continues to describe the method as a batch process and never suggests that its method involves a continuous extraction process.”).

Petitioner disputes Patent Owner's proposed construction, and advocates that “exposing” and “predetermined period of time” should “be given their plain and ordinary meaning.” Reply 7–8. Petitioner notes that in the co-pending litigation, the parties agreed that “predetermined period of time” means “period of time determined in advance,” and the court issued a claim construction order that includes this agreed-upon construction. Id. at 7 (citing Ex. 3001, 11).

We agree with Petitioner that “exposing . . . for a predetermined period of time” should “be given [its] plain and ordinary meaning.” Reply 7–8. To the extent the plain and ordinary meaning needs elaboration, we adopt the agreed-upon construction of “predetermined period of time” from the related district court litigation, i.e., a “period of time determined in advance.” $^{12}$ Ex. 3001, 11.

For clarity, we note that nothing in this plain and ordinary meaning limits the term to a batch process or excludes a continuous process. We do not see any basis to read into this limitation requirements that the source material contact a volume of solvent within a closed or sealed container. See Sur-reply 4. Although the Specification indicates that the step of exposing the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time may take place in a canister that is “closed to fluid communication,” Patent

Owner has not pointed us to any persuasive intrinsic evidence indicating that the exposing step must take place in a canister that is “closed to fluid communication.” The Specification “uses the broad permissive term ‘may’ when describing a ‘user may use the output valves to direct the extract mixture contained in an associated canister to extract container.’” Reply 10 (quoting Ex. 1001, 5:37–39, emphasis Petitioner’s). Accordingly, Patent Owner does not persuade us that the intrinsic record “strongly support[s] that ‘exposing the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time to create an extract mixture’” requires a batch process or excludes a continuous process. See PO Resp. 6.

## 2. Conclusion

We determine that “exposing . . . for a predetermined period of time” should be given its plain and ordinary meaning, i.e., “exposing . . . for a period of time determined in advance.”

## D. Legal Standards

“In an [inter partes review], the petitioner has the burden from the onset to show with particularity why the patent it challenges is unpatentable.” Harmonic Inc. v. Avid Tech., Inc., 815 F.3d 1356, 1363 (Fed.

Cir. 2016); 37 C.F.R. § 42.104(b). This burden of persuasion never shifts to the patent owner. See Dynamic Drinkware, LLC v. Nat'l Graphics, Inc., 800 F.3d 1375, 1378 (Fed. Cir. 2015) (discussing the burden of proof in inter partes review). To prevail, Petitioner must demonstrate patentability by a preponderance of the evidence. 35 U.S.C. § 316(e).

A claim is unpatentable as obvious “if the differences between the claimed invention and the prior art are such that the claimed invention as a whole would have been obvious before the effective filing date of the claimed invention to a person having ordinary skill in the art to which the claimed invention pertains.” 35 U.S.C. § 103; see also KSR Int’l Co. v. Teleflex Inc., 550 U.S. 398, 406 (2007). The question of obviousness is resolved based on underlying factual determinations including: (1) the scope and content of the prior art; (2) any differences between the claimed subject matter and the prior art; (3) the level of ordinary skill in the art; and (4) any objective indicia of nonobviousness. Graham, 383 U.S. at 17–18. An obviousness determination requires a reason to combine “accompanied by a reasonable expectation of achieving what is claimed in the patent-at-issue.” Intelligent Bio-Sys., Inc. v. Illumina Cambridge Ltd., 821 F.3d 1359, 1367 (Fed. Cir. 2016).

## E. Analysis

Independent claims 1 and 9 recite a step of “exposing the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time to create an extract mixture having the solute in solution with the solvent.” Ex. 1001, 17:35–37, 18:44–46 (emphasis added). Independent claim 14 recites a similar limitation. See id. at 19:31–34 (“exposing the source material in a canister to a liquid solvent for a predetermined period of time to create an extract

mixture having the solute in solution with the liquid solvent") (emphasis added).

Petitioner challenges independent claims 1, 9, and 14 in Grounds 1, 4, and 5, respectively. See Pet. 6. In each of these Grounds, Petitioner uses Hebert to teach the predetermined period of time claim limitation. See id. at 14 (for Ground 1, arguing that “Britt does not explicitly describe exposing the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time to create an extract mixture,” and that “Hebert cures this deficiency”), 25–26 (for Ground 4, arguing that Buese does not disclose the “predetermined period of time” limitation and that Hebert cures this deficiency), 41 (arguing same for Ground 5).

Patent Owner argues that Petitioner fails to explain how or why a person of ordinary skill in the art would have modified Britt or Buese with the teachings of Hebert. See PO Resp. 20.

After considering all of the arguments and cited evidence of record, we determine that Petitioner has not established by a preponderance of the evidence that any challenged claim is unpatentable as obvious. In particular, we agree with Patent Owner that Petitioner fails to explain how or why a person of ordinary skill in the art would have modified Britt or Buese with the teachings of Hebert. See, e.g., PO Resp. 23.

In Ground 1, Petitioner maps most of the limitations of independent claim 14 to Britt. See, e.g., Pet. 15–17. In Grounds 4 and 5, Petitioner maps most of the limitations of independent claims 1 and 9 to Buese. See id. at 28–31. Britt and Buese both teach continuous extraction systems, wherein exposing the source material to a solvent “leads immediately, inexorably, and continuously to the solvent percolating (Britt) or flowing (Buese)

through the source material and to the separation component.” Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl. ¶ 46); PO Resp. 2, 22–23. Petitioner asserts that neither Britt nor Buese “explicitly describe[s] exposing the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time to create an extract mixture.” Pet. 14, 25, 41–42. For this teaching, Petitioner turns to Hebert. See id.

Hebert “relates to a method for extracting oil from oil-bearing plant parts.” Ex. 1009 (Hebert), 1:16–22. In an exemplary method, Hebert discusses a system for extracting oil from rice bran. See id. at 3:62–67. A reactor vessel is loaded with rice bran, and liquid solvent is added. See id. at 4:1–2, 4:25–28. Hebert teaches that after sufficient solvent is added,

[t]he liquid solvent is constantly maintained by means of heat exchanger 22 within the temperature range of $68^{\circ}-125^{\circ}$ F., preferably $68^{\circ}-72^{\circ}$ F., prior to and during liquid filling of reactor 2 in order to cause the liquid solvent to dissolve the rice oil from the rice bran in reactor 2 at a predetermined rate and time.

Id. at 4:40–45 (emphasis added). Petitioner specifically relies on the above-quoted portion of Hebert as “provid[ing] a rationale for a PHOSITA to expose the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time.” Pet. 14 (citing Ex. 1002 (Chess Decl.) ¶ 72; Ex. 1009 (Hebert), 4:40–45), 25, 41–42; see also Reply 16 (repeating same statement).

The failing in Petitioner's arguments is that the Petition never identifies what “rationale” Hebert allegedly provides for a person of ordinary skill in the art “to expose the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time.” Pet. 14. We have examined each citation and argument in the Petition, but find no articulation of a reason why a person of ordinary skill in the art would have applied this teaching in Hebert to Britt and Buese's processes. We walk through each citation and argument below. $^{13}$

Petitioner cites Mr. Chess's declaration at paragraph 72, but that paragraph merely repeats the Petition, and thus also fails to specify a rationale for why a person of ordinary skill in the art would have imported Hebert's teachings into Britt or Buese. See Ex. 1002 (Chess Decl.) ¶ 72; Pet. 14.

Petitioner next contends that a person of ordinary skill in the art “would have been motivated to combine Britt with Hebert” because

both concern closed loop extraction systems and methods that use a recycled solvent to extract plant or other extractable material following similar steps of exposure to a solvent to create a solute/solvent mixture, separation of the solute and solvent, and collection of the solvent. (Exhibit 1009, Abstract; Exhibit 1005 $^{14}$ , Abstract; Exhibit 1002, ¶73). Thus, a PHOSITA viewing Britt would have looked to Hebert (and vice versa) to inform possible improvements or alternative approaches to perform the same functions within the same general structure.

Pet. 15. The first sentence of this quotation establishes that Britt and Hebert's respective systems share some similarities, but does not proffer a reason why a person of ordinary skill in the art would have combined the specific cited teaching from Hebert with Britt. The second sentence quoted above refers to “possible improvements or alternative approaches,” but fails to elaborate on what specific “improvements” or “alternative approaches” Hebert’s teaching offers Britt’s system. $^{15}$ This quotation also cites Mr. Chess’s declaration at paragraph 73, but that paragraph in the Chess Declaration again merely repeats the Petition, and thus adds nothing to the analysis.

Finally, the Petition presents a claim chart. For the relevant limitation of independent claim 14 (designated 14(b)), the Petition provides the following:

<table><tr><td>14(b) exposing the source material in a canister to a liquid solvent for a predetermined period of time to create an extract mixture having the solute in solution with the liquid solvent;</td><td>Britt discloses a step wherein “[t]he solvent then flows through the petals 44, extracting oil from the petals 44 to yield a mixture of the solvent and the oil.” (Id., ¶13).Hebert teaches the maintenance of solvent temperature of the liquid solvent “during liquid filling of reactor 2 in order to cause the liquid solvent to dissolve the rice oil from the rice bran in reactor 2 at a predetermined rate and time.” (Exhibit 1009, 4:40–45).Accordingly, it would have beenobvious to a PHOSITA to expose the source material of Buese $^{16}$  to a liquid solvent for a predetermined period of time to create an extract mixture having the solute in solution with the solvent. (Exhibit 1002, XIII.14(b)).</td></tr></table>

Pet. 16–17. The table above quotes claim limitation 14(b) on the left, and presents three paragraphs of analysis on the right. The first and second paragraphs of the analysis simply quote teachings of Hebert and Britt. The third paragraph of the analysis does little more than recite the claim limitation, prefaced by the words “Accordingly, it would have been obvious to a PHOSITA to [insert quote of claim language].” This portion simply asserts a legal conclusion. This third paragraph also cites a corresponding claim chart in Mr. Chess’s declaration, but that chart is identical to the chart in the Petition, and thus adds nothing to the analysis in the Petition.

Patent Owner argues that Petitioner's assertions as to how and why a person of ordinary skill in the art would have modified continuous extraction systems like Britt and Buese's with a teaching from Hebert, which relates to batch processes, are cursory and unsupported. PO Resp. 2–3. We agree.

First, Petitioner never elaborates on how a person of ordinary skill in the art would have modified Britt or Buese to implement Hebert's teaching of maintaining temperature of the liquid solvent in order to dissolve the oil from the source material “at a predetermined rate and time.” See Pet. 16. For example, to the extent a person of ordinary skill in the art would have made changes to Britt or Buese’s processes to implement this teaching, Petitioner fails to explain what changes a person of ordinary skill in the art would have made.

To the extent Petitioner is contending that a person of ordinary skill in the art would have “[m]odif[ied] Britt or Buese to stop the continuous flow of solvent through the source material in order to control solvent exposure for a ‘predetermined period of time,’” we agree with Patent Owner that this “would ‘fundamentally alter’ their operation,” given the different types of extraction systems in Hebert on the one hand (a batch process) and Britt and Buese on the other (continuous processes). PO Resp. 25 (quoting Plas-Pak Indus., Inc. v. Sulzer Mixpac AG, 600 F. App’x 755, 759 (Fed. Cir. 2015) (nonprecedential)); Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl.) ¶51. The extraction methods of Britt and Buese continuously and repeatedly wash source material with fresh solvent, whereas Hebert’s batch process extracts solute from a source material in a single exposure. See PO Resp. 23; Ex. 2008 (Miller Decl.) ¶47. Petitioner does not address the differences in the processes and how a person of ordinary skill in the art would have addressed them.

Second, Petitioner nowhere explains why a person of ordinary skill in the art would have seen an advantage in implementing Hebert's teaching in the context of Britt and Buese's processes. For example, as Patent Owner correctly notes, “Petitioner provides no particular explanation of why a POSA would apply Hebert’s teachings to Britt or Buese without undermining the benefits of these continuous extraction systems.” PO Resp. 19–20. To the extent a person of ordinary skill in the art would have seen an advantage in applying Hebert's teachings to Britt and Buese, Petitioner does not identify it. This is not sufficient to sustain an obviousness challenge.

Petitioner's arguments find no additional support in the testimony of Mr. Chess. First, as noted above, his declaration simply parrots the arguments in the Petition and thus does not add any analysis not already discussed above. See, e.g., TQ Delta, LLC v. CISCO Sys., Inc., 942 F.3d 1352, 1359 (Fed. Cir. 2019) (“conclusory expert testimony is inadequate to support an obviousness determination”); InTouch Techs., Inc. v. VGO Commc’ns, Inc., 751 F.3d 1327, 1353–54 (Fed. Cir. 2014) (reversing district court’s judgment of invalidity because the expert testimony “failed to provide any meaningful explanation for why one of ordinary skill in the art would be motivated to combine these references at the time of this invention”); Xerox Corp. v. Bytemark, Inc., IPR2022-00624, Paper 9, 15 (PTAB Aug. 24, 2022) (precedential).

Second, we agree with Patent Owner that Mr. Chess's opinion on motivation to combine is entitled to little, if any, weight in view of his cross-examination testimony, which expressly undermined any reason to combine Britt and Hebert. See PO Resp. 2–3, 12. When specifically asked to explain paragraph 73 of his Declaration regarding his opinion articulating a motivation to combine Britt and Hebert, Mr. Chess responded: “I’m personally not motivated to combine anything from any of these guys together. I think they’re all knuckleheads and didn’t know what they were doing, to be frank.” Id. at 24 (quoting Ex. 2009 (Chess Depo. Tr.), 185:2–6, see also 181:11–12 and 183:5 (directing Mr. Chess to paragraph 73 of his Declaration)). Petitioner attempts to rehabilitate this testimony by arguing that during cross-examination, Mr. Chess identified “inefficiencies” that

actually “provide a PHOSITA motivation to combine the presented prior art references more efficiently,” but Petitioner fails to point to any testimony or other evidence of record to support this attorney argument. $^{17}$ Reply 14.

Petitioner also argues that “Mr. Chess indicated the prior art references surrounded systems with similar components, processes, and operative purposes, and could benefit from combination to create a more desirable product through more thoughtful assembly and piping to create more efficiency.” Reply 18 (citing Ex. 2009 (Chess Depo. Tr.), 113:6–115:8). However, even if Petitioner accurately characterizes the cited testimony, it still “does not supply the details missing from [Mr. Chess’s] Declaration and the Petition as to why a POSA would be motivated to combine Britt or Buese with Hebert.” Sur-reply 13.

On Reply, Petitioner quotes case law indicating that “the teaching, suggestion, or motivation test is flexible,” and a “motivation to combine may be implicit.” Reply 12–13 (citing MPEP 2143 G; DyStar Textilfarben GmbH & Co. Deutschland KG v. C.H. Patrick Co., 464 F.3d 1356, 1366 (Fed. Cir. 2006)). Petitioner also states that “the Federal Circuit has held that ‘a challenger need not prove that there was a known problem with the prior art in order to demonstrate that there was a motivation to combine prior art references.’” Id. at 16 (quoting Arctic Cat Inc. v. Polaris Indus., Inc.,

795 F. App’x 827, 833 (Fed. Cir. 2019)). While these are all accurate statements of the law, they do little to articulate any specific reason to combine Hebert with Britt or Buese—which the law also requires. See, e.g., KSR Int’l Co., 550 U.S. at 418 (teaching that obviousness requires “determin[ing] whether there was an apparent reason to combine the known elements in the fashion claimed by the patent at issue”).

Petitioner also argues on Reply that “the combination of the references would result in a more efficient product and process, capable of running continuously, and allows for ongoing maintenance and replacement of components and elements without requiring down-time.” Reply 16–17. Patent Owner correctly notes that this discussion is missing from the Petition as a reason to combine Hebert with Britt or Buese. See Sur-reply 11–12. Petitioner also nowhere elaborates on exactly how or why the proposed combinations would have been more efficient, been capable of running continuously, or would have minimized down-time, let alone with citation to evidence of record. See id.; see also Johnston v. IVAC Corp., 885 F.2d 1574, 1581 (Fed. Cir. 1989) (stating that attorney argument is “no substitute for evidence”).

As discussed above, the Petition asserts that a person of ordinary skill in the art would have looked to Hebert to inform possible improvements or alternative approaches to Britt and Buese. See, e.g., Pet. 15. When asked at oral argument what “improvement” would flow from following Hebert’s teaching to soak the source material in the solvent for a predetermined amount of time, Petitioner’s counsel stated that the improvement would be applying a more analytical mindset to the extraction process. Tr. 45:1–18. That rationale, however, is not stated in the Petition.

Petitioner's counsel also appeared to suggest that the concept of soaking the source material in the solvent for a predetermined amount of time is inherent in the flow rate of a process. See id. Although that rationale has some appeal, on this record we cannot credit it, because as counsel candidly agreed, it is not articulated in the Petition. See id.; see also id. at 36:1–25 (Patent Owner's counsel correctly arguing that Petitioner (i) used Hebert as a motivating reference (as opposed to an evidentiary reference), and (ii) did not make an inherency argument).

We note that any necessary modification of Britt or Buese with Hebert (to the extent Hebert is even needed at all) to reach the “predetermined period of time” limitation, particularly under our construction of this term as meaning a “period of time determined in advance,” may have been trivial or a product of common sense. Petitioner, however, is “master of its complaint.” SAS Inst., Inc. v. Iancu, 138 S. Ct. 1348, 1355 (2018). Petitioner elected to contend that neither Britt nor Buese teaches this limitation, and to argue that “Hebert provides a rationale for a PHOSITA to expose the source material to the solvent for a predetermined period of time.” See Pet. 14. It is thus incumbent on Petitioner to prove up its arguments by a preponderance of the evidence, and as our reviewing court has explained, “[i]t would . . . not be proper for the Board to deviate from the grounds in the petition and raise its own obviousness theory.” Sirona Dental Sys. GmbH v. Institut Straumann AG, 892 F.3d 1349, 1356 (Fed. Cir. 2018); see also In re Magnum Oil Tools Int’l, Ltd., 829 F.3d 1364, 1381 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (“[T]he Board must base its decision on arguments that were advanced by a party, and to which the opposing party was given a chance to respond.”).

On the full trial record, Petitioner fails to adequately explain its proposed combinations of Hebert with Britt or Buese. The Petition does little more than identify Hebert as purportedly teaching the “predetermined period of time” limitation that it contends is missing from Britt and Buese. However, the “[d]etermination of obviousness cannot be based on the hindsight combination of components selectively culled from the prior art to fit the parameters of the patented invention.” ATD Corp. v. Lydall, Inc., 159 F.3d 534, 546 (Fed. Cir. 1998). An obviousness determination also requires a reason to combine, which is wholly missing from the record. See KSR Int’l Co., 550 U.S. at 418 (stating that obviousness “cannot be sustained by mere conclusory statements; instead, there must be some articulated reasoning with some rational underpinning to support the legal conclusion of obviousness”). This failure to articulate a reason to combine infects all six of Petitioner’s grounds. See PO Resp. 24 (“Petitioner fails to support a motivation to combine either Britt or Buese with Hebert in all six grounds.”). Accordingly, for the reasons discussed above, we determine that Petitioner has not demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence that the challenged claims are unpatentable as obvious.

## III. CONCLUSION

Based on the information presented, we conclude that Petitioner has not demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence that claims 1–19 are unpatentable.
