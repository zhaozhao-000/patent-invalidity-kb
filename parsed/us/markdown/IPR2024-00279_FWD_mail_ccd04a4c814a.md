## C. The '675 patent (Ex. 1001)

The '675 patent is titled “Systems and Methods for Delivery of a Therapeutic Agent.” Ex. 1001, code (54). According to the '675 patent, “certain neurotoxins have found widespread use in millions of patients worldwide for a variety of conditions.” Id. at 1:43–47. The '675 patent states that neurotoxins have been proposed for controlling rhinorrhea and many other conditions. Id. at 2:17–46. The '675 patent explains that “[r]hinorrhea is the term describing the effluence of mucus from the lining of the nasal passages, nasopharynx, or paranasal sinuses” and “can be a

symptom of a number of diseases such as the common cold, sinusitis or rhinitis.” Id. at 7:37–41.

The '675 patent states that “[d]ue to their extreme toxicity, neurotoxins are highly controlled and can have disastrous consequences if not used and controlled properly, especially when used in vivo.” Ex. 1001,

## 2:52–54. Thus, the '675 patent explains that

it would be desirable to provide improved methods and systems for delivering toxins, such as botulinum and active botulinum fragments, to the nasal membrane of a patient, particularly a patient suffering from rhinitis or other conditions associated with nasal inflammation and conditions, such as sinus headaches and migraine headaches. The methods and systems should be capable of providing for selective and repeatable delivery of the toxins to defined target areas within the nasal cavities, including particular paranasal sinuses, the nasopharynx, and in some cases substantially the entire nasal cavity. The systems and methods should provide for the safe and effective delivery of the toxins, and in particular should reduce or eliminate the risk of toxin being delivered to non-targeted tissues outside of the nasal cavity.

Id. at 3:50–64.

The '675 patent discloses “methods and apparatus for the control of autonomic nerve function comprising delivery via permeabilization of targeted cell membranes a therapeutically effective amount of a portion or fragment of a neurotoxin”, or “[m]ore particularly, the present invention relates to methods and systems for delivering toxins, such as botulinum toxin light chain fragments, to target cells in a nasal cavity.” Ex. 1001, 1:33–42. The '675 patent states that

[a] further aspect of the invention is to provide methods and apparatus for treating or inhibiting a variety of diseases or syndromes that have an underlying neurogenic component, by disrupting the neurogenic activities of the promoters or mediators of the disease or syndrome. Such disruption is facilitated by delivering an active fragment of botulinum toxin, such as the light chain portion of botulinum toxin serotype A (BoNT-LC/A), in the presence of an electric field or ultrasonic energy applied to permeabilize the wall of targeted cell under conditions which induce reversible poration of the cellular membrane and efficient passage of the BoNT-LC fragment to the cell cytosol, its catalytic environment.

## Id. at 4:59–5:4.

The '675 patent describes delivering neurotoxins and active fragments thereof “to a patient’s nasal membrane while applying energy to target cells within the membrane under conditions which cause a reversible (or in some instances non-reversible) poration of the cell membranes to enhance delivery of the toxin into the cells.” Ex. 1001, 8:4–11. According to the '675 patent, the toxin may be introduced to “any portion of the nasal cavity, such as a single paranasal sinus or portion thereof, a main nasal passage, two or more paranasal sinuses,” “the entire nasal cavity of the patient,” or the “nasopharynx which is at the back of the nasal passage” and “comprises a cluster of goblet cells which are responsible for mucus secretion and which are susceptible to the disabling mechanism of the botulinum toxin and other neurotoxins.” Id. at 8:11–18. The '675 patent reports that targeting the nasopharynx allows “a high percentage of mucus-secreting epithelial or goblet cells [to] be treated with a device which is relatively small and which may carry a relatively low infusion volume and require less energy.” Id. at 24:2–6.

## Further, the '675 patent describes:

The energy is preferably selectively applied to a targeted region containing a variety of cell types, including goblet cells, epithelial cells, ciliated and non-ciliated columnar cells, basal cells, and less or no energy applied to untargeted regions. It will be appreciated that the energy may be applied to regions of the nasal membrane which are the same or different from the regions to which the toxin has been introduced. By controlling the delivery area of both the toxin delivery and the energy delivery, the methods and apparatus of the present invention can more specifically target the epithelial or goblet and other recipient cells of interest while minimizing the amount of toxin which enters non-targeted cells. That is, only those cells in the nasal membrane which are exposed to both the toxin and the applied energy will preferentially be permeabilized or porated to receive the toxin within the cytoplasm of the cell.

Id. at 8:22–37; see also id. at 9:58–61 (“When the energy applicator is incorporated with or within the catheter, the delivery pattern will usually be at least partially overlapping with the toxin delivery pattern of the catheter.”). According to the patent, in the case where the regions of applied energy and toxin dispersion only partially overlap, “the delivery of the toxin will be enabled or enhanced principally within the regions of overlap”). Id. at 10:1–3.

The '675 patent describes systems which comprise "catheter[s] or other structure[s] adapted to introduce the toxin or toxin fragment to a region adjacent the target cells," "an energy applicator for applying energy to the target cells under condition[s] which cause poration of the cell membranes to enhance delivery of the toxins and/or their active fragments," and "a source of the toxin or active fragments suitable for introduction from the catheter or other delivery structure." Ex. 1001, 5:11–19. According to the '675 patent, "[t]he energy applicator will typically be adapted to selectively apply energy to target cells within the region where the toxin is to be introduced, e.g., by focusing energy distribution to the particular target cells or regions which are rich with the target cells," but "may be adapted to apply energy non-selectively within the target region where both target cells and other cell

types may receive the energy.” Id. at 5:20–27. The energy applicator may apply electric energy (such as radiofrequency energy, direct current, alternating current, or combinations thereof), ultrasonic energy (such as high acoustic pressure ultrasound or low acoustic pressure ultrasound), X-ray beam energy, microwave energy, or “any other energy type which can achieve a reversible poration of the target cell walls.” Id. at 5:35–53.

## III. ANALYSIS

## A. Legal Standards

“In an [inter partes review], the petitioner has the burden from the onset to show with particularity why the patent it challenges is unpatentable.” Harmonic Inc. v. Avid Tech., Inc., 815 F.3d 1356, 1363 (Fed. Cir. 2016) (citing 35 U.S.C. § 312(a)(3)).

A claim is unpatentable under 35 U.S.C. § 103 if the differences between the claimed invention and the prior art are such that the claimed invention as a whole would have been obvious at the time the invention was made to a person having ordinary skill in the relevant art. KSR Int'l Co. v. Teleflex Inc., 550 U.S. 398, 406 (2007). The question of obviousness is resolved on the basis of underlying factual determinations including: (1) the scope and content of the prior art; (2) any differences between the claimed subject matter and the prior art; (3) the level of ordinary skill in the art; and (4) secondary considerations (i.e., objective indicia) of nonobviousness, when presented. $^{2}$ Graham v. John Deere Co., 383 U.S. 1, 17–18 (1966). “An obviousness determination [also] requires finding both that a skilled artisan would have been motivated to combine the teachings of the prior art references to achieve the claimed invention, and that the skilled artisan would have had a reasonable expectation of success in doing so.” CRFD Rsch., Inc. v. Matal, 876 F.3d 1330, 1340 (Fed. Cir. 2017) (internal quotation marks and citation omitted).

## B. Level of Ordinary Skill in the Art

In determining the level of ordinary skill in the art, various factors may be considered, including the “type of problems encountered in the art; prior art solutions to those problems; rapidity with which innovations are made; sophistication of the technology; and educational level of active workers in the field.” In re GPAC, Inc., 57 F.3d 1573, 1579 (Fed. Cir. 1995). The prior art itself can also reflect the appropriate level of ordinary skill in the art. Okajima v. Bourdeau, 261 F.3d 1350, 1355 (Fed. Cir. 2001).

Petitioner proposes that the person of ordinary skill in the art (“POSA”) at all relevant times (between the years 2005 through 2019) would have had:

(1) a Doctor of Medicine (M.D.) or at least a bachelor of science degree in engineering or a related discipline and (2) at least three years of experience in the clinical use, design, development, and/or testing of medical devices, including medical devices that deliver energy to tissue. Less work experience may be compensated by a higher level of education, and vice versa.

Pet. 17 (citing Ex. 1002 ¶¶ 26–30).

Patent Owner disagrees with Petitioner's definition of the POSA's qualifications. PO Resp. 18–20. Patent Owner contends that “Petitioners’ definition allows for a person with no knowledge of nasal anatomy to opine on issues in this proceeding.” Id. at 18. Patent Owner contends that a POSA “must have knowledge of the nasopharyngeal passage given the scope of the claims.” Id. at 18–19. Patent Owner further contends that Petitioner’s definition is “improper” because, under that definition, the POSA could be an M.D. with clinical experience using relevant medical devices, but no specific “medical device design or development experience.” Id. at 19 (citing Ex. 2003 ¶ 37).

Patent Owner alternatively proposes that a POSA should be defined as a person having:

(1) a Doctor of Medicine (M.D.) or at least a Bachelor of Science degree in engineering or a related discipline and

(2) at least three years of experience in the design and development of nasal medical devices, including nasal medical devices that deliver energy to tissue.

Id. at 19 (citing Ex. 2003 ¶ 38) (emphasis omitted).

Finally, Patent Owner contends that the testimony of Petitioner's expert should be given “little weight” because Dr. Bu-Saba does not have device design/development experience. PO Resp. 19 n.4 (citing Ex. 2002, 43:20–45:11).

In response, Petitioner contends that Dr. Bu-Saba qualifies as a POSA and that his testimony should be credited on the issues in dispute in this case. Pet. Reply 27–29.

Petitioner argues, for example, that Dr. Bu-Saba is a clinician, nasal cavity/nasopharynx anatomy expert, and that his “testimony is supported by his extensive clinical expertise.” Pet. Reply 27 (citing Ex. 1089 (Levine Tr.), 170:21–171:2 (recognizing Dr. Bu-Saba’s experience with RF ablation procedures and conceding Dr. Bu-Saba is an expert on the anatomy of the nasal cavity and nasopharynx)). Petitioner also argues that Patent Owner’s definition is flawed insofar as it limits the POSA to one having design or development experience, which Petitioner contends is not required. Id. at 28–29. In support, Petitioner argues, inter alia, that: 1) the challenged claims relate to delivering RF energy with generic (not specialized) components (id. at 28 (citing Ex. 1001, 27:43–28:47)); 2) “[c]linicians used RF devices safely and effectively in the nose, including using ‘factory settings’” (id. (citing Ex. 1001, 16:42–49, 18:5–10, 18:24–36)); 3) clinicians understood and carried out safe and effective RF device operation through, e.g., clinical use and testing (id. at 29 (citing Ex. 1089, 62:7–63:11 (Dr. Levine admitting safe and effective operation is learned through clinical use and instructions)); and 4) clinicians need not have developed a device to “see another place it could be used” (id. (quoting Ex. 1089, 124:19–125:13)).

To further support its contentions, Petitioner points out, inter alia, that: 1) “[t]he ’675 patent discusses ‘delivering neurotoxins’ in ‘any . . . target region,’” not just the nose, and “identifies no purportedly challenging nasal anatomy” (Pet. Reply 30 (citing, e.g., Ex. 1001, 2:23–67, 5:3–17, 4:59–63, 8:10–34, claim 1)); 2) “[p]rior-art problems and solutions were addressed with ‘factory setting’ generic RF devices” (e.g., Ex. 1006, 876–77), and “devices for multiple locations” (e.g., Ex. 1005 ¶ 43 (ear, nose, throat); Ex. 1009, 1:15-23) (Pet. Reply 32); 3) “[t]he named inventors are engineers, and [Patent Owner] offers no evidence either had prior nasal experience” to support its position (id. at 31); and 4) “Dr. Levine confirmed biomedical engineers ‘take in information from clinicians’ to create devices” (id. (citing Ex. 1089, 202:6–203:2 (Dr. Levine agreeing that “it’s the job of a biomedical engineer, working on a medical device, to take in information from clinicians to create better tools.”))).

In its Sur-Reply, Patent Owner reiterates its disagreement with Petitioner's POSA definition, arguing that definition would allow “an engineer without any RF or nose experience to assess and apply the prior art.” PO Sur-Reply 25.

Based on the record before us, including the types of problems and solutions described in the '675 patent and the cited prior art, we find a POSA would have had (1) a Doctor of Medicine (M.D.) or at least a bachelor of

science degree in biomedical engineering or a related discipline, (2) at least three years of experience in the clinical use, design, development, and/or testing of medical devices that deliver energy to tissue, and 3) knowledge and experience in treating nasal conditions, or access to a person with that knowledge and experience. Less work experience may be compensated by a higher level of education, and vice versa.

In reaching our finding, we note that, although we agree with Petitioner that the '675 patent discloses delivering neurotoxins to any target, and not just the nose, the claims of the '675 patent are directed to methods of treating a nasal condition by delivering energy to the nasal cavity. Pet. Reply 30. Thus, to fill a gap between Petitioner's POSA definition and the scope of the challenged claims, we add to Petitioner's definition the requirement for knowledge and experience in treating nasal conditions, or access to a person with that knowledge and experience. We additionally add that a POSA's educational background may include a bachelor of science degree in biomedical engineering or related discipline to reflect the nature of the field of invention. Nonetheless, we also note that our analysis and conclusions herein would not change even under Petitioner's proposed skill level for a POSA.

We do not agree with Patent Owner that prior experience designing or developing nasal medical devices is required for a POSA. PO Resp. 19. A definition inclusive of the clinical use, design, development, and/or testing of nasal medical devices that deliver energy to tissue is consistent with the skill level reflected in the disclosures of the '675 patent and prior art. Consequently, we disagree with Patent Owner that Dr. Bu-Saba fails to qualify as a POSA because he lacks experience specific to designing medical devices. PO Resp. 19 n.4. Accordingly, we do not agree with Patent Owner that we should discount the testimony of Dr. Bu-Saba and we give Dr. Bu-Saba's testimony appropriate weight in reaching our conclusions here.

## C. Claim Construction

In inter partes review, we construe claims using the same claim construction standard used to construe claims in a civil action before the courts under 35 U.S.C. § 282(b), including construing claims' language in accordance with its ordinary and customary meaning as understood by the POSA, in view of the patent's specification and considering the patent's prosecution history. 37 C.F.R. § 42.100(b). We need only construe terms that are in controversy and only to the extent needed to resolve the dispute. Realtime Data, LLC v. Iancu, 912 F.3d 1368, 1375 (Fed. Cir. 2019); U.S. Surgical Corp. v. Ethicon, Inc., 103 F.3d 1554, 1568 (Fed. Cir. 1997) (holding claim construction is for “resolution of disputed meanings”).

Petitioner contends that, “[f]or purposes of this IPR, the Board need not construe any terms.” Pet. 18. Patent Owner responds that “the Board need not construe any terms to determine Petitioners have failed to carry their burden in this proceeding.” PO Resp. 20.

On the record before us, we conclude that the claims need not be expressly construed to resolve the dispute before us. Insofar as the parties dispute the mapping of the prior art to dependent claim 22, we address that dispute below including, to the extent necessary, any implicit disagreement about the subject matter encompassed by claim 22.

## 1. Makower (Ex. 1005)

Makower is a U.S. patent application that published October 27, 2005. Ex. 1005, code (43).

Makower is titled “Devices, Systems and Methods for Diagnosing and Treating Sinusitus [sic] and Other Disorders of the Ears, Nose and/or Throat.” Id. at code (54). Makower “relates generally to medical devices and methods and more particularly to minimally invasive, catheter based devices, systems and methods for treating sinusitis and other ear, nose & throat disorders.” Id. ¶ 1. Makower states that “[t]he term ‘sinusitis’ refers generally to any inflammation or infection of the paranasal sinuses.” Id. ¶ 8.

Makower explains that “[t]he sinuses consist of a series of cavities connected by passageways, ultimately opening into the nasal cavity.” Ex. 1005 ¶ 16. The “passageways and cavities are formed by bone, but covered in mucosa.” Id. Makower reports that “[i]f the mucosa of one of these passageways becomes inflamed for any reason, the cavities which drain through that passageway can become blocked” such that “the primary target for sinus therapy is the passageway, with the primary goal to regain drainage.” Id. According to Makower, “focus on the passageway and the use of flexible instrumentation suggests an entirely new approach to sinus intervention: one utilizing flexible catheters and guidance tools, with passageway and cavity modifying devices capable of being delivered with minimal damage to the surrounding tissues.” Id.

One such intervention is surgery to reduce the size of the inferior turbinates and the associated mucosa or soft tissue. Makower explains that

[s]urgy to reduce the size of the inferior turbinates can be accomplished with endoscopic visualization (with magnification where desired) and is typically performed with the patient under general anesthesia. An incision is typically made in the mucosa that lines the turbinate to expose the underlying bone. Some quantity of the underlying bone may then be removed. If selective removal of some of the mucosa or soft tissue is also desired, such soft tissue can be debulked or removed through by traditional surgical cutting or by the use of other tissue ablation or debulking apparatus such as microdebriders or lasers. Less frequently, chronically enlarged inferior turbinates have been treated by cryotherapy. It is typically desirable to remove only as much tissue as necessary to restore normal breathing and drainage, as removal of too much tissue from the turbinates can impair the ability of the turbinates to perform their physiological functions of warming and humidifying inspired air and conserving warmth and moisture from expired air.

## Ex. 1005 ¶ 30.

Another example of an intervention disclosed in Makower is laser or radiofrequency turbinate reduction. According to Makower, “[i]n cases where it is not necessary to revise the bone that underlies the turbinate, the surgeon may elect to perform a laser or radiofrequency procedure designed to create a coagulative lesion in (or on) the turbinate, which in turn causes the soft tissue of the turbinate to shrink.” Id. ¶ 39. Makower discloses that “[o]ne example of a radio frequency procedure that may be used to shrink enlarged inferior turbinates is radiofrequency volumetric tissue reduction (RFVTR) using the Somnoplasty® system (Somnus Medical Technologies, Sunnyvale, Calif.)” which “includes a radio frequency generator attached to a probe.” Id. ¶ 40. Makower explains that

[t]he probe is inserted through the mucosa into the underlying soft tissue of the turbinate, usually under direct visualization. Radiofrequency energy is then delivered to heat the submucosal tissue around the probe, thereby creating a submucosal coagulative lesion while allowing the mucosa to remain intact. As the coagulative lesion heals, the submucosal tissue shrinks thereby reducing the overall size of the turbinate.

## 2. Fang (Ex. 1006)

Fang is an article titled “Nasal endoscopy combined with multiple radiofrequency for perennial allergic rhinitis.” Ex. 1006, 876. $^{3}$ Fang discusses the results of a study “[t]o evaluate the therapeutic effects of nasal endoscopic surgery combined with multiple radiofrequency in the treatment of perennial allergic rhinitis.” Id. According to Fang, “[s]ixty-three patients with perennial allergic rhinitis whose symptoms failed to be controlled by medication received nasal endoscopic surgery for submucous resection of the nasal septum combined with radiofrequency ablation of the bilateral sphenopalatine foramen, anterior ethmoidal nerve, and inferior nasal concha.” Id. “[A]nother 20 patients with the same disease who underwent nasal septum surgery alone were selected as the control group.” Id. After the surgeries, “efficacy was evaluated according to the evaluation criteria for allergic rhinitis” (id.), and “[a]ll the patients were followed up for 2 years, and no antihistamines or corticosteroids were used for treatment during the observation period” (id. at 877).

## Fang reports that

[a]t 1 month after operation, the rate of excellent recovery was 92.1% (58/63), the efficacy rate was 7.9% (5/63), and the total efficacy rate was 100%. At 1 year after operation, the rate of excellent recovery was 88.9% (56/63), the efficacy rate was 7.9% (5/63), the rate of no efficacy was 3.2% (2/63), and the total efficacy rate was 96.8% (61/63). At 2 years after operation, the rate of excellent recovery was 82.6% (52/63), the efficacy rate was 12.7% (8/63), the rate of no efficacy was 4.8% (3/63), and the total efficacy rate was 95.2% (60/63). (1) Alleged Lack of Reasonable Expectation of Success in Treating Nasal Tissue with the Makower Device

Patent Owner's argument that Makower is “directed to” the use of flexible catheters and not “RF energy devices” is legally and factually flawed. First, the law does not require “conclusive proof” of efficacy to demonstrate obviousness. Acorda Therapeutics, Inc. v. Roxane Labs., Inc., 903 F.3d 1310, 1333 (Fed. Cir. 2018) (“This court has long rejected a requirement of conclusive proof of efficacy for obviousness”) (internal quotation marks and alteration in original); but see Ex. 2003 ¶ 71 (Dr. Levine opining a POSA “would need to see conclusive proof that RF energy alone was responsible for the improvement in perennial allergic rhinitis”) (emphasis added). That is especially so where the claims here, insofar as they recite efficacy at all, only do so at the most generic level as we discussed above. See, e.g., Ex. 1098 ¶ 111 (testifying the claims require no particular degree or measure of efficacy).

Second, we agree with Petitioner that Fang teaches RF ablation of the parasympathetic nerves in the nasal cavity/nasopharynx improved rhinitis outcomes. Pet. Reply 18. Indeed, Fang reported statistically significant improvements between the control group (no ablation) and the study group (with ablation). Ex. 1006, 876 (disclosing that “[c]ompared with patients in the control group . . ., the total efficacy rate [among the experimental group] . . . was significantly improved, with statistical significance.”); see also id. (concluding nasal endoscope “with radiofrequency ablation may achieve good therapeutic effects on perennial allergic rhinitis”). Even considering that some patients may have received additional surgical interventions for additional conditions (e.g., nasal polyps), we are unpersuaded that a skilled artisan would have dismissed Fang’s favorable results. Ex. 1098 ¶¶ 104–

106, 116–120 (testifying, inter alia, it is common for clinicians to treat multiple pathologies simultaneously and that a POSA would have considered (and not discounted) Fang's attribution of positive rhinitis treatment results to the RF ablation technique Fang applied).

Third, although Patent Owner criticizes parts of Fang's experimental method and the reference to diagnostic/efficacy criteria in a document outside Fang's four-corners, Patent Owner "does not dispute Fang was right about" the benefit of RF ablation in treating rhinitis. Pet. Reply 18–19. We find that a POSA would, on balance, have known or at least reasonably expected that Fang was correct—as "Fang's findings are consistent with multiple undisputed prior-art references." Id.; Ex. 1002 ¶¶ 46 (discussing background references), 190 (discussing Chen); Pet. 14–15 (asserting "[a] distinct but well-known technique was to use ablation devices to deliver energy to destroy cells that cause rhinitis symptoms" (citing Fang, Chen, and Arora (Ex. 1054, 80)), 79, 83 (discussing Chen in the context of motivation and reasonable expectation of success). Chen, for instance, discloses that "radiofrequency thermocoagulation of the sphenopalatine nerve" reduced the symptoms of rhinitis, consistent with Fang's results. Ex. 1044, 239–240 (describing the technique as "highly effective, easy to perform . . . [and] a good way to treat PAR [(perennial allergic rhinitis)]"); see also Ex. 1054, 80 ("[c]ryodestruction of the nasal branches of the Vidian nerve . . . in cases of vasomotor rhinitis"); Ex. 1045, 341–342 ("[RF] treatment . . . can cause varying degrees of damage to the deep ethmoidal nerve . . . thus achieving the goal of treating allergic rhinitis"); Ex. 1098 ¶ 98 (testifying it was already known "that ablating parasympathetic nerves, even with various forms of ablation such as [RF] ablation or cryotherapy, treats rhinitis" (citing references)). Considered in the context of Chen and other background art

cited by Petitioner, which Patent Owner does not address in its papers, the criticisms about lack of detail in Fang fall flat. Tr. 51:20–53:8. $^{17}$

Lastly, Patent Owner's contention that Fang has been cited only once in the technical literature is incorrect and unpersuasive. In response to Patent Owner's argument, Petitioner provides evidence indicating that Fang has been cited at least seven times, including in the Hytönen reference that Patent Owner alluded to, but did not submit. Pet. Reply 19–20 (citing Ex. 1103). Hytönen cites positively to Fang's results and urges that clinical trials on nasal RF ablation treatment should proceed. Ex. 1112, 1260–1261, 1264 (noting the statistically significant “SS” results reported in Fang

(among other studies) and recommending initiation of double-blind, placebo-controlled randomized trials).

In sum, Patent Owner's critiques of Fang would not have discouraged the skilled artisan from considering its teachings, nor do they detract from the reasons given by Petitioner for combining Makower and Fang.

(b) Whether Makower is “Directed to” Flexible Catheters

Patent Owner argues Makower is “directed to flexible catheters” and that Petitioner provides “no reason” why a POSA would have looked to Makower for energy delivery devices. PO Resp. 48–53 (citing, e.g., Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 36–37, 40–42, 132–143; Ex. 2003 ¶¶ 77–85).

We addressed substantially the same argument above, and explained why it was unpersuasive. See supra Section III.F.1.b & b.1. Makower is undisputedly analogous prior art, and we presume that a POSA is aware of all such relevant prior art. In re Rouffet, 149 F.3d 1350, 1357 (Fed. Cir. 1998) (explaining that the law “presumes that all prior art references in the field of the invention are available to this hypothetical skilled artisan”); In re Lemelson, 397 F.2d at 1009 (holding all relevant disclosure must be considered and a reference’s teachings are not limited to the “problem” solved by the reference). Makower is not limited to “flexible catheters” and, indeed, it describes and claims working devices—such devices include an RF-ablation catheter device designed for insertion into the nasal cavity such as described and shown in Makower’s Figure 5H. See, e.g., Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 160–162, 164, 187, claim 1, Fig. 5H. Makower also teaches flexible catheters can be used with or incorporated into such working devices to facilitate safe and accurate placement at the appropriate region for which treatment is desired. See, e.g., id. ¶¶ 161, 187.

We determine the evidence, including the teachings of the references themselves, favors Petitioner's argument that a POSA would have been motivated to use Makower's RF ablation device to target the regions of the nasal cavity identified by Fang and, thus, treat rhinitis. ## (c) Alleged Hindsight and Lack of Reasons to Combine the Art

Patent Owner contends Petitioner's reasons for combining Makower and Fang are tainted with hindsight. PO Resp. 53–63. Patent Owner invokes a variety of contentions that allegedly reveal this flaw in Petitioner's challenge. Patent Owner contends a skilled artisan would not have looked to Makower's device for damaging nerves because Makower discloses that its device shrinks tissues as a means of reducing obstructions in the nasopharyngeal passage. Id. at 54 (citing Ex. 1005 ¶ 92; Ex. 2003 ¶ 45–49). Patent Owner argues Makower and Fang have different objectives; Makower is allegedly focused on flexible devices as a way to avoid bloody or painful treatments that historically arose from use of rigid devices, and Fang's methods included “destructive surgical methods,” including septum surgery. Id. at 55–56 (citing Ex. 1006, 877; Ex. 2003 ¶¶ 89–98). Patent Owner

contends the electrode arrangement of Makower's device could cause undesired ablation to tissues beyond those targeted for treatment. $Id.$ at 56–58 (asserting Makower's Fig. 5H shows a device positioned to cauterize tissue on both sides of a passageway and, if such a device with oppositely disposed struts were placed against the lateral nasal wall (i.e., tissues targeted by Fang), the septum opposite the lateral wall might be cauterized too). And, Patent Owner contends, if anything, a skilled artisan would select other devices of Makower—like the Figure 5A device, which includes only a single side opening that could be positioned against adjacent tissues—rather than the two-sided treatment device shown in Figure 5H, which comes with alleged risks of ablating and potentially perforating the septum. PO Resp. 59–60 (citing Ex. 1005 ¶ 166, Fig. 5A). $^{18}$

Patent Owner's contentions are unavailing. We find that a skilled artisan would have considered Makower for nasal disorder treatments. See, e.g., Ex. 1005 ¶ 1 (describing field of invention as related to catheter based devices and methods for treating sinusitis and other nasal disorders). Insofar as Makower's Figure 5H device can be used for tissue shrinking, it shrinks tissues through the application of ablation energy. Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 92, 164, 187; Ex. 1098 ¶ 132 (testifying that tissue-shrinking electrodes include multiple bipolar electrodes delivering ablation energy to tissues). Moreover, treatments aimed at reducing obstructions and treatments aimed at reducing mucus secretions are not incompatible or mutually exclusive. Ex. 1098 ¶¶ 133–134 (testifying blockages and mucus production are both symptoms of rhinitis, and treatments also involved reduction of nasal blockages); Ex. 1107 (Levine article), 453 (describing rhinitis treatment using laser coagulation to reduce turbinate and decrease mucus-secreting tissues).

We do not agree that Makower and Fang have diametrically opposed objectives, as argued by Patent Owner. PO Resp. 55–56. Both references teach or suggest treating nasal conditions by application of RF-ablation energy. Ex. 1006, 876–877; Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 1, 43, 132, 164, 187. Makower does not teach that cutting or similar surgical interventions are inconsistent or inappropriate with its devices and methods. See Ex. 1005 ¶ 43 (disclosing that devices of the invention may be used for “remodeling,” “cutting,” and “debulking” nasal tissues “such as nasal polyps” and other “enlarged” or “abnormal” tissues). Drs. Levine and Bu-Saba agree that “it’s not unusual to combine several procedures in practice” when treating nasal conditions. Ex. 1089, 89:12–91:8 (testifying, e.g., that a clinician may correct a deviated septum during the same procedure in which a turbinate is reduced in size); Ex. 1098 ¶¶ 136–138 (testifying “combining multiple procedures was (and remains) common”). And nothing in challenged claim 1 forecloses surgical or other therapeutic interventions above and beyond RF ablation.

We also disagree with Patent Owner's contention that a POSA would not have used Makower's Figure 5H device based on concerns about ablation of undesired tissues. As Petitioner argues, a POSA would simply adjust the device's positioning or its RF parameters, such as “by selectively activating electrodes contacting target nasal-wall tissue.” Pet. Reply 22–23 (citing Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 43, 161, 164, 187); Ex. 1098 ¶¶ 139–143; KSR, 550 U.S. at 421 (explaining that a skilled artisan is “not an automaton”). That a

POSA might have favored and, therefore, selected a different device described by Makower (i.e., the embodiment of Figure 5A) for the surgical procedures in question is not decisive. A petitioner need not show a motivation to pursue only the best or most obvious solution. Intel Corp. v. Qualcomm Inc., 21 F.4th 784, 800 (Fed. Cir. 2021).

For the reasons above, we find that the preponderance of the evidence supports Petitioner's contention that a POSA would have combined Makower and Fang in the manner proposed.

## c) Conclusion on Claim 1

Upon considering the full trial record, we find by a preponderance of the evidence that the combination of Makower and Fang teaches or suggests the method of claim 1, and that the skilled artisan would have had reason to combine the references as proposed by Petitioner and done so with a reasonable expectation of success. We, therefore, conclude that claim 1 would have been obvious.

## 2. Claim 17

Similar to claim 1, independent claim 17 is directed to “a method of treating a nasal condition of a patient” in three steps: 1) “positioning a distal portion of a treatment device proximate to target tissue in a nasopharyngeal passageway of a patient;” 2) “administering therapy to the target tissue with the treatment device” to “alter” the target tissue “such that congestion in the nasopharyngeal passageway is reduced;” and 3) “administering therapy” comprising “delivering energy to the target tissue.” Ex. 1001, 2822–31. Claim 17 differs from claim 1 primarily in the result achieved by treatment. Claim 1 requires “administering therapy to the target tissue with the treatment device so as to disrupt neural activity such that mucus secretion is reduced in the nasopharyngeal passage,” while claim 17 requires “administering therapy to the target tissue with the treatment device to alter nasopharyngeal cells such that congestion in the nasopharyngeal passage is reduced.”

There is no dispute between the parties, however, specific to claim 17. Petitioner's challenge of claim 17 relies heavily on its analysis of claim 1, and Patent Owner does not argue the patentability of claim 17 separately. See Pet. 82–83; see also PO Resp. 32–45. Thus, for the same reasons discussed above with regard to claim 1, we find by a preponderance of the evidence that the combination of Makower and Fang teaches or suggests the method of claim 17, and that the skilled artisan would have had reason to combine the references as proposed by Petitioner and done so with a reasonable expectation of success. We, therefore, conclude that claim 17 would have been obvious.

## e) Analysis

We find that Makower discloses electrosurgical devices for the nasal cavity or nasopharynx that use multiple bipolar electrodes that cauterize or heat adjacent tissue such that the function of a nasopharyngeal passage of the patient is altered. Ex. 1005 ¶¶ 43, 160–162, 164, 187, Fig. 5H; Pet. 28; see supra Section III.F.1.b.1. (discussing the operability of Makower's device). We further agree with Petitioner that, in order to treat “tissue adjacent” to Makower’s electrodes, energy flows from the electrodes into tissue that is lateral relative to the distal portion of the device, as required by claim 22. Here, we credit the testimony of Dr. Bu-Saba that “a person of ordinary skill in the art would have understood that current traveling between Makower’s bipolar electrodes would create electrical fields that concentrate current lateral relative to the axis of the distal portion of the RF ablation probe and into the targeted tissue.” Ex. 1002 ¶ 206.

Patent Owner's contentions to the contrary are unavailing. We considered Patent Owner's arguments that Makower's electrodes are configured to deliver energy uniformly as compared to laterally. PO Resp. 65 (citing Ex. 2003 ¶ 109; Ex. 2005 ¶¶ 74–75). However, we are persuaded by Petitioner's arguments and the testimony of Dr. Bu-Saba that Makower's device directs energy laterally into the adjacent tissue that is in contact with the electrodes and would “not travel through surrounding air.” Ex. 1098 ¶ 152; see also id. (“As a matter of operation, electrodes of the device taught by Makower would contact target tissue and portions of the electrodes that are not contacting tissue would be insulated by air.”); id. (“a person of ordinary skill in the art would have understood that mucus or blood would not affect treatment or would be removed from the nasal cavity and nasopharynx prior to ablation”). Thus, even if the ring electrodes of Makower are capable of generally directing energy uniformly, in the context of Makower’s disclosed use, we find that “a POSITA would have expected ring electrodes in Makower [to] concentrate energy laterally because air would insulate any portion not contacting tissue.” Pet. Reply 24 (citing Ex. 1091, 218:9–11, 221:18–222:7; Ex.1001, 18:11–14; 23:65–24:3; Ex. 1098 ¶¶ 147–148, 151–152). $^{20}$

For the reasons above, we determine that the preponderance of the evidence favors Petitioner's challenge and that claim 22 would have been obvious over Makower and Fang.
